fuji, mon and tsuba

by yamaimojg

Hi all,
I'm looking for thoughts on the reading of the sukashi design on a Ko-Tosho tsuba with a wisteria motif. Please take a look at the photos on the group site at:

http://tinyurl.com/cfw7

Real Japanese wisteria grow with the flower clusters hanging downward... see photo of an actual wisteria currently blooming in my yard. Some fittings depict this plant in a naturalistic way as in the example of the Momoyama period Ko-Kinko o-kozuka. This motif has also been stylized in mon with double strands of flowers either hanging down or pointing upward (which as far as I know they don't do in nature). I have only run across single strand fuji mon with the flowers "hanging" up as illustrated in the mon photo.

Questions:
Does anyone have an example of a single strand wisteria mon with the flower cluster hanging down in the naturalistic way?

Does anyone know if there is some particular significance to the "upside down" versions of the mon?

Which orientation of the tsuba makes sense to you? Is it depicting the actual plant (katana orientation) or the single strand mon (which would require a tachi orientation)?

Any ideas appreciated,
Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] fuji, mon and tsuba

by soshuju

Jim-
It is a tachi tsuba! From your mon page you can see there are
left (turned, curled) and right (turned,curled) fuji. Remembering
this is stylized, you need not confine you point of view to "up and
down". Imagine that you are looking not from the side (up down
orientation) but from above. You can see the green leaves and the
flowers blown left or right by the wind. Or the same POV but your cut
flowers are now floating in a circular bowl...
To my eye the tachi orientation looks correct. Imagine this
on a sword, and how the human eye would "read" the design. The katana
orientation looks awkward and I think doesn't read right. I think it
more likely the greater portion of this design being on the ura side
of the tsuba is more natural. In the katana orientation the greater
portion of of the design is "outside" and under the sword, not an
especially auspicious arrangement. It is only my humble opinion but I
feel it is a tachi tsuba.
-t

by yamaimojg

Hi John,
Agreed on the hammer marks, etc. The owner originally saw this as a katana tsuba, based on a naturalistic reading of the wisteria. Bob Haynes convinced him that it was a tachi tsuba based on its similarity to the published single strand mon. I was initially convinced that it was a katana tsuba, also based on a naturalistic reading and my bias from my experience with that fuji kozuka. I thought that finding a matching mon would be easy given that there seems to be every variation of mon under the sun, but so far we haven't turned up an exact match. Although that it does or does not match a published mon doesn't prove the intention of the maker or wearer. Could it be a tachi guard... I think yes. Is it probably a tachi guard... maybe.

Tom's idea of the "inverted" motif being viewed from above or below rather than up or down is very interesting. The idea that a design being "under the sword" is inauspicious is something I hadn't encountered before. That would still be true for katana orientation even with a front-back flip. Maybe Tom can say some more about that.

Some other thoughts... it doesn't look like it could have been mounted with o-seppa. Of course without having a surviving tachi koshirae mounted with a Ko-Tosho tsuba, we don't know if they were mounted with o-seppa or not. It has no hitsuana, but there are intact period katana koshirae with no kozuka or kogai. The owner thinks that the date is mid Muromachi, which is possible given the diameter, thickness and appearance of the plate. The design strikes me as very Momoyama, although it's possible that someone important enough to be wearing a tachi might have ordered this kind of elaborate guard much earlier.

I've been looking at the dang thing so long I'm not sure which orientation looks better... I'm currently leaning toward tachi, but "flipped" katana is tough to rule out.

Spreading the confusion...
Jim
