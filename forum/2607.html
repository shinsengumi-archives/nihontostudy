Some more observations after comparing
http://www.jpsword.com/files/kn/nobuiye.html to
http://www.nihonto.com/11.28.04.html some more.

1. The nakago and hitsu ana look 'too regular and wrongly shaped' on the
Kuwabara tsuba.
2. The mei seems to be placed a bit too low in relation to the nakago ana
and the characters are slightly too far apart(?)
3. Specifically the second character looks like it is completely out of
place, much too low, nearly next to the bottom of the nakago ana. On the
genuine examples the second character is much higher up on all of them.

Does anyone have any more comments? I think this makes quite an interesting
exercise for the newbies like myself. (at least it is for me :-)

Re: [nihontostudy] Gimei Nobuiye?

by matthewhoyle2000

Last comment/observation: I get the distinct impression that all the hitsu
ana on the genuine Nobuiye examples were added later.

The ones on the Kuwabara look like they were made at the same time when the
piece was.

Correct me here if I am wrong, but weren't hitsu ana not very popular when
Nobuiye was active? Ergo they were 'hardly' put in and when they were they
were quite different in shape from the ones in the Edo period. More
elongated and oval, in the Edo period they are more "standardized" and all
look a bit the same.

A forger in the 19th cent like for example one of the Norisuke guys might
not have thought about this and put in modern looking hitsu ana out of
habit. The hitsu ana added to the genuine Nobuiye would probably have been
added after manufacture, but still much earlier than the 19th cent (maybe
beginning edo period) and would therefore look different.

Or am I being ridiculous here?

Matt

RE: [nihontostudy] Gimei Nobuiye?

by musashibobenkei

Matt and all,

The book on the page you refer to is a Kanie-Nobuie exhibition piece that
has a number of high quality loose plates like those shown.

The book Arnold referred to is Kyusaku Akiyama's "Nakamura Kakudaifu Nobuie
Tsuba shu [Collection of Rubbings of Nobuie's Sword Guards Belonging to
Kakudaifu Nakamura]" from 1926 and I think several reprints. Many of the
Nobuie guards we see, including Cary's that was written up in the English
edition Token Bijitsu, are in that book. All illustrations are rubbings that
Akiyama made, which are actually more useful for study than many of the
photographs we see. Many of the guards in the book were destroyed during the
war. While the current scholarship as to the generations and authenticity of
Nobuie signatures has changed since it's publication, it is still a very
useful and interesting book.

I have a copy for sale on my website, though it is a bit pricey considering
it is from Lundgren's library, and contains notes transcribed in 1964 by
Robert E. Haynes from the notes he made in 1961 while studying in Japan with
Dr. Torigoye.

Re: [nihontostudy] Gimei Nobuiye?

by yamaimojg

Matt,
I haven't seen the guard in person, but here's something I wrote in response to an off line question a couple of weeks ago.

Jim
There were a number of makers working
in the Edo period that signed Nobuie, often including their place of
residence. There are guards signed Kaga, Owari, Echizen, Akasaka, etc.
Nobuie. Sometimes the work style of a particular later guard with only a
two-character signature can be tied back to one of these. I don't think
that I have ever seen a formal kanteisho written attributing a
two-character-signed guard to a specific regional Edo period maker, though.
I have seen an NBTHK kanteisho written to "Nobuie (Edo period)" to show that
it is not a forgery, but is one of the later makers. I have seen another
written to "betsujin Nobuie," meaning a different person, i.e., not the
famous maker(s). I have also seen good quality, early Edo period tsuba
signed Nobuie fail shinsa as forgeries.

Most of those follow-on makers didn't try (or weren't able) to reproduce the flavor of the
iron and rim of the original guards, though. They use classic designs, but
the plate is more like typical early-mid Edo period work. It became the
fashion during the late Edo period to produce copies of the earlier style.
Some of these late works are signed by shinshinto sword smiths or Myochin
makers and can be quite nice. The two Norisuke were famous for faking
Nobuie, Yamakichibei, Yagyu, etc., guards. The way that the rim is worked
here seems like it's from that period and might even be their work. There
is a definite attempt to recreate the original style, but the result is forced and overdone.

Photos are no substitute for seeing a piece in
person. I can't see the signature very well in the photo, but the quality
of the metal and workmanship are harder to fake than a signature.
