By the way, Hatori Jiichiro published the uchigatana and koshigatana details from Ban Dainagon in his book "Tsuba no Bunkashi" in 1969 (page 26). That was ten years before Sasano did the same in "Tosogu no Kigen."
Jim

For those who haven't seen the book, the best detail online from the Ban Dainagon I've found so far is here:

http://www.kyohaku.go.jp/gakuso/gakuso02/gs2a28ie.htm

Look at the fellow in the upper left hand corner. He is carrying an uchigatana with a tsuba, however it's rather difficult to say whether it might be made of iron, yamagane or leather.

Gordon - do you have any sort of image of the nagamaki tsuba? Is it a plain iron plate without sukashi?

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Ban Dainagon stuff

by yamaimojg

Funny that you mentioned it, I was thinking about the same thing today. I first wondered if maybe a drop in the cost of iron production was responsible for the later proliferation of iron tsuba. But when you look at the list of individuals who gave their tachi to Oyamazumi, they could obviously afford whatever they wanted on their swords. These are rather undecorated mounts, so I'd guess that there was some perceived functional advantage rather than just a display of status and/or aesthetics at play.

I'd also guess that the nerikawa tsuba took more time and labor to make (curing, soaking, gluing, drying...fitting a fukurin...). Iron guards were probably quicker to make, but the cost of the material (including fuel) would be higher. It'd take some research into the economies of the time to figure out and answer.

I'd guess that kawa would also be expensive in the sense that it'd need to be replaced more frequently than iron. Getting a tsuba made of it wet would not be a good idea, for example. It may be that the durability of iron is what made it more attractive for the lower status mounts.

Speculation sure is fun...
Jim
