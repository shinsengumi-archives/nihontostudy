group was for discussing Kodogu.

collection?

by tsubahunter

Lucien,

The best Goto book out there is Goto Book from NBTHK By: Fukushi Sensei
Another good book is SWORD FITTINGS BY THE FAMILY OF GOTO 1440 - 1879 1994.
Sano Art Museum, Nezu Institute of Fine Arts, Tokugawa Art Museum.

I'm sure that there are other books on Goto fittings, but these two come to
mind first.

Re: [nihontostudy] goto kodogu study

by sworddad

Lucien -

I believe the NBTHK book by Fukushi Sensei is a very large volume that came out a year or so ago. If so--and I have a copy myself but have yet to really "get into it"--it is pricey and (I believe) out-of-print. The only book on Goto I had prior to this large work was the one titled: The 17 Generations of Goto (Japanese text). I have this in both hard cover and soft cover, I believe both being the same inside the covers. It seems very good but as always, so much is missed unless you can read Japanese.

Hopefully those who attended this year's San Francisco show saw the small but significant display of Goto fittings put on by Rick Mantigani and Dan Burns. Absolutely awesome kodogu! Unbelievable crisp and "minty" items of high, high quality makers! This display has been shown the last few years but I really wonder how many people stop to study the treasures displayed.

Some of these Goto fittings--and others--have been included as special inserts (high quality photos and printing) in the Northern California group's newsletter from time to time.

Ron
STL

Re: [nihontostudy] goto kodogu study

by sworddad

Curran -

Rick and Dan are both "as good as they come" and true "students" of Goto works and of the sword. Rick is the treasurer of the newly formed American branch of the NBTHK. Neither one participate on these discussion/chat groups. I have not checked the NCJSC's web site in awhile but it wouldn't surprise me to see some of these or some other equally fine kodogu pictured there.

Quality made photographs are great but to see this quality of work a few inches from one's eyes, in the "flesh," is something else! This brings to mind a really fine tsuba I once owned signed Natsuo. It was good and Bob Haynes agreed, but also added that it would never "pass" in Japan. A couple years later I was at the Boston MFA and for the first time I saw a "real" Natsuo work and immediately, I recognized that my wonderful tsuba simply did not at all have the quality that radiated from that "real" Natsuo. Same with mainline Goto works. That "quality" speaks for itself!

Ron
STL
