If the fuchi kashira really were Goto, they'd probably be worth more than the estimates, but lots of black and gold Goto-esque kodogu of many levels of quality were produced. Re: [nihontostudy] RE: Question....

by Barry Thomas

Kim,

I'm sure we're all alive and well on this list, but perhaps a "statistical
clump" means we are all busy at something else in this instant of time...

I remember well the pepper keeness of my beginning days (or should that be
"daze"!!!), but one thing I have learnt over time is that it is near-impossible
to assess blades from photos. My maxim is not to buy anything I cannot hold in
my hand and judge for myself. There are far too many subtleties to do otherwise
on my very limited budget. If I were a rich man it would be a slightly
different story, but that's the way of the world. Fittings are a little better
judged from GOOD photos, but from a description such as yours one can only
comment in the most general of terms in essay format, which in practice amount
to nothing that would really help you. The best way to learn is to buy and make
mistakes - you WILL make mistakes, I can guarantee that. So, yes, please give a
link to what you seek an opinion on.

Barry Thomas.

Re: [nihontostudy] RE: Question....

by Barry Thomas

Kim,
Hope you don't mind a tip or ten!! This advice is based on the assumption that
you are going to use a digital camera.
- Use the highest resolution your camera is capable of.
- Study the User Manual for a CLOSEUP option use if your camera has the
function.
- Get AS MUCH of the object you want in the image (LCDscreen or viewfinder)
before taking the pic. This gives you more pixels on what you want people to
see.
- Take as many pics as you can from different angles and distances. It is no
joy to get home and find all your images (or the otherwise best ones) are out of
focus or have reflections off a glass case (the camera always sees what the eye
does not!!).
- Find out how to increase the exposure of your camera if it has the function.
Take extra images at +0.5, +1.0 or even +1.5 if the lighting is not bright
enough.
- Unload and carefully view all images with an image view application. I use a
rather simple program called Microsoft PhotoEditor. It is basic but allows
viewing at up to 400x but I hardly use any more than 150x - if your image is
beginning to pixellate at 125x then it doesn't have enough resolution.
- PhotoEditor allows one to rotate and crop images for best presentation and
viewing so people can concentrate on what you want them to see.
- Preserve the original images by renaming any image you manipulate with a
meaningful name. Resave at the highest resolution possible - the jpeg save
option in PhotoEditor, for example, should be at 100% to preserve image quality.
- I'm a rank amatuer, but if you're a professional photographer I apologise for
the imposition...

Finally, don't post large file sizes to the list. Put them on a hosting site
somewhere.

Reply off-line if I can be of further assistance.

Best regards,

Barry Thomas.

by sworddad

Hi Mark -

>...maybe I should pick another hobbie! I laugh at it now but it didn't sit well...

Had to laugh at this comment because we have all experienced this thinking somewhere along the way. Actually, I doubt that many of us "picked" this hobby; rather, I think the hobby picked those of us having that "spark," that fascination (obsession?) with ancient things needing preserving.

>not having a "ton of money"...

Today, I sometimes wonder how the newer collectors bitten by this interest survive, seeing the cost of things today. Then I realize that 35 years ago when swords could be bought for $25, many of us struggled to purchase those "cheapies." Fresh out of the navy and with a new wife and baby, and making squat in salary, I had to peddle whatever could be found and then hoard those dollars for when a sword of interest was found. Fun times, indeed! So I suppose things never change.

>Whatever you spend on books, you will save when you come to buy your pieces.

Truer words where never written! My only regrets regarding books are the books I sold off, thinking I'd never really use them. Buy they better books as they come out because once they are out-of-print, the price exculates. Yes, the internet has a wealth of information on it but there is nothing like researching a sword of kodogu with a good library at your fingertips. You recommended "educate yourself" and that is the key to advancing in our area of study. I too was told to "study, study, study" when I met my first knowledgeable person familiar with Japanese swords (Dr. Thomas Hoopes) back around 1968.

Sorry...feeling "grandfatherly" today...just had to add my few cents worth to this discussion.

Ron
