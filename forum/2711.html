Roger,

Welcome! As far as pictures go, what do you have to work with in the
way of camera/lighting? there
are a number of techniques, not all of which are applicable to all
cameras... Also, what are
you trying to capture? The activities in the hada/ha, the overall shape
of the blade with the mune "lit up"
and the hamon line visible, closeups of the grain structure, pictures
with the nie/nioi "lit up", or...?

A great place to start is to read Darcy's write up on nihonto.ca - have
you read this?

Lately I've been experimenting with just using one big light and
blocking/reflecting light as needed - this seems
to work pretty well - I place the light at the end of the nakago shining
along the blade to light up stuff, and then I use
white cards to illuminate the back and kissaki - I also either use
filters or simply block the light shining on the nakago and
then use a white card to put some back (or a less dense nd filter
between the nakago and the light and no reflector card...).
here's a typical result:

http://home.teleport.com/~rkg/photos/kaneyoshi_korekane_tanto.jpg

here's a hada shot:
http://home.teleport.com/~rkg/photos/sukesada_boshi.jpg

and a nasty hamon shot:
http://home.teleport.com/~rkg/photos/ubu_nag_hada.jpg

here's one of my earlier attempts trying to use multiple lights
http://home.teleport.com/~rkg/photos/yasu_front.jpg

I don't seem to have any glowing hamon/activity shots on my computer at
the moment - sorry...


for getting pictures of hada/hamon structures, you can shoot straight on
(TURN OFF THE FLASH!!!) with a light to either side coming in
at a steep angle (or the beforementioned one side), and to get glowing
nie/nioi you really need to do the same thing you
do when looking at it (camera at an angle to the sword, light at an angle).

Also, don't be afraid to use a really high f/stop to get more depth of
field (using a wider lens gets you more DOF as
well, but that can cause other problems if you go too wide) - just put
the camera on a tripod (use the shutter lockup
if you have it) and go for it - maximum "sharpness" might be at f/8, but
usually you're beyond the resolution of the system
that cranking on over to f/16 or tighter will result in no visable
degradation...

The other thing to remember is that swords are effectively complex
mirrors - I tend to surround the sword with white/black
cards (sheets of foamboard) filling up the angles that would reflect
into the lens (I also have a big black sheet and a stand to
block stray reflections from ceilings, walls, etc., but that's another
story....).

A good dissertation on lighting stuff is in a book called "color, light,
and magic" (can't remember the author at the moment) .

Gotta run, Good Luck,

rkg
