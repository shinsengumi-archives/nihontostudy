Tsuba carving terminology

by sworddad

Hi all -

What is the Japanese name (term) for sunken relief carved design on tsuba? I'm talking about the design being made by carving the plate surface away. The tsuba I'm looking at has been posted under photos...

http://tinyurl.com/2jq42a

and shows a 24-petal kiku sukashi where the petals are rounded down below the surface of the rim and overall plate. (In this case, the tsuba is actually tapered, the flat edge being 1 mm less than the seppa-dai surface.) In the Boston book Ogawa called this sukidashi takabori in the Hayne/Harvie study book it seems to be called horiage (if I'm understanding the description correction) and also another term which I can't find right now.

Is there a common name for this type of sunken relief carving?

Ron

RE: [nihontostudy] Tsuba carving terminology

by robsongord

Hi Ron,



I think in the case of your two tsuba, niku-bori would be the correct term,
but, I could be wrong. Sukidashi-bori and sukidashi takabori are used when
there is carving that digs downward into the flat surface of the tsuba and
creates high relief carvings. Such carving typically does not include
sukashi work.



Gordon

Re: [nihontostudy] Tsuba carving terminology

by shirogitsune99

Hi Ron:

In the case of solid tsuba, the term would be
"Sukidashi-bori", but I don't think I've ever seen it
applied to this particular type of tsuba, although I
suppose if you were writing a description for sale you
could say that the blossoms are carved in
sukidashi-bori.
The tsuba itself is pretty clearly Bushu-Ito work,
despite the slender Akasaka like lines. Typically,
I've seen these pieces coming from the Tokugawa
domains, either Edo (the mimi-zogan is characteristic
of the Edo Bushu Ito school) or Hitachi. You've got a
slightly unusual design here because it looks like it
might have a bit of influence from the Higo Hayashi
school as well (I'm thinking of the shape and
positioning of the larger branches, but the carving is
definitely Bushu-Ito style as is the mimi-zogan. Not
really surprising to see these little cross-style
techniques creep in because of the Sankin-Kotai. It's
obviously a high-class piece and my guess is that it
was probably custom made for one particular sword and
did not go the typical route of a gazillion remounts
like the more typical trade-goods tsuba.

Cheers,

Phil

RE: [nihontostudy] Tsuba carving terminology

by robsongord

Hi,

The two terms are often used together particularly with deeply carved Mino pieces
where the top of the cravings are at the surface level, but a great deal of metal has
been removed using the sukidashi technique that results in takabori.

Gordon
