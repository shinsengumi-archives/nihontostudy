Re: [nihontostudy] mei help please

by yamaimojg

Rich,
It doesn't look like mitsu. The bottom part of the first character is the same as in the bottom part of the gyosho style writing of sada (Self/Hirose 384) and kore (S/H 782), but the upper part of those kanji in gyosho or sosho style are different. I can't find any kanji that fit reasonably with a gyosho writing of the whole with that lower part.

Looking through the gyosho/sosho section at the end of the Daijiten I find a very similar rendering of AI / chika. The kaisho style writing of AI is much more complex, but here are two "script" versions of that character in the Daijiten:

http://tinyurl.com/37z8y

I don't find any makers listed with the name Airi or Chikatoshi, or under any variations I could come up with, including other possible but less likely kanji. The tsuba is interesting only because the workmanship is very similar to the many unsigned shiiremono variations taken from Kaneie and Nara styles. The taganemei also look like the ones on those shiiremono. Unfortunately there isn't a place or group name inscribed here. Given the workmanship I wouldn't be surprised if the maker has not been recorded, but I hope that someone can do better than me with the reading and research on this one. It'd be good to pin these things down.

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] mei help please

by yamaimojg

I first thought mitsu also, but the loop in the first stroke doesn't make sense. When writing in gyosho style the strokes flow together and there can be some simplification, but you don't add in extra things. That loop stroke is usually used to combine a vertical and horizontal stroke.

The more "cursive" types of writing can look so stylized that it seems like anything goes, but they're still written based on the strokes and order of the basic "printing." Putting in an extra stroke changes the kanji.

Anyway,
Jim

by neobliviscar

Rich,

I posted both the Akasaka tsuba I have in my folder.
They are both papered (NBTHK & NTHK respectively) to
Akasaka.
Check there if this link does not work:

http://tinyurl.com/2mfvf

Compare it with yours. Similar central design element.
Along a timeline, I'd say yours definitely falls
chronologically between my 2 examples.

To be honest, I was a little surprised the Waterwheel
Tsuba papered to Akasaka. The photo's is odd/bad and
in hand the tsuba is more distinct, with nice iron
nodules. Iron is very refined, not needing to be so
heavily reworked as the 3rd gen? tsuba.

The 3rd gen? tsuba is very thick, and feels it was
hammered out from from a center mass. Heavy as a
brick.
From memory, dimensions are 68mm x 65mm x 6.25 mm.
Thins out about .75mm to 1mm to the mimi, with mimi
thickness greatest closer to design elements.

Curran
