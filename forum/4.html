Re: [nihontostudy] EXCELLENT!!! and startin' off with a question...

by yamaimojg

Richard,
The Nambokucho O-dachi koshirae that I have seen have moderate-sized tsuba.
I don't have measurements handy, but the ones I'm thinking of are nerikawa
tsuba that I would estimate to be in the 8 cm diameter range.
Proportionally, they look rather small on these very long koshirae.

This would seem to favor the idea that the tsuba functioned as a hand stop
or a hand guard (or probably both) and that there was no need to scale the
tsuba up beyond hand-sized.

On the other hand, some of the very large Ko-Tosho and Ko-Katchushi tsuba
are attributed to the Nambokucho jidai. This may or may not be correct. I
don't recall seeing a pre-Muromachi period koshirae mounted with one of
these.

The question of tsuba size changing with time and function is a complicated
one that I don't think I have a pat answer for. It's something I'd like to
continue to trade ideas on though.

Re: [nihontostudy] EXCELLENT!!! and startin' off with a question...

by yamaimojg

If anyone is interested, I can send you a copy of the Excel spreadsheet that
Richard mentioned. While hardly a scientific sample, what I did was to go
through the Tokyo National Museum Uchigatana Koshirae book and record the
tsuba diameter, saya length and tsuka length for each illustrated katana or
wakizashi koshirae. It's somewhat interesting to play with.

It turns out that correlation between saya length (a proxy for blade length)
and tsuba diameter is fairly weak. The correlation between tsuka length and
tsuba size is actually better. The correlation between saya length and
tsuka length is very good.

The average katana tsuba is 8.03 cm in diameter, but the range is 5.85-10.80
cm.
The average wakizashi tsuba is 6.59 cm in diameter, with a range of 4.90 to
8.60 cm.

So, making definite statements about katana-size or wakizashi-sized tsuba on
size alone is pretty difficult. There are no dates given for most of the
koshirae in the book, so that is not included in the spreadsheet. There's
more work to do here.

Jim
