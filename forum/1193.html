A little help please?

by crankybeech

Hello all.

I've been following the emails of this group for a while, and I must say
that I've truly enjoyed reading many of the messages that go out. The
collective knowledge in this group is amazing.

This leads me to my question. Well, first a little background. I'm
currently working on my MA in Art History at LSU. My thesis work is on
cross-cultural currents of martial arts in the global community. As a bit
of side research, I've been studying sword mounts, especially those of
Japanese origin. I'm aware that the steel sword is introduced to Japan
from China sometime pre-Nara. I'm also fairly up to speed on the
transformation of the sword from the Chinese design to one that is purely
Japanese.

Now, what I'm trying to determine is if there is any evidence of the
Japanese style mounts ever being made in China (or SE Asia, Phillipines,
etc) prior to the modern age. If any of you could point me in the right
direction, I'd be most grateful.

Thanks,

Buddy Ethridge

LSU Libraries

Buddy,
No one has picked up the loose end you dangled in front of us, perhaps for
very good reason. I will do so, but only very carefully. Before responding
to a part of the specific question you have put before the list, let me
make two general points.
First, I really like the idea of cross-cultural trends in martial arts - by
which I assume you mean the embellishment of weapons and associated
material objects as opposed to the behaviors of fighting. This strikes me
as terribly interesting. I have always wondered about the distribution of
written messages on blades, for example. The worldwide occurrence of swords
with side blades, is another thing that somebody really ought to describe.
You are operating in a very interesting - and even significant - area.
Second, I think you will find that the wonder of Japanese swords is in the
details. You will have to be very careful about how you use and treat
Japanese data, or the grey beards of nippon-to will be very grumpy.

Now on to your question.

Over the past couple of years, I have gotten quite interested in tsuba that
seem - to me - to show direct influence between Japanese and foreign
swords. In generic terms these are assigned to the "Namban" style. Within
that categories there are masses of clearly Japanese stuff. Then there are
some guards that seem to have obvious referents to European rapiers or
smallsword guards. And then there are guards that have been adapted for
use with Japanese blades altho they also seem to be of Chinese manufactures
These come in two type - at least. First there are some cupped tsuba that
seem to have been made in China and used in Japan. The other type is more
classically "Namban" but with no sign of accommodation to nippon-to. And
they show up in placed like Tibet and Nepal.
To my eye, these tsuba show diverse international influence that is not
surprising, given the way East Asia worked in the 16th and 17th century.
John Lissenden wrote a very interesting MA thesis on this school at the
University of Durham, last year. I am sure that he will try to see it
published, but that has not happened yet. Beyond that there is a lot of
other discussion of these issues whenever the Namban category is described.

Best of luck
P. Bleed

Buddy and Peter,
I've been mulling this one over, too. The only example I can think of that's a hard data point is a tsuba in the Boston Museum collection. It's number 354 in the Ogawa catalog. It is a piece that has nakago, kozuka and kogai ana that are typical of a guard made for a Japanese sword. I don't see any evidence that it was made for another type of sword and modified for nihonto use. Quoting from the catalog, this tsuba is signed:

"Nan-ching yu -- Ch'ang Lo-chiao tsao K'ang-hsi (ting) - wei (sui) Ch'ang Lo-chiao tsao

(Residing in Nanking, made by Ch'ang Lo-chiao. Made in the [ting]-sheep [year] of K'ang-hsi [1667] by Ch'ang Lo-chiao)"

It is not in one of the typical "Nanban" tsuba styles, but there is some of that taste to it in the overlapping foliage motif. Can anyone discuss or dispute the reading of the signature? Any comments on the guard?

The text mentions another example signed with the same name. I found one unsigned example with the same decorative motif and workmanship.

I don't remember seeing any guards for Chinese swords with signatures. Has anyone?

Jim

by matthewhoyle2000

Jim. that would be interesting, as at the start of the Kangxi period,
the whole of China was effectively still at war since the Qing were
taking over from the earlier Ming dynasty rulers.

When dating Chinese porcelain, we make use of a term called the
"Transitional Period" that lasts from 1620 - 1683 approx to indicate
that these pieces were made during a time of great turmoil, when the
imperial porcealain factories were not functioning (they had been
ransacked)............ however I digress.

We also know that hundreds of thousands of Japanese swords were exported
to the Chinese Court from the Kamakura period onwards into the 15th
century. However I have no literature stating that this continued into
the 17th cent, does anyone know anything about that ?

One can just imagine some metal worker in Nanking making a tsuba for his
Great Grandfathers treasured Japanese sword to take it to war once
again........

I might add it is extremely unusual for any Chinese artisan to sign
their work with the exception of perhaps paintings.

Matt
