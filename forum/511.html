Cary,
Interesting question... my initial thoughts without any effort at research:

I think that I picked up the Mito Shoami designation from Bob Haynes years ago. The term does appear in his auction catalogs, although that is not sufficient grounds for proving the existence of the group. Calling something "nameless shiiremono" would of course not advance a sale.

"Tetsu no Isho - Mito-To to Tosogu no Meihin" is indeed the book I was thinking of. Of course we wouldn't expect to see the low quality examples from any group or region illustrated or referenced. So the absence of mention is not necessarily proof of non-existence.

I don't believe that I have ever seen a kanteisho to Mito Shoami. However, when I assisted Hagihara-sensei at last year's shinsa in Jersey City, the so-called "Mito Shoami" type examples submitted were all rejected as "poor quality work," so there was no opportunity for such an attribution to be made on an NTHK kanteisho. If that is the standard judgment of the NBTHK, we would not expect to see any Mito Shoami kanteisho at all, even if the group existed.

I do not recall seeing any examples signed Mito Shoami. I have seen examples signed Aizu ju Shoami and I have seen many mumei pieces, although of better quality than the "Mito Shoami" examples, attributed to Aizu Shoami.

I assume you can picture the type of tsuba that we have been calling Mito Shoami. There are many of them in the US and the type is quite uniform. Who do you think made all of those tsuba? Serious question.

The merit of the usage of the term Mito Shoami deserves follow up. I think that we'll find that these guards are indeed ignored as shiiremono and are not likely to be documented or recognized by Japanese sources. Again, interesting observation.

Jim



Jim, Alan, Rich et al.

My remarks may be radical and heretical considering
how casually the term "Mito Shoami" is bandied about
on this list and others.

I have never seen a tsuba or other kodogu attributed
to Mito Shoami. I am of the opinion that Shoami
artisans were not productive there.

There are certainly pieces that have papered to just Mito.
But none that I know of to Mito Shoami,
in which case the basic premise is incorrect.

Jim,
The book you are referring to I presume is titled:
Tetsu no Isho - Mito-To to Tosogu no Meihin (1996).
Ibaraki Prefectural Historical Museum.

In section II - Mito Kinko no Tosogu,
The introductory text records prominent individuals
and groups that were active in Mito (Hitachi province).
Among them:
Gunji Yogoro
Yatabe Tsuji
Seiryuken Ha
Ichiryu (Hitotsuyanagi) Ha
Tamagawa Ha
Uchikoshi Ha
Yasuchika Ha (from the 4th gen. Yasuchika)
Saotome and Myochin.
And of course, Hagiya Shohei (Katsuhira) and Unno Shomin.


NO mention of Shoami in Mito.

Checking Haynes- Tsuba, An Aesthetic Study.
Under the section dealing with SHOAMI he mentions:
Kyo Shoami, Awa Shoami, Bizen Shoami, Iyo Shoami,
Aizu Shoami, Shonai Shoami, Akita Shoami,
Bushu (Edo) Shoami & Sakushu (Mimasaka) Shoami.
In addition he adds individuals who signed:
Oshu Shoami, Soshu (Odawara) Shoami, Seishu (Ise) Shoami,
Osaka (Settsu) Shoami, Banshu (Harima) Shoami,
Inshu (Inaba) Shoami & Kurume (Chikugo) Shoami.

Again NO mention of Mito Shoami.

I have never liked the term Mito Shoami
and have never used it. This thread presented
an opportunity to research the issue
to see if my opinion had any basis.

Can anyone document a piece that has been
authoritatively classified as Mito Shoami
or have we been perpetuating a fallacy?

Cary,
No doubt that these guards will remain anonymous in that we will never know the names of the individual makers. However, the uniformity of the production suggests that there was a "group" making these guards. Maybe we can know the name of the group.

I called Bob Haynes to ask about the Mito Shoami designation. He said that he thinks that he is the originator of the term, however he also said that he doesn't have any concrete proof to tie these guards to Shoami working in Mito.

I have seen tsuba signed only "Shoami" that appear to be better quality precursors of the so-called Mito Shoami shiiremono style. Those in turn are similar to some (better yet) Shonai work, and we have already touched on signed and attributed Aizu Shoami work, which Bob also mentioned today. There seems to be some regional similarity of style, and some association with the Shoami. However this does not add up to proof of the authorship of these guards or of the existance of a Mito Shoami.

Your original point is valid. I am going to continue to look into the subject. Now we have hundreds if not thousands of nameless "anonymous shiiremono" tsuba out there. Scary...

Jim
