Kuchi-beni

by klepet33319

Good day to all. I was looking at a Bizen Suruga tsuba and reading up
on the school in comparison to Bizen Shoami when it occured to me that
the placement of the kuchi beni (sekigane) by the tsubako as part of
the design may have been a purposeful endeavor to reduce wear to the
nakago or possible shock-related fracture to the tsuba as well as for
the fitting of the blade. It just hit me that the use of a more
plastic metal would result in a two-fold benefit of helping to prevent
these named problems and also in fascilitating the fitting and later
tightening of the tsuba to it's sword. That this practice occured in
Bizen could make sense in that they had a long history of sword
manufacture and when the Haruta family moved to Bizen and began tsuba
fabrication (Tsuba An Aesthetic Study pg. 31)they looked for advice
from the local smiths and began placement of the copper shims on their
observations/advice. Just a bit of conjecture but as I have seen
swords with considerable wear to the nakago from ill-fitting
(assumption) tsuba it may be a plausible assumption. The placement at
the top and bottom would also seem to make sense as the primary axis of
shock-force delivery would occur on this plane from strike/rebound
energy and would help explain why circumfirential placement was not
utilized. Interesting.

I'm up to date on email...not. Interesting idea Pete. Bushu Kunihiro used the same type of sekigane. Akasaka Tadashige made them some of the time as well. The Toryusai group used a smaller, different form, but still standardized inlaid sekigane. Anyway, the Suruga, et al, style of sekigane is often seen quite beaten down and it may well have been originally done with the intention of saving the iron plate from abuse. I don't know whether it would have affected wear of the nakago.

Jim

Re: Kuchi-beni

by excelsiorilluminati

Yes, very interesting. But I think cart before the horse. As the
early swordsmiths made their own tsuba with sword to hand and could
be fitted in situ the use of shims of any form would be unnecessary.
As swords are forged by a particular smith the general dimensions
would have been consistent and the nakago-ana would have been made
consistent to these dimensions. As the fabrication of tsuba became a
cottage industry, dependent on the swordsmith hence the close
proximity of them, the tsuba-ko had to fit their tsuba not knowing
what measurements they may see and the sekigane shims developed. The
effect that had on the ability to absorb shock or reduce wear would
have been a subsequent discovery. Also may I suggest that sekigane
may have been added to some tsuba later that had worn loose to
tighten the tsuka as well as adding seppa.The rattle of a loose
tsuba is extremely annoying. These are just my take on the possible
development of such.
John
