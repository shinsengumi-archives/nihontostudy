by retalt

Patina on tsuba is placed on at manufacture and in the old days,
included a mixture of red clay, straw, rat dung, sulphur, gunpowder,salt
and other things. Many layers and applications are applied. It's a time
consuming thing. These are of course old recipes and they may have
chemical replacements for things like dung these days.

Rich

Re: [nihontostudy] Nihiki design ...

by yamaimojg

John,
If I am picturing it correctly, the rectangular openings are usually interpreted as representing measuring boxes (masu). The design with two boxes is called sayu-masu (left and right boxes).

My Japanese language skills are minimal, but I only know of nihiki as the "counting word" used for two small animals. I asked my shodo teacher (first native speaker I remembered to ask) if she could think of any other meaning for nihiki or a way that it might relate to that motif, but no luck.

Do you know what the kanji are for that nihiki?

It just occurred to me to try a google image search on nihiki. Most hits are photos of pairs of dogs, cats, rabbits, etc., but interestingly this jpg http://www005.upp.so-net.ne.jp/minbun/news/news21/nihiki.jpg
of a kimono turned up. Kimono sleeves are normally called sode as far as I know, but the two rectangular sleeves would fit the two rectangle motif. The nihiki 二引 in the caption I think refers to the kind of garment, but I don't know the meaning. It's probably a red herring. The source page is here:

http://www005.upp.so-net.ne.jp/minbun/news/news21/news21.html

Oddly on the same page I noticed this
http://www005.upp.so-net.ne.jp/minbun/news/news21/kodougu.jpg
which looks like a large harvesting knife in shirasaya with a koshirae. I've seen plenty of bamboo splitting knives with blades like that, but not with a habaki or koshirae. Something for a performance? Does anyone know?

Jim
