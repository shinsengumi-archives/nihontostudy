Re: [nihontostudy] Book Information

by arnold frenzel

Hi:
I should know a little about Fred's book as I wrote a brief foreword
for it. I contains some useful information derived from various sources,
however the whole area of books on gendai is rapidly changing and there
is no definitive book out there to the best of my knowledge. Fred's book
is a contribution to the literature and there are a number of similar
books in the same space.
The problem is that the very term "gendai", meaning current era, has
never been well defined in year brackets and it is slipping into disuse.
For some it is for
all blades made after the Haitorei regulations which culminated in the
banning of civilian wearing of swords in public in 1876, for others the
term starts with the blades of the Showa era, that being 1926-1989, and
even after, and for others it ends in 1945 or shortly after, say with
the end of the Occupation in 1953. The term used now for blades made
contemporarily is "shinsakuto" or "new made swords" and that gets pushed
back to the end of gendaito, whenever that might be, so take your pick.
Much of what is out there now represents culled work from other sources
and there is little original research. Mr. Chris Bowen, who was resident
in Japan for some years, since having returned to the USA, has gathered
a lot of information on gendai smiths of the Tokyo area and has stated
that a book will eventually be produced. That is something I am eagerly
looking forward to as it will contain original information in English on
a number of smiths, particularly those working during the last war,
about whom little is known outside or inside Japan. I have no idea when
it will see the light of day.
One recent book that you should get is Yasukuni To by Tom Kishida and
published this year by Kodansha. It is focused primarily on the blades
made at the Yasukuni Jinja in Tokyo, but in putting them in context it
says a fair amount about the low state of blade making prior to 1933,
tells us something about the destruction of the basis for the craft
right after 1876 and also discusses the traditions from which the
Yasukuni smiths came from and something of their work after the war. It
is a must have book!
I hope this helps.
Regards,
Arnold Frenzel
