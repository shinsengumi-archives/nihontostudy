Re: [nihontostudy] Will this Katana polish?

by Peter A Bleed

Dear Peter,
I think anyone looking at these images will tell you that this blade can
polish just fine. The shape does not seem to be highly compromised and we
have all seen blades in far worse condition come thru polish to be
beautiful. Before you decide to commit to a polish, however, I think I will
not be the only one to urge you to move slowly.
First, I'd try to do a bit of kantei. This sure does not look like a
shin-shinto to me. If it is as new as you think, then somebody has
falsified the nakago in ways that would make it hard for me to ever "like"
this blade. Its been dummied up and no matter how well it comes thru
polish, after a while I suspect that the tang will become hard to over
look. In fact, I bet this is a koto that deserves to be brought back.
Secondly, the fact that you cannot see a hamon has to be investigated. A
yakiba will typically show even when a blade is in wretched condition. If
you can't see anything - even tho you look hard - I think you have to
assume that the blade has "lost its temper." In that case, a polish would
be a total waste of your time and money. The good news is that I think your
images DO show a tempered edge. Again, I think the blade deserves
restoration. To be sure, tho, I suggest that this is a prime candidate for
having a window opened to be sure that it is intact.
Good luck
Peter Bleed

Re: Will this Katana polish?

by nihontochicken

A brief experience of mine in my former criminal life as an amateur
polisher (gunto only!). I had two blades, both Pacific War era,
nearly identically rusty (i.e., very rusty, big crusts of red/black
oxide, hard to see the hamon). One was a traditionally made blade by
Masamura, the other a Showa stamped blade by Kumemune. As it turned
out, the pitting was very shallow on the Kumemune, but very, very
deep on the Masamura, despite the nearly identical appearance on the
surfaces of the two blades. I couldn't get all the pitting out of
the Masamura, was just too deep, would've had to whittle the blade
down to nothing! With the Kumemune, I had more work to do to iron
out the waves left from the original polish. Bottom line, you can't
tell by looking (except perhaps if you are a professional polisher).
You need to determine if it's worth the expense to have a
professional evaluate the blade and perhaps open a window to see
whether a full polish is appropriate. FWIW.

NC

RE: [nihontostudy] Will this Katana polish?

by moses_becerra

Hello Peter,



Your sword is in a very bad condition and the responsible thing to say
is that it may not polish out well. From what I can see the rust is
very deep. The fact that it is so deep is not a good thing because a
polisher will need to go just as deep to remove it from the sword. It's
not about having a soft hand as a polisher; it's about how deep the rust
actually is. Going that deep in a sword can bring up fukure, shintetsu
and other flaws. Not to mention the fact that the sword's hamon can
also get softer after going down deep like that. The nakago was worked
on in some way, perhaps a false signature was removed? I don't know.
In any case to me the sword looks like a Shinto blade but that's just my
humble opinion. Now for some good news, it is very possible for swords
in this condition to polish out properly and become fine looking swords.
But just take your time, because the expense and risk involved in the
restoration of such a sword is not inexpensive.



Moses Becerra

Re: Will this Katana polish?

by nihontochicken

I see Moses has joined in on this thread, so I will, of course, defer
to whatever he says. As an addendum to my earlier comments, I burned
up a Lohman polishing kit ($550) on full polishes for three gunto,
plus some minor polishes on a few others. The Kumemune was a
pleasant surprise (crusty rust over relatively minor pitting). The
Masamura was more typical (crusty rust over deep pitting). This
brings me to Personal Rule #1 re pitting on rusty swords: "It is
always deeper than you think." Even light white frosting has pitting
involved, properly requiring the use of foundation stones in my
experience. Hence Personal Rule #2: "Never buy rusty swords."
(Unless, of course, a sword is still a bargain with the price of a
professional polish included, which is a big roll of the dice,
considering on what may show up during a major repolish.) JMHO.

NC
