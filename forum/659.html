gold inlay question

by matthewhoyle2000

Gang, I was looking at a sword with a cutting test inscription inlayed
in gold today. Part of it had fallen out.

Now I understand the the inscriptions were done (composed) by the Honami
family, but who actually inlayed the inscription in gold and how was
this done ?

Did a smith do this, or a kinko artist perhaps ? Or did they do it
themselves ?

- Matt

Re: [nihontostudy] gold inlay question

by yamaimojg

Matt,
Not exactly my field, but I believe that they were done by kinko. The Umetada did some kinzogan-mei for the Honami. I seem to recall another specific group that worked for the Honami but I don't remember who it was. I'm sure that a variety of kinko inlaid kinzogan-mei and cutting tests. Maybe someone else on the list knows more.

If I understood correctly, during one of his lectures at the Tampa show this year Tanobe-san mentioned that original Honami kinzogan-mei should be slightly lower than the surface of the nakago. He explained that as the nakago rusts over the years the volume of the resulting iron oxide is greater than that of the iron it developed from. So the gold inlay stays at the level of the original filed surface, but the surrounding iron "grows thicker" as it rusts. I haven't checked it out for myself, though. Anyone have a good Honami kinzogan-mei handy to check?

Jim

by yamaimojg

Matt,
Tanobe-san was referring to Honami "attribution mei" rather than cutting tests. I don't know whether cutting test inscriptions were initially filed flush with the surface of the nakago or not. If the inlay was raised at all there won't be a reference point. Also, an old but false cutting test or attribution would presumably have the same "depth" as a correct one of the same age. I wouldn't take it as a certain test of the validity of an inlaid inscription.

I heard another tidbit about Umetada habaki once that I haven't been able to verify. Their habaki are certainly of very high quality and the construction and finish are distinctive, but I was told that the Umetada were exceptionally good at fitting habaki to o-suriage swords. I'm not sure what to make of that... anyone have any ideas? I've certainly seen Umetada habaki on ubu tanto, so their type is not strictly developed for the nakago of shortened blades.

Jim

was probably made by a country maker, any thoughts on this would be welcome.
