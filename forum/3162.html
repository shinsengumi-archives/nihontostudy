Question for tsuba experts

by davidzincavage

I have a tsuba which has a certain amount of what looks like black lacquer remaining on a minority of its surface. Should I be boiling this tsuba, trying to remove whatever is left? Or should I preserve it as is?

An almost identical tsuba (evidently without lacquer) is identified in Haynes catalogue 2 as "Ko Umetada style."

Re: Question for tsuba experts

by neobliviscar

David,

I would say yours is later than the ko-umetada. I
don't know how much later. I'm just basing my opinion
off the seppa dai and some of the other measurements.
Maybe yours is 50 to 100 years younger than the
ko-umetada photographed. My confidence level isn't
super high though, so don't quote me. Just sticking my
neck out to risk an opinion.

As to the black lacquer- thats your call. If the
lacquer is fair to good quality, I personally would be
inclined to leave it. I'm of the minimally invasive
school of thought. Clean it to a preservation point of
view.
If the lacquer is well done and has survived this
long, I consider it part of the atmosphere of the
piece. If its a hack job, boil away.

Curran

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Question for tsuba experts

by musashibobenkei

David,

Not disagreeing with Curran at all, as I am a cautious
preservationist. "Do no harm" is my motto, and from the photos though
it looks like the lacquer doesn't add to the appeal, I would be
worrying what the patina will look like in those areas if you do
remove it. If it ends up blotchy, you would have to look at
repatinating the whole piece, leaving it hardly original.

Of course the argument can be reasonably made that the lacquer (or
paint, Milt) - being a later add-on - makes it impossible to have the
"original" article without some restoration. While there are those who
believe that all tsuba as we have them today have been messed with and
should be taken down to bare metal and patinated "as originally
intended," they are in the minority, and I would question their claim
to certain knowledge of what was "originally intended" by the artisan.

Wait a few weeks, David, you will get a number of more qualified
opinions in person at the San Francisco Token Kai.

Craig

by arnold frenzel

Hi:
Almost all old iron, particularly tosho and katchushi, would have had
lacquer. It is a real plus to have even traces remaining. Sometimes soft
metal will also have lacquer traces. Do not remove.
Arnold
