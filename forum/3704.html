Tsubas made of iron meteorite? May be.

by sosidore

Dear all,

In Chinese documented 4000 years of history meteorite have long been
regarded as precious materials. Ancient Chinese craftsmen processed
stone meteorites into varieties of item for religious worshipping
purposes. I guess iron meteorites were also used for same purposes.
Only they weathered away in time sooner than their stonny counter part.
I posted once again 2 iron tsubas onto my folder entitled SOSIDORE.
This time they revealed cleared gilded finish.
I suspeceted they were made of iron meteorite based on following
reasons.

1. They are lighter than iron.
Iron meteorites are rarely pure iron. They usually contain nickel. Some
meteorites contain plenty of iron. They are heavy in weight and
weathered away much sooner. Some iron meteorites contain plenty of
nickel. They are apparently lighter and can better resist weather. The
two iron tsubas showed here are relatively light. They showed
superficial rusting only, evidencing higher content of nickel. Initial
observations support the idea that they might be once a part of an iron
meteorite with considerable content of nickel.
The chiseled holes revealed a secret about the distribution of
materials within tsubas are not even. Again it points to a possibility
that the tsuba materials could have been iron-nickel alloy with content
of which not evenly distributed. And this is characteristics quite
common in case of meteorite.

I purchased these two iron tsubas from a Chinese vendor who used to
sell antiques. There is strong possibility these twp artifacts were
unearthed in China.

They way these 2 iron tsubas gilded was typical Chinese. However, I am
pretty sure that in Japan mirror craftsman followed exactly same method
in treating mirror surface with mercury. The same technique can come
out with gilded finish when adding gold-grains into the mixture of
rubbed-on materials.

Now, I am wondering if these two iron tsubas were of Japanese origin.

The most convincing clues of all are the inscriptions.
1. Chinese way of arranging characters always comes from top down and
from left to right. In this case. They are to be read from top down
then left to right.
2. The content of inscriptions are typical Japanese.
3. The style of fonts are typically Japanese.

The three points mentioned excluded the possibility that they were
originally made in China. Even if they were once contracted with
Chinese craftsmen made to Japanese order the font of characters
concerned betrayed the fact that Chinese people never trained to write
nor processed kanji onto surface of any materials in Japanese style.
Only Japanese can..

For sure these two iron trubas are not isolated case. I wonder where
are the rest of them and in what form? Anyone knows any record about
iron meteorite processed into any other type of items that became part
of Nihonto?

SOSIDORE
Chen, Ying-chaw
Taipei

Re: Tsubas made of iron meteorite? May be.

by drjohnplissenden

While I cannot add any knowledge to "sosidore"'s on the subject of
meteoric iron, I cannot let his comments pass without making some of
my own.

The two artefacts that he illustrates, with their square apertures,
demonstrate a common enigma in deciding what use they were intended
for. They were clearly not intended for mounting on a Japanese
sword and, for this reason cannot be considered to be 'tsuba'.
Neither is this aperture compatible with the tang of a Chinese
sword, and my own belief is that such artefacts were intended for
mounting on a tie-jian, or Chinese 'fighting stick'.

The question of their nickel content can surely be resolved, either
by modern physical tecniques such as laser micro-spectral or atomic
absorption spectral analysis. Surely the effect of the forging
process would be to more evenly distribute the nickel thoughout the
base metal? Much more simply, the simple measurement of their
density might be a guide.

As for the use of mercuric gilding, this is clealy only practicable
on copper artefacts or on those of the soft-metal alloys containing
this element. Before the use of this gilding tecnique on iron, the
artefact needs to be coated with a copper film, and this can
normally be seen where the gold has subsequently separated.

Regards, John L.

Re: Tsubas made of iron meteorite? May be.

by sosidore

Thank you Dr. Lissenden for your article.
The inscriptions shown on oblong shape tsuba read "To-yo(Oriental)
Martial Sword" in English. (I guess it strongly suggested that the
iron pieces are tsubas and not anything else.)
The way To-yo combined into new Kanji phrase is unique to Japan. It
indicats that the tsuba could be dated as early as when emperor
Meiji came to power or even earlier as I would explain later.
Western metal processing technique was then not yet introduced into
Japan. This probably explains why the quality of workmanship appears
to be out dated.
Meteorite of all kind have their own earth age respectively
measurable counting from the day they reached the globe.
I hope someday some scientists would be interested enough in doing
the dating for this tsuba.
Once determined of its age people could better tell its background
by linking its age with history of Sino-Japanese cultural enchange.
Tsuba or any other name one might have it called I am sure it must
have been a part of one very unusual martial sword exactly as the
inscription illustrated.
The owner of which could be rather important so qualifiable to claim
the ownership of the rare iron meteorite. Who is this man? I wonder.
By inscripting tsuba with "TO-YO(oriental) MARTIAL SWORD" The sword
must have been made with an western reciepient in mind. Again an
important figure. Who is this western man?
Here comes one interesting question. Why the Japanese craftman did
not inscribe the said tsuba straight forwardly with "Japanese
martial sword"?
Please note that during Chinese Ming dynasty, which is earlier than
Meiji period, Japan shipped a lot of porcelain art to Europe bearing
mostly Chinese designs. And remarkable portioned of them marked with
Chinese way of dating.
(This was happened when China sealed its seaport to contain its own
people within.)
Japanese porcelain exporters were aiming to impress European buyers
that the porcelain they shipped were of China origin. For in
European's mind then whatever oriental was mysteriously Chinese.
Somewhere in Japan long ago there might have been a recorded
meteoric fall. An iron meteor broke into many pieces before falling.
There in the local museum some specimen could still be found. What
if this tsuba happens to contain exactly same content as the
exsisting ones?
Should that be the case I would be happy to laon my tsuba to that
Japanese museum for the sake of happy reunion -- after many
centuries of roaming.

Chen, Yingchaw
Taipei
