Matthew and Folk -

Drilling is self-explanatory. A hole is drilled with a tool steel
bit. In punching, the smith heats up the section of the nakago to
red heat, and using a round steel punch, hammers a deep dimple on one
side. He then turns the blade over and centers the punch over the
dimple on the other side, and whacks it with a hammer. Done
correctly, a small, round coin is ejected, and the hole is complete.
Fresh punched holes will show rounded shoulders, and may show in the
interior the section where the little coin broke off. Drilled holes
will have sharp, square shoulders, and uniform walls, likely showing
spiral marks on the sides. I believe most of the WWII traditional
nihonto had punched ana, but don't know what percentage of non-
traditional gunto had drilled ana.
