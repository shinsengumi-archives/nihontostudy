Tsuba artisans: Norisuke I and Norisuke II

by neobliviscar

Dear Lists,

I've been reading and researching Norisuke I &
Norisuke II for a while. There is a good bit of
information on them in Art & Sword Vol. 3, the Owari
Tsuba book, and even a book dedicated entirely to
their work and that of a few of their students.

Question: Has anyone ever seen a tsuba signed by
both Gen1 ('Niwa' Norisuke) and by Gen2 ('Iwata'
Norisuke)?
I would think there would be more than a few, as
their lives overlapped a good bit.

There are no joint signed examples in the books, so
I'm hoping someone has seen or owns a joint work.

Thank you in advance,

Curran

I haven't seen one illustrated or in person. Jointly signed works are very uncommon in fittings. Most examples that I can think of are where the plate was prepared by one person and the decoration applied by another.

It'd be an interesting study to assemble a group (even photos) of jointly signed pieces of any type.

Re: [nihontostudy] Tsuba artisans: Norisuke I and Norisuke II

by neobliviscar

Dear Jim,

Thank you for the reply.
I have a nice tsuba signed (Niwa) Norisuke on the
front and Norishige (early sig. of Iwata Norisuke) on
the back. It is a pleasant design of a pine tree. A
design I like alot.

I was away in Connecticut for a while and did not have
access to my books. Hitting the Owari to Mikawa no
Tanko (aka the big blue Owari book), I noted a similar
one signed and dated 1851 by Norisuke the 1st. It is
usually the last one listed in any book, as I believe
it is the latest date at which he was known to have
made a tsuba.

Given that these guys were fairly prolific:
I expected to eventually find some other joint
works. A list member even loaned me the Norisuke book.
There are no joint works in there or any other
reference I have opened.

To me the workmanship of the tsuba looks like that of
Iwata Norisuke. But the signature on the front is Niwa
Norisuke.
So maybe Iwata Norisuke made it and his teacher
signed it- or a fun but much less likely possibility
(remember that these are master forgers) Iwata
Norisuke forged his teacher's signature? Unlikely, but
given the marked similarity of their signatures- fun
to imagine.

Any recommendations who would be best in Japan to ask
about this tsuba?

Curran
