by sworddad

Hi Curran -

It is probably wise to become familiar with some of the "enhancements" techniques used on iron tsuba, simply to recognize them when you come across them. More important though, would be to jump at any opportunity to actually "see" first-hand, truly first class iron tsuba and to let your mind absorb (even subconsciously) what you are seeing. The Higo display and Jim Gilbert's sukashi display (which I missed) in Chicago was super! These days, each show has similar displays for us to enjoy. I believe Bill Miller did one on Satsuma tsuba last Tampa show. Check out those "high dollar" tsuba on dealers' tables. San Francisco had a fair number of Juyo tsuba (and other kodogu) out for sale. Evenually, exposing yourself to this real "art" quality--what's really fine art--will (hopefully) develop into a better appreciation of iron tsuba, and you will find yourself passing over the hundreds of average works and homing in on that one tsuba that has that "something extra" which your mind is drawing you to. Buy that one!

Long winded yes, but you see what I'm getting at. Personally, I continue to admire lots of different tsuba but when that special one comes along, hopefully, I'll grab it.

Ron
STL

Re: [nihontostudy] Primped/doctored tsuba

by yamaimojg

Ken,

>I would think that papered pieces would not be very primped.

Usually.

>Wouldn't the judges be fairly good at detection and the piece's score would be negatively impacted by such a discovery?

Yes.

A clumsy repatination, resurfacing of the piece, major rusting, paint, shoe polish, etc., will result in a failure.

OTOH, a perfectly done restoration that is exactly right for the original piece will be no problem.

The in between stuff will have a point consequence. Condition counts.

Cleaning off rust is of course OK as long as the surface isn't damaged, but pits will remain and they will count against condition.

A piece that has been cleaned to the point that it looks younger than it should can be a problem. This is where cleaning out the sukashi openings can hurt you even if you personally like the way that looks. If the age cues are contradictory, the judgment tends to go to the younger date. The object of conservation or even restoration is not to make the guard look new.

Clear wax isn't going to help a bad surface at shinsa, but it shouldn't hurt a good one either. If an owner really likes his pieces waxy or lives on the ocean it shouldn't be a big problem, since the wax can be seen through and is not a permanent coating. I suppose that someone could come up with a way to over do it to the point where the guard looks so strange that the real origin comes into question.

Polishing guards to the point where the surface becomes shiny and looses surface character is a problem. Burnishing obscures the traits of the iron necessary for kantei. It's really a slower process to get to the same place as resurfacing and/or poor repatination.

Like Ron said, look at all of the great pieces you can get your eyes on and remember them.
Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Primped/doctored tsuba

by yamaimojg

Milt,
If it is actively corroding you should stop it. There is no sense in allowing a piece to degrade in your care. If the sukashi is extremely fragile, maybe a drop of camellia oil would be the right approach. It will darken the rust (a lot), but obviously that is not as bad as damaging the iron. If a tool is required to scrape, I'd pick a very thin, flexible one that would bend with less force that it'd take to damage the sukashi.

Any other ideas on stabilizing corrosion in a sensitive area like this?

Jim
