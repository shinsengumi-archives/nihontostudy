Hello Patrik -

Actually, I like the looks of this sword even though the blade condition is out-of-polish. The mountings look interesting with Higo style fuchi and kashira, nice shakudo menuki that look possibly early, nice tsuba. The blade seems to have a nice shape and the kissaki being extended makes me think possibly sue-koto possibly. The hamon as was mentioned, seems a chu-suguha. I just wonder if this could be a sue-koto Bungo blade, a wild guess considering its condition but I'll say that anyway. Hope you end up with it.

Re: [nihontostudy] need some advices on a sword...

by sworddad

Hi Mark -

As Patrik wrote a few messages ago, he has purhased the sword and at the
price, I would think he could not had gone wrong with it. Will be quite
interesting to see if the blade has a mei or not! (Trying to get an atari
with my Bungo-to thought!)

>Has anyone else out there been denied access to a nakago? I'm curious.

In the past fast-approaching 40 years (now that sound scarey!!) I would
suspect I've been denied access to a nakago perhaps half-dozen times. If you
have the opportunity to have the sword in-hand, then at least you can make a
judgement from the blade's quality. If buying it from a photo, well, it is a
gamble and would certainly limit how much you would offer for it because of
that gamble. I've found that most "owners" will, if you explain it to them
in a knowledgeable and polite manner, will allow the tsuka to be removed. If
buying through the mail, once they learn about the possibility of a
signature, curiosity usually takes over and off comes the tsuka. (Always
important to give clear instructions for doing this such as protecting
habaki, tsuba, etc., from excessive force!)

When looking at a sword that's presented to you, it is always advisable --
for your own education -- to make a judgement about the blade before looking
at the nakago. That alone can save you some money by being dazzled by a "big
mei." All fun stuff!!

Ron
STL
