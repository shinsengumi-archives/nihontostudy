I didnt get the sword as pictured in the ebay auction ...

by ranox27157

I didnt get the sword as pictured in the ebay auction, but what I
actually got has now caused a bit of concern. I brought the sword
over to a freind's party wearing it as a costume accessory, as I
thought it was a fake with little value. At the party their was a
gentleman that is a metallurgy expert, he told me he has spent years
researching the ancient technology of Japanese sword making and
personally had met with several professors in Japan. He carefully
examined my sword and made a few comments. His comment are as
follows. The blade was hand forged, not machine forged and not
etched with acid. He spoke in lenth about the temper of the blade
and types of metal used. The tang of the sword had been shorted and
reshaped from the original form. The overall shape and shape of the
point, was consistent with an older form (the blade narrows down to
a small point). I am seeking to learn the parts of the blade, and
not sound so much like a rookie.
He further told me some interesting things about the sword, and
it these things that have caused my concern. The mounting of the
sword is consistent with special mountings used as presentation
swords to be given to visiting German Officers. The said that about
183 swords were remade as these presentation swords. The thing for
me to worry about is that the swords used were in some cases family
swords, some were obtained without the family's permission, and some
were donated because there was now aparent heirs. He went on to
explain that the family swords were in many cases not marked by the
maker or smith (no MEI). But a good clue to a family sword is the
the etching or engraving of a name on the blade. That is the name of
a warrior killed by the sword (and by placing the warriors name on
the blade, his soul is captured in the steel). Swords having these
markings on the blade are very traceable, and there might be a legal
dispute should someone in the family seek the return of the sword to
their family.
I took the handle off the tang and photographed the parts of the
sword as best I could. I will be posting the pictures in a photo
folder named GERMAN. I would like any comments on the blade directed
to me personally. < Ranox27157@... > Should it turn out that
this is one of the presentation swords, but without a legal issue, I
will either sell it or donate in to the loical WWII museum.

by bescat

I have a book called, Military Swords of Japan 1868-1945, by Richard
Fuller & Ron Gregory and in parts of the book it does say that some of
the Swords which were surrendered (not all, but some) had letters with
them, some had engravings put onto them.

Here is a caption from page 103, Plate 110:
This well-engraved surrender inscription appears on the habaki of an
army officer's shin-gunto. The obverse reads, 'Surrendered by Shosa
(Major) Nagamura Kiyotaka, Japanese VIII army, Emirau Island, 21st March
1944'. The reverse reads, 'To A. B. Boyles, U.S.M.C.'. This is an
example of a spurious postwar engraving added to increase value:
research indicates that Emirau was evacuated by the Japanese two months
before the invasion of the US 4th Marines on 20 March 1944. The USMC
has no record of an A. B. Boyles. (R. Gregory collection.)

My point is, there are swords with Inscriptions on them. You need to
thoroughly check it out. Research your pieces.

Kim
