sometimes got only a kogai?

Re: [nihontostudy] kogai on katana

by alangbale

Richard,
It was the custom to have a kogai and matching menuki on a katana in the
16th century, the set was called a futokoromono, these were often turned
into a 3 piece set or mitokoromono with the later addition of a kozuka made
to order with the same motif . Kozuka were much less common until the later
16th century, the mitokoromono then were mainly mounted on wakizashi.
Alan

I don't KNOW but I am always full of theories...
Think of the uses for these two, a kogai was used to dress the hair
from way back, there is also evidence it was used to carry captured
heads. Practical battlefield applications.
The kind of cutting done in battle was too rough for a small
knife and was certainly work more suited for a tanto or wakizashi.
Once you enter the Edo period, there were many more uses for a
"pocket knife" EG; cutting paper to leave a small note somewhere,
trimming a frayed edge, opening a bundle...
Todays trivia question; what does Michelle Yeoh pull from her
hair to hand Zhang Zyi to use as a "pass" into the Wudan warehouse in
the final scene of Crouching Tiger Hidden Dragon?

Re: [nihontostudy] kogai on katana

by robsongord

Hi Tom and all,

In the Sasano book I previously mentioned, he claims that kozuka as well as
kogai date back to the Heian period. He supports this with ubu-ana in early
tsuba. Of course, you have to accept that his dating of undated,
undocumented tsuba are as early as Heian and Kamakura periods. It has
recently become fashionable to make all of Sasano's dates much later
(younger?). I also have serious doubts about kogai being used for marking or
carrying heads. I don't think they would stand up to that kind of abuse.
Picking ears and combing hair, certainly. Nonetheless, there certainly are
tsuba, and tsuba that are clearly early -- Nambokucho to be conservative --
that have ubu kozuka ana as well as ubu kogai ana.

Gordon
