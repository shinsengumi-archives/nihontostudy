by Arnold Frenzel

Hi. The most common theory as to why there is such a high incidence of
shintetsu showing in Hizen swords is that they were economizing in the
production process, ie, reducing costs by using less of the outer skin
material which requires more folding and hence is more expensive. I have
never found that to be a very good answer as they were, after all,
patronized by a major Daimyo and Hizen, as a major trading center, both as
an entrepot and as a ceramic producer, and large koku domain, could hardly
have been poor. I have no basis whatsoever for this but I wonder if the
answer might lie in their choice to make blades with a relatively larger,
in a cross section sense, core steel. That would make the blade more
flexible. Do any of you who are up on the construction part of swords find
this makes sense from a functional point of view?
It is interesting to note that in the Chicago display of Rai blades last
year, from which Hizen took its model, there was vertually no shintetsu to
be seen!
Cheers.

Arnold Frenzel

Re: Hinzento

by nihontochicken

Arnold (and Folk) -

It's so good to hear from you, Arnold. I appreciate your delving
through the massive week's input to nihonto ramblings and picking the
wheat from the chaff. I hope I don't pop a bubble here, but
re "flexibility", the thickness of the kawagane vs. the shingane
doesn't make any difference from a metallurgical viewpoint. It is
perhaps surprising that all iron and steels have the same Young's
modulus (ratio of strain to stress in the elastic region). This is
perhaps amazing, in that, say, low carbon steel (as in shingane) is
mostly pearlite embedded in body centered cubic iron matrix, whereas
quench hardened kawagane and hagane is mostly acicular needles of
martensite, body centered tetragonal unit cell. And yet (to me,
counterintuitively), the Youngs moduli are the same. For the same
elastic stress, the same elastic strain (bending) results. Now, this
applies only up to the elastic limit in the gross sense. That is, at
a certain point, soft steel will permanently bend at a lower applied
stress than a hard steel. But as long as neither's elastic limit is
exceeded, the "flexibility" is identical. As a personal note, I have
read supposed old Japanese comments that western swords are "too
flexible". Again, this makes no sense below the elastic limit. All
iron and steel acts the same below the elastic limit. The difference
is that, at a certain stress, Japanese blades will permanently bend,
whereas the overall harder European blades will not. Then again, the
European blades may be more prone to brittle fracture. But this very
much depends on the quality of the individual Japanese and European
blades being compared. But, in general, European swords are no
more "flexible" than Japanese blades, and no Japanese swords are
more "flexible" than any others; it only depends (roughly) on the
cross-section vs. length, up to the point the blades are permanently
bent. And, generally, Japanese blades (hard edge, soft core) will
permanently bend before European swords (spring steel throughout)
will (but, then again, maybe not fracture).

NC

Re: Hinzento

by nihontochicken

Arnold (and Folk) -

Yup, believe it or not, the modulus of elasticity is approximately
the same for all steels. Amazing, but true. Here's a quote from
Physical Metallurgy For Engineers (Clark & Varney, 1962), p.198:

"Sometimes stiffness of a part is an important consideration. For
this requirement, aloy steels do not offer any advantages over the
plain carbon steels. Stiffness is determined by the modulus of
elasticity or the relationship between the stress and the strain
within the elastic limit. An alloy steel may have a higher elastic
limit than a plain carbon steel, but the modulus of elasticity will
be the same, regardless of heat treatment. The solution to stiffness
problems (ed. - no dirty cracks from Das Klub!), when steel is
demanded, is one of design and not metallurgy."

So as far as limiting "flexibility", the soft core does nothing. I'm
guessing it's mostly a cost consideration (at least in the old days,
just a carried over tradition nowadays). Of course, there might be
other considerations. Perhaps a low carbon steel core (pearlite in a
ferritic matrix) as compared to an unhardened medium carbon steel
core (mostly pearlite) exhibits greater resistance to crack
propagation and resultant brittle fracture. I kinda doubt it, but
it's a possibility. Given the very clean traditional tamahagane, I
imagine the Reference Transformation Temperature (temp below which
the steel behaves brittly) is below zero degrees F for unhardened
hagane and kawagane (of course, hardened hagane is quite brittle at
normal room temp).

I have discussed this sword construction consideration with amateur
US smiths, some of whom skip the soft core insertion, and just fold
forge the blade from the same medium carbon steel throughout, and
maintain the result is better than the traditional alternative, since
using hagane throughout will yield a sword more resistant to bending
due the the higher yield strength of an unhardened medium carbon
steel core as compared to an unhardened low carbon steel core.

So the comments of the Japanese abroad that western swords are "too
flexible" ring hollow. If such were indeed the case, it was due to
geometry (as ratio of length to cross sectional thickness), not due
to the difference in the steels. Class closed, test tomorrow!

NC

Friends,
Much of what NC and Arnold have discussed about metallurgy is beyond me,
but I wish to mention a variable that my (now sadly deceased) colleague
Bill Weins suggested to me about the nippon-to. Bill and one of his
students (and I) published a small paper calling attention to the residual
stresses within Japanese swords. Bill's insights was that strong
compressive pressure on the hamon contributed to - indeed, allowed - the
martensite of the edge to withstand the abuse of a swung sword. I think
this is NOT entirely germane to the point the NC is making, but Bill argued
that a factor that contributed to the ability to set up the compressive
tension was the fact that nippon-to are in essence composite structures.
That is, they are combination of different orders of steel heightens a
blade's ability to hold the cutting edge in compression. That may explain
the development of the multi-part cross sections.
Peter Bleed

Re: Hinzento

by nihontochicken

Peter (and Folk) -

You are correct re the compressive residual stress inside a nihonto
ha, which allows the very brittle martensite to survive the quench
hardening process (well, usually). However, this residual
compressive stress is enhanced by the relative expansion of the
martensitic ha as compared to the ferritic/pearlitic core, but is not
fundamentally caused by it. Due to the much more rapid cooling of
the ha, there would be a residual compressive stress left in it even
were there no phase transformations at all, such as in a uniform
austenitic nickel steel. I am dubious, however, as to how much this
residual compressive stress adds to the longevity of the ha in battle
use. Imagine the brittle ha under compression like a string of, say,
20 child's wooden blocks squeezed between your hands. Push really
hard! Now have someone tap the middle block from one side, and watch
the blocks spray out. And so I've seen nihonto with big chips blown
out of the ha, perhaps likewise ejected by an off-center, sideways
strike to the ha by something hard. In any event, I don't think any
of the following has any effect on the "flexibility" issue, the
elastic bending of the sword below the yield stress or brittle
fracture stress. Again, "flexibility" of an iron/steel sword does
not depend on carbon/alloy content or heat treatment. Not intuitive
at all to me.

BTW, I obviously forgot which list I was on in a previous reply, and
so apologize. Also, the elastic modulus (Young's modulus) is, of
course, the ratio of the stress to the strain, not the reverse as I
erroneously said before. If you maintain the units, any error
becomes obvious. That is why physicists rarely have a problem,
whereas engineers tend to get into trouble, crashing space probes
into planets and whatnot. ;o)

> > > wheat from the chaff. I hope I don't pop a bubble here, but
> > > re "flexibility", the thickness of the kawagane vs. the shingane
> > > doesn't make any difference from a metallurgical viewpoint. It
> is
> > > perhaps surprising that all iron and steels have the same Young's
> > > modulus (ratio of strain to stress in the elastic region). This
> is
> > > perhaps amazing, in that, say, low carbon steel (as in shingane)
> is
> > > mostly pearlite embedded in body centered cubic iron matrix,
> whereas
> > > quench hardened kawagane and hagane is mostly acicular needles of
> > > martensite, body centered tetragonal unit cell. And yet (to me,
> > > counterintuitively), the Youngs moduli are the same. For the
> same
> > > elastic stress, the same elastic strain (bending) results. Now,
> this
> > > applies only up to the elastic limit in the gross sense. That
> is, at
> > > a certain point, soft steel will permanently bend at a lower
> applied
> > > stress than a hard steel. But as long as neither's elastic limit
> is
> > > exceeded, the "flexibility" is identical. As a personal note, I
> have
> > > read supposed old Japanese comments that western swords are "too
> > > flexible". Again, this makes no sense below the elastic limit.
> All
> > > iron and steel acts the same below the elastic limit. The
> difference
> > > is that, at a certain stress, Japanese blades will permanently
> bend,
> > > whereas the overall harder European blades will not. Then again,
> the
> > > European blades may be more prone to brittle fracture. But this
> very
> > > much depends on the quality of the individual Japanese and
> European
> > > blades being compared. But, in general, European swords are no
> > > more "flexible" than Japanese blades, and no Japanese swords are
> > > more "flexible" than any others; it only depends (roughly) on the
> > > cross-section vs. length, up to the point the blades are
> permanently
> > > bent. And, generally, Japanese blades (hard edge, soft core)
> will
> > > permanently bend before European swords (spring steel throughout)
> > > will (but, then again, maybe not fracture).
