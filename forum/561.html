Paul,
A thick, round rim is typical for early Akasaka guards. Later ones tend to be thinner. The early Akasaka really favored a very "high," rounded rim compared with other groups working at the same time.

Sometimes tekkotsu are seen in early examples. Senkotsu is more common, which is a sort of long linear bone that forms where the inner and outer plates are joined.

David,
I can't think of seeing it in Owari work or reading of Owari guards described that way. Owari guards can show elongated or "linear" tekkotsu when compared to Kanayama's "bumpy" tekkotsu for example, but it looks quite different from senkotsu. Easier to show than to describe.

In Akasaka guards that have been rusted and cleaned, the join between the plates can be very prominent. Most early examples will show it in some places around the rim, but not others. Some do not show it. Some early ones have tekkotsu, but I don't think I've ever seen a piece with both features together. I have to wonder whether the ones with tekkotsu are the predecessors.

I can't think of any examples of pre-Edo period work with senkotsu, so to that extent it would aid in dating.

There are guards that show a many-layered lamination in the rim. It looks something like a puff pastry crust. I don't think that that feature is described as senkotsu, but it's also seen in Edo period work.

Anyone have different observations?

Jim
