here's probably a really stupid question

by retalt

but I have been know to ask plenty of these in my life so why stand on
ceremony now :-).

I was looking at some pitting that is on a new tsuba I picked
( either Akasaka or Tosa Myochin I think -
http://www.nihontomessageboard.com/personal/tsuba/akasaka2.jpg )
and it has a bit more pitting than I thought from the auction images
which is a shame.

My question for the day then is, if we polish and restore pittings,
scratches, chips and the likes from swords, is anything like this ever
done on iron tsuba ?, I know the obvious thing is the patina but if we
can re patinate nakago, why not tsuba ( at least iron tsuba anyway ) and
are there any artisans in Japan presently making tsuba in the old ways
and using original patina recipes ?. I know Yoshindo Yoshihara sensei
does apparently, and I would love to see some examples of his work. I
was thinking about this recently, and wondered about the recipes in the
back of the Aesthetic Guide and if anyone still does this stuff. If so,
could very good tsuba with minimal damage be repaired and re patinate
and if so, how effectively.

Right then, foot back in my mouth and over to the experts.

cheers

Rich Turner

Re: [nihontostudy] here's probably a really stupid question

by sworddad

Rich -

Repatination of tsuba can be done--correctly--but refinishing such as removing pits, etc., no, I don't think anyone would want to do that. Sometimes a terrific tsuba is found that has been buffed or whatever and then, repatination can be done to save it. Skip Holbrook used to do this but today--if I understand things correctly--only handles really high quality tsuba. We all experiement with repatination I'm sure, but care must be taken not to (further) damage an important quality tsuba.

Several years ago a Japanese gentleman (artist) was at one of the sword shows displaying his prize-winning newly made tsuba. His tsuba were traditionally made and quite beatufiul...and quite expensive! So there are those around today who continue the craft.

Ron
STL

I suspect most tsuba collectors do a little " restorations ". Getting rid
of active rust with a little horn/ivory rub job for example, wearing it in
your pocket is another.
I restored ( more than rust removal) a tsuba that passed Hagihara sensei's
scrutiny and passed shinsa always brings a little grin to my face.

there are a few methods out there . I wouldn't recommend trying them on
good tsuba at the get go, but you can try your hands at the typical po-edo
39.99 rusty thingie and learn about iron...................
