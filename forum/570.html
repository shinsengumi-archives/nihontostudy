a polarizing subject

by rkgatteleport

Hey guys,

I've been experimentin' more with taking pictures of tsuba, and have a
new trick for ya'all out
there to play with in your copius spare time (yeah, right :-) )

the test subject is a kagamishi tsuba. the first pic is my "generic"
tsuba setup (a couple of reveal bulbs
in gooseneck lamps, with the tsuba on AR glass over a graycard. Note
that I probably could
have got some more detail by skimming the lights more, underexposing,
etc., but you get the idea...
Note that I have a circular polarizer on the lens, set to the point of
maximum light throughput

http://home.teleport.com/~rkg/misc/kagamishi_front_non_polarized_small.JPG

In the next picture, I cleverly twisted the polarizer to where the
picture looks the darkest

http://home.teleport.com/~rkg/photos/tsuba/kagamishi_front_polarized_small.JPG

You'll note the improved detail.

the next picture is of a nagamaki tsuba - I found that a bunch of
detail now
more or less "leaps" out of the picture with a polarizer, notably the
line of brass "buttons" that seem to be
rising out of the lacquer at the top of the piece

http://home.teleport.com/~rkg/photos/tsuba/nagamaki_front_small.JPG

Here's an earlier picture of the piece - my best shot at picturing it,
with steep skim lighting, underexposure by
2/3 of an f stop, yada yada..

http://home.teleport.com/~rkg/misc/nagamaki_front_unpol_small.JPG

I think you'd actually get much improved results with a polarized light
source (the "blooms" at the edges are mostly
direct reflection, which is mostly non polarized light) - I've ordered
some sheets of polarizing material to test this...

Enjoy,

rkg
