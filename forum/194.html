Remounted swords for WW2

by swordnsig

Hi everyone!
I once had a rather nice later Muromachi Bizen katana that had the
nakago and the mei ground down to bright metal..in fact, the end of
the nakago was ground almost to a point and a threaded post was
welded on so this blade could be mounted in one of those Meiji/Taisho
period D guard mounts. Obviously the 'armorer' who mounted the sword
knew nothing about swords, nor cared! I believe that, like in the US
during the war, people donated swords to the government (in the US it
was fighting knives) for issue to new officers. I don't believe that
every 'ancestral' blade in a Gunto or Kaigunto mount was mounted by
the family of an officer with a 'family' blade. When swords were
needed, many were donated to the cause; be it the invasion of China
and Manchuria or WW2. That one sword told me that the armories were
merely making sharp shiny things for the military and that is as far
as their knowledge went. This is speculation, of course, but it
makes sense to me especially with some of the swords I have seen.

Dear Friends,
At the risk of making my first contribution to this list argumentative, I
must respectfully disagree with Swordnsig.
Over the years I have owned a couple of blades that were modified in Meiji
times to accommodate gunto koshirae of the era. They have always stuck me
as very well-crafted and legitimate - if unfortunate - adjustments to
changes in sword technology. Whether equipped with a thread bolts of simply
bent to fit a kyu-gunto tsuka, the work of making these adjustments is
always very carefully done so that handle is snug and graceful. Notice that
the work is always done to allow a habaki to be installed and removed as
necessary. Furthermore, the reworking on nakago is a well established part
of the nipponto traditions. Think of all the osuriage swords we see and
nakago with the remnants of wonderful old hori! Finally, I doubt that this
work was done in "armories." I assume instead that it was done by smiths
eager for the work and to please their patrons. In any case, it seems
entirely wrong to consider the swords that won the Wars again the Chinese
and the Russians as "merely ..sharp shiny things." They were well designed,
excellent weapons. Not necessarily to traditional tastes, but cutters none
the less!
Peter Bleed
