Kantei setsumei

by yamaimojg

The scan of the front of the guard does not do it justice, but is sufficient to see what's there. The photos of the mimi give some sense of the tekkotsu that place the guard in the Owari region. Given the shape of the rim and the design, the two most likely choices are Kanayama and Owari sukashi. I think either answer is acceptable given that you are working from pictures.

The thickness and somewhat squared rim lean toward Kanayama, but can also be seen in Owari work. The guard is on the thick end of the spectrum for either type. The diameter is possible for either group, although on the large side for Kanayama. The style of the tekkotsu is the most important point. Kanayama tekkotsu tend to form pronounced, sometimes rough bumps. Owari tekkotsu are low, smooth and emerge from the plate with more subtlety, forming "bean" shaped and "rope" forms. This guard shows both of these latter types. Also, Kanayama guards will often show some tekkotsu on the face, but Owari guards do not.

Wild goose designs are common in Owari work. It's not impossible to imagine a design like this in a Kanayama guard, but it is more naturalistic, complicated and even a little delicate for that group. The majority of the evidence points toward Owari sukashi.

The guard is in very good condition, so it may look younger than it is. The quality of the iron and the tekkotsu are unlikely to date to the early Edo period, and are consistent with pieces from mid to late Muromachi. The birds at the top and bottom of the sukashi design seem too free spirited to be from that time, though and seem more like Momoyama. Given that design is easier to manipulate than iron, I lean toward late Muromachi, but Momoyama is possible.

The guard has a kanteisho to Owari from the NBTHK. No date given.

Yagyu is in the correct region, but the texture of the metal will be cloudy/sandy and will tend to show many linear laminations in the rim. It is also not a Yagyu design.

I included this tsuba in the Akasaka display at Tampa to show the likely Owari origins of the Akasaka group. While some Ko-Akasaka guards will show tekkotsu, they are not of this type and the color and texture of the iron is different. The rim would show a more rounded shape in Akasaka. The cutting of the sukashi would have sharper edges. The design does have a bit of Akasaka feeling, but there would probably be more asymmetry and abstraction in an Akasaka piece.

That's all I can think of at the moment. Any other observations or questions?

Jim

I am taking the liberty of posting a reply from Jim I copied awhile
back on the board about tekotsu:

'Checking the Yagyu article in the European NBTHK "Selected Fine
Swords..." book
I found these interesting comments':

"Masters like shodai and nidai Yamakichibei, Nobuie and Hoan of Owari
had
studied the workmanship of old Owari and Kanayama tsuba, whose iron
has the
0.6-0.8% of carbon which produces tekkotsu. Nevertheless certain
schools
demanded higher standards, among them Yagyu; the Hayashi and Shimizu
groups in
Higo; and Nobuie, Hoan and Yamakichibei of Owari; while the Katchushi
group such
as Myochin used traditional forging methods.

"Forging to produce the jitetsu and that for tekkotsu should be
carried out
separately. Each tsuba-ko decides the proportion of tekkotsu metal to
use, for
example 1-5% of that for of jitetsu. This is cut into one centimeter
pieces,
4-5 mm thick, which are mixed with the jitetsu during forging. If the
forging
is not good enough, it can open up later when heated in the process of
tekusarakashi, which results in cracks or so called forging mistakes.
Interestingly enough most of the highly valued futoji-mei Nobuie
tsuba show
those cracks.

The bibliography cites an article "A Brief Summary of Tekkotsu" in
Token Bijutsu
#522, July 2000, 9-11 by a tsubako named Kazunari Naruki. I'm
guessing that
this is the source for the above comments.

Jim

Barry,
I would have posted photos sooner if I had known. In general, yes, tekkotsu are a good thing, at least for Owari region work. I can't think of a case where it would indicate poor quality. It's part of what separates the better Owari, Kanayama, etc., work from the routine products.

The mid to late Muromachi period is prime time for tekkotsu and they continue to a lesser degree in Momoyama. Early Edo period Yagyu, Ono and sometimes Akasaka will also show them, although not in quite the same way as Muromachi period Owari sukashi or Kanayama. In general they are not seen in Edo period work. Norisuke and other late Edo copies of Yamakichibei, Kanayama, Nobuie, etc., will show tekkotsu, but they generally look forced and artificial, often oversized and overdone. Of course sometimes maybe they got it right too, and we're still being fooled by those pieces.

Occasionally Edo period Shoami work will be carved to simulate tekkotsu and other features of earlier-period smiting.

Rich,
I tend to agree with Alan. Looking at the front of the guard it does appear to be Edo period and indeterminate style. I suspect that what you're seeing in the rim may be the structure of the forge welding brought out by the rust. It's not unusual to see pronounced layering in, for example, Akasaka guards that have been badly rusted. Seeing a sharp ridge like that would be unusual for tekkotsu. Also tekkotsu are usually smoother, darker and more lustrous than the surrounding iron (although not always so with Kanayama).

I do however have a Kanayama tsuba that I think is quite old that has a somewhat similar look in the rim. I've never been able to decide how much of the appearance is due to how it was made vs. how it might have rusted. Next time I set up to take macro photos I'll get some images of the rim for comparison. The face of that guard also looks quite old and in Kanayama style, though.

> If there is very bad rust pitting, it's possible that you will be

Hi, trying to get caught up, and probably violating the five minute
rule. Be that as it may, let me interject two self-learned Chicken
truths about rust:

1. It's always worse than it looks. If there is ANY surface
discoloration (red, black, gray, even white), then there is pitting.
More a problem, of course, on blades as opposed to tsuba. But any
rusting of any kind means more than just finger stones on blades to
correct the damage. I have not the experience on kodogu to say
something even semi-intelligent, but suspect that if any discoloration
is removed, the surface will not be as smooth as when first made,
unless considerable nearby unrusted metal is removed as well.

2. When there is scabby red/brown rust on blades, the pitting is kinda
bad to extremely bad, and it's difficult to impossible to tell simply
by looking which it is. This Chicken truism comes from polishing
("Forgive me, God, for I have sinned") two Pacific Era gunto with
equivalent scabby surface rust. Paradoxically, on the the traditional
tamahagane (no stamps) gunto, the rust pits went to China, in depth
they were as long or longer than their width. I couldn't get out all
the pitting without grinding away over three hundred years worth of
metal, and had ot give up with about five percent of the pits
remaining. On the non-traditional, Seki-stamped gunto (Kumemune
mentioned a while ago), though the surface rust was essentially
identical, the pits were no where near as deep, and, as it turned out,
not deeper than the waves in the crap wartime polish, and so were not
the major concern in the end.

Bottom line, rust is either bad, or extremely bad, and it's tough to
tell between the two before getting serious. FWIW, JMO.
