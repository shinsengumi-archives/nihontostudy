Re: [nihontostudy] Ko-Kat scratches - yasurimei on kozuka and kogai ura

by yamaimojg

Pages 179 and 180 of Kajihara's glossary have terms for a variety of these decorative finishes. Nekkogaki yasurimei, "irregular scratches as if done by a cat," is one of them. I don't think that it's meant to refer to the uniform file finish that's seen on Goto pieces and others, although it does get misused that way in the West.

There are definitely Edo period machibori kozuka with falling rain pattern yasurimei. It's not so densely done and does look like ukiyo-e shigure (drizzle). I suppose that the sujikai yasuri on Goto pieces could depict a downpour... but probably not.

Given the aesthetics of early Goto pieces, I don't think that it was intended as a representational element. Think of how austere their presentations are. If it did represent rain and this was somehow important to accompany the shishi, karasu, dragon, etc., they why didn't it also appear on the shakudo versions of these motifs?
Jim



I never did understand the "cat scratches" idea whereas "falling rain" seems right in keeping with things Japanese. Suspect some 19th century European came up with blaming the cat.

by Barry Thomas

Jim and all (a memorable phrase)

> Nekkogaki yasurimei, "irregular scratches as if done
> by a cat," is one of them. I don't think that it's meant
> to refer to the uniform file finish that's seen on Goto
> pieces and others, although it does get misused that
> way in the West.
>...
> If it did represent rain and this was somehow important
> to accompany the shishi, karasu, dragon, etc., they why
> didn't it also appear on the shakudo versions of these motifs?

Note that the scratches on habaki nearly always end in a "burr" which is
hammered flat and forms part of the decoration. Only rarely is the chisel cut
clean. One can imagine a cat scratching a tree (or you!!) and leaving a curled
bit of bark (or flesh!) at the end of the scratch. Hence nekogaki. In the case
of the Goto gold kozuka finish, or those kozuka finished without the "burr" at
the end of the scratch, then "rain" is most likely a correct descriptor. Why
scratches on gold and not shakudo?? I can only think that shakudo is "shibui" -
subdued, whereas gold is "hade" - flashy - and needs to be subdued, the
scratches doing this to a "T".

Barry Thomas.

Re: [nihontostudy] Ko-Kat scratches - yasurimei on kozuka and kogai ura

by Peter A Bleed

Note that the scratches on habaki nearly always end in a "burr" which is
hammered flat and forms part of the decoration.

Barry Thomas.

Forgive a technical note. I happen to have seen Hagawa-san making "cat
scratches" (sorry, I can't recall what he called them). I have seen him do
both gold and silver. I think in both cases, the marks are made with a
special tool. It has a series of about 6 sharp teeth on a blade that is
curved upward. The tool is pushed on to the surface to be finished in a
rocking motion. This is why the marked are denser on one side. Obviously,
seeing this done is pure ballet, but it goes extremely fast, like 30
seconds to a habaki. The "burr" that Barry mentions is done by burnishing
the surface after it has been scratched. In the case of gold, the
burnishing tool is itself gold with a carefully maintained rough finish. It
had to be cleaned (degreased?) before it was used, but it only took a
couple of stokes to finish the surface. After that the tools went back to
its neat little saya!
Peter Bleed

Re: [nihontostudy] Ko-Kat scratches - yasurimei on kozuka and kogai ura

by Barry Thomas

Barry Thomas wrote:
>> Note that the scratches on habaki nearly always
>> end in a "burr" which is hammered flat and forms
>> part of the decoration.

and Peter Bleed replied:
> Forgive a technical note. I happen to have seen
> Hagawa-san making "cat scratches"...
> ...the marks are made with a special tool...

I have never thought much about the techniques of making habaki (having simply
admired great work), so this is an interesting observation of Peter's. This
would make a habaki where the whole surface is covered with short excusions
ending in a "burr" - like some cruder WW2 gunto habaki I have seen, but the
"burrs" are plainly put in individually rather than with a multiple-toothed
tool. However, I'm sure there are many different techniques for making the
nekogaki/amegaki. I had in mind those where the "burr" clearly occurs at the
end of a longer excursion or cut made (I guess) with a burin-like instrument,
hence the "burrs" are rather sparse in number compared to the technique Pete
described. Interestingly, the solid silver habaki on Showa period Gassan swords
has a pattern of "burrs" in a repeated square arrangement. I could put up an
image if any one wants to see it.

Barry Thomas.
