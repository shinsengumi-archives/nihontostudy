Satsuma Rebellion mountings

by Peter A Bleed

Dear Friends,
Please let me ask a question about a style of koshirae, even if I
suspects that it falls well below the standards of most those on this
list.
I am suddenly, and I hopefully briefly, interested in so-called
"Satsuma rebellion" fittings. I recall seeing these over the years, but
never had a term for them until Fuller and Gregory published a couple of
pages about them in their first volume on "Imperial swords." In that book,
they say - without reference - that the samurai army raised against the
newly formed Imperial Army of the Meiji government by Saigo Takamori was
equipped with swords that have a distinctive flat wrap and very ordinary
kodogu. Notably, they often have simple washer style menuki.
There is no question that these fittings are a "type" that does occur
in the real world. By that I mean that it is a fairly consistent style that
we do see it from time to time. It looks expedient and very
"utility-grade." But is anyone really aware of evidence that they really
were associated with the Satsuma Rebellion? I'm wondering if they might
simply be very low-end koshirae produced in terminal Edo/Meiji times for
any market there was for low-end swords. If any one has insights on this, I
would appreciate them. Thnak you
I might as well explain why I ask. I recently acquired some (about
140 in all ) 1910 vintage photos of North Pacific ethnographic objects that
were being sold by a dealer. A couple of these pictures show Japanese
swords and at least one of them certainly looks like a "Rebellion sword." I
know the Rebellion failed, but I have a hard time believing that Saigo left
Kyushu for the Aleutians!
Peter

Re: [nihontostudy] Satsuma Rebellion mountings

by yamaimojg

Peter,
Given the uniformity of that style of koshirae I'd suspect that they were assembled in a fairly narrow space of time and place rather than being a general style of late, low quality mount. They give the impression of being made for imminent use rather than routine daily wear. The Satsuma rebellion label seems plausible, but I don't know of any hard evidence supporting F&G's attribution. For what it's worth, the book published last year on Satsuma koshirae does not include any illustrations of this type of mount.

On the subject of your photos, I've seen a lot of references to George I. Quimby's 1985 paper "Japanese Wrecks, Iron Tools, and Prehistoric Indians of the Northwest Coast" published in Arctic Anthropology 22(2):7-15. I haven't read it, but it might be of interest.

Here is a link to an interesting on-line article from the Royal British Columbian Museum web site that disputes Quimby but does cite intentional and unintentional travel by pre-20th C Japanese to the western coast of the Americas:

http://rbcm1.rbcm.gov.bc.ca/hhistory/japaneseshipwrecks/toc.html

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Q on Kunihiro tsuba

by yamaimojg

Rich,
I don't think that there is any connection with the Ito school. Bushu is of course the other name for Musashi province, where Edo/Tokyo is located. There were many fitting makers living in Bushu.

Given the variation in the Kunihiro signatures and the quality of the work there may have been more than one person making those tsuba. Haynes proposes 4 or 5 makers working over the course of 100 years in his Index of Japanese Sword Fittings and Associated Artists.
