Shakudo Short Sword Hilts?

by matthewhoyle2000

Gang, although not entirely Japanese Sword Related, I still thought it
might be interesting to bring this up. It seems that tsuba makers also
made shakudo hilts for Rapiers and Short Swords for the Dutch in the
early 18th cent. I have included an excerpt below from the book I am
reading at the moment:

[On the topic of Short Swords]

'The Dutch also traded with Japan and as a result a variety of wares
made by Japanese craftsmen were carried back to the West. These included
hilts makde of shakudo, a mixture of copper and gold, and patinated to a
rich blue colour to contrast with the gilded panels of relief
decorations set at intervals on the guard, knucklebow and pommel. As
with the ivory hilts from Ceylon, contemporary Dutch hilts were the
prototypes for these exotic pieces. The craftsmen who made them were
specialists in tsuba, Japanese sword guards, and came from the area
around Nagasaki. The majority of these exotic hilts echo standard
smallsword or hunting sword hilts of the 1700's but hilt forms of the
1750's were also copied.'

The author then goes on to illustrate such a Japanese shakudo hilt. It
actually kind of looks a tiny bit like Mino work. Very remarkable. Now
has anyone ever seen any of these items first hand? Is this phenomenon
documented? Do we know which tsuba makers got involved in this new
business?

And since the Dutch were ordering them back in the 18th cent, I might
want to keep my eyes peeled other here, to see if any turn up at auction
or a local antiquefair (I am in The Netherlands). I would be so lucky
hahahahaha..

Regards, Matthew

by yamaimojg

Matt,
Try to find a copy of the book "Sawasa, Japanese export art in black and
gold, 1650-1800." It was published by the Rijksmuseum in 1998. ISBN 90 400
9279 6.

I have seen some of these pieces in person and the work is quite different
from Mino, although I see what you're thinking. I'm not aware of anything
documenting who the specific makers were. They weren't necessarily nihonto
fittings makers. All sorts of decorative items were made in this style,
boxes, bowls, buttons, buckles, tea services, pipes, etc. You should be
able to see some examples at the Rijksmuseum in Amsterdam. The Metropolitan
Museum here in NYC has a few examples on display.

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Shakudo Short Sword Hilts?

by Peter A Bleed

Dear Matt and Ron,
I am very tardy in picking up this thread, but I am glad that someone
has surfaced this topic once again. It comes up every so often, but there
does not seem to be enough material - either documentary or material - to
put it to rest.
The book that Ron mentioned is called simply "Sawasa" ISBN 90 400
9279 6. It was published by the Rijkmuseum in Amsterdam. In addition to a
bunch of other stuff, it shows about a half dozen Japanese mounted western
swords. These are either smallswords or hangers and they are NEAT! I would
love to have one. Indeed, I have kept my eye open for one of these for
several years, but have not be able to snag one. I attended a sale at
Christies in London some years back and watched a beauty sell so I know
that they are out there ... and that i can't afford them. Most studies of
European small swords include mention of these swords, but I have never
seen them treated in Japanese literature, As you point out, Matt, they are
clearly made with Japanese style, iconography, and techniques. I agree that
they seem "Mino" but that scould be simply because of the fine shakudo
work.
I urge you to keep you eye open for these in the Dutch market. Often they
are called either "Tonkinese" or "Chinese" but I think there can be no
question that they were made in Japan. Obviously, if you find any of these
that you do NOT wish to own, I hope you will contact me first!
I love finding evidence that Japanese swords havelong been part of
the larger world and this seems to be one of those times. These swords show
that the world of the17th and 18th century knew that the Japanese were
making some beautiful swords.
Peter
