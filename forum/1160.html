Re: Metal-on-metal...

by alchemyst

Louis -

Generally there is little metal to metal contact with Japanese
swords unlike European fighting styles. When there is, it is
normally on the back of the blade as a block to a cut. If
there is chipping on the edge, it requires repolishing
and possibly reshaping of the blade. If the chip is too deep,
that is thru the hamon, the blade is generally considered
ruined. Cut marks on the back (mune) or side (shinogi) are
usually left alone and some collectors consider them marks
of honor.

So, if I am understanding this properly, due to the way in which is the

Louis et al-
Ideally there is no sword to sword contact but of course it happened.
The softer back and core were mainly to absorb the shock of a cut (TO
something else) and not to spare the attackers blade. Ideally cuts are
anticipated and avoided, "if there is time to block there is time to
cut" or so goes the teaching. Blocks are often hinted at in kata but
not emphasized. When a sword blocks another you get chips in the edge,
cuts along the back, bends, wrinkles,you can actually "shave" chunks
out of the back, cut a sword in half and the heat of impact can
actually fuse the two together, then you are both dead...
