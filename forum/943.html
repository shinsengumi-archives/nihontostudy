Re: [nihontostudy] tsuba id help

by yamaimojg

Rich,
I'll have to rush a bit here. The design looks more like something derived from Kyoto and 3 mm is pretty thin for Owari. Without seeing the metal in person it's hard to say with a guard like that. I've seen a few tsuba with classic Kyo sukashi designs that had purely Owari iron and no doubt were Owari, but I'm sure I would have said Kyo if I had only seen a face-on photo.

The guard that Curran posted is attributed to Owari, but mid Edo period. Most of the Owari characteristics of the iron are usually lost by then, but it looks like that one still has a bone or two. It's 5 mm thick and appears to have the squarish rim that you'd expect from Owari work. The design is also a bit more blocky. BTW, Owari-esq guards that have rounded rims are usually attributed to Shoami.

Some later Kyo and Owari guards can be difficult to separate. I can't think of an obvious alternative school for your guard. Enjoy cleaning it and studying it further.

Rich,
The seal script kanji on the upper right of the front side is KAN, cold or midwinter. Also read samu(i), cold or chilly. The seal script doesn't particularly resemble the modern character, but it's an exact match in the Daijiten. I don't know what the next character is and it's hard to even see the rest in the photo.

I have seen a fair number of Heianjo-zogan guards with hitsu-ana shaped like that and irregularly surfaced plates. I can't say that I recall that inlay motif, but it is something that they'd be capable of doing. Maybe early Edo period. How thick is the plate?

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: tsuba id help

by yamaimojg

Rich,
It depends on the guard. It could be Shoami, or it could for example be Kaga-zogan as in the guard I posted yesterday. How much earlier Mitsutada might have been working before Myoju isn't clear, but even with him we only have examples with nijimei. We don't know for sure whether or not he was associated with the Umetada. He could have been a Kyo-Shoami worker. I don't think that there are any fittings known to have been made by Myoju's father Shigetaka.

Sasano said:
"Umetada Myoju established the style and the fame of the Umetada name, which flourished greatly after his time. But he was not the first to make sword guards in the Umetada style. Now unknown craftsmen and a certain Mitsutada are known to have done this kind of work before Myoju. It seems likely that -- as the case with Shoami guards -- Umetada guards began being produced in the Muromachi period. Those that predate Myoju are based on Shoami ideas and are called Ko-Umetada, or old Umetada."

Even Sasano is making a rather narrow distinction between Ko-Umetada and Shoami.

Maybe evidence supporting the existence of Umetada workers predating Myoju will come to light.

On the other subject, I still can't see the three characters at the bottom of the guard very well. The character at the 3:00 position might be shu. I don't have much confidence that I could read the characters even if I got a better look at them.
