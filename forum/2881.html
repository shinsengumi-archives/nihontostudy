Han Bing Siong book now in stock

by musashibobenkei

I am pleased to announce that "Japanese Swords in Dutch Collections," the
2003 publication of the late Mr. Han Bing Siong by the Netherlands Token
Society, is now in stock.

Though I have over the years read and admired Mr. Han's articles in the
JSS/US Newsletter, English Token Bijitsu, and elsewhere, spending some time
with this book has overwhelmed me with the extent of his scholarship and the
magnitude of our loss with his recent passing.

As the former owner of a katana signed (gimei, of course!) Hankei, I read
with interest the first entry in the book, a wakizashi by Hankei. I felt
like I learned more from that two pages than I had in all the reading I had
done back when I owned that blade and dug up every article, oshigata, and
reference to Hankei that I could find.

This is the kind of book that makes me proud to be a bookseller.


SIONG, HAN BING. Japanese Swords in Dutch Collections A Selection from 500
Descriptions in the Series Japanse Zwaarden in Nederland Bezit. Rijswijk,
Netherlands: The Netherlands Token Society, 2003. First edition. 244 p.
Translated from the Dutch by J. M. Bonsel and M. Laros. This selection of
85 blades of the 500 cataloged by Han Bing Song since 1979 was published to
celebrate the 25th anniversary of the Netherlands Token Society. The
cataloging project included swords in museums as well as private
collections. Of those presented in this book, a range of swords from Koto
through Gendaito are shown with oshigata and Mr. Han's scholarly and
insightful comments. Seventeen of the blades are also presented in
photographs, and there are 14 pages of full color photographs of the best of
the koshirae. An outstanding publication of the highest quality and most
valuable content. Pictorial Hardcover. As New. $100.00

Re: [nihontostudy] Akao tsuba smith's and their work.

by yamaimojg

Rich,
I don't have a lot of personal experience with Akao work, but my understanding is that the later generations did some work in iron, although not as much. Also, while the first two generations started working in copper alloy after leaving Echizen, I don't think that they stopped making iron tsuba. FWIW, I have an iron sukashi guard signed Kofu ju Akao Yoshitsugu. This would probably be the third generation or later. Your kanji for the nidai signature did not come out for me. Sorting out generations from the signatures without personal names is not straightforward.

Jim
