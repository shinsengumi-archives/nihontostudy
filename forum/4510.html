Ryan et al -
Many an uneducated swordsman has, and many still do. The key is of
course recognition. Do many people recognize your style as having
practical, spiritual and cultural value, enough that your style will
continue long after your passing. Do otherwise uniterested parties
recognize what you are doing as Japanese Swordsmanship or at least
swordsmanship. Finally does the pioneer himself recognize the
contributions of past masters, and that it takes really big cahones to
pronounce to the world they have broken with tradition and started his
own way and how that might rub folks the wrong way no matter how good
or how logical his new style may be.

you might go to the Iaido/l list at the University of Guelph, or
swordforum.com for some other opinions.

You didn't ask but, my advice would be to align yourself with an
establish tradition, then work hard and if you're lucky enough, open
your own dojo. You get to teach what you like and you're students will
be part of something bigger than the backyard Ryuha. and for goodness
sakes if you aren't Japanese don't give it a Japanese name...
MHO
