Paul,
Thanks for the photo. By round, I was picturing a rounded maru mimi, not a round cross section. A round cross section is not typical for early Akasaka guards. Later when the Akasaka were copying Higo work, they used their thinner style of round section rim. I don't think I've seen this kind of rim on an Akasaka piece, but I can't say it's impossible. They did some unusual things.

I'm not sure about your piece. The looseness of the sukashi design reminds me of early Akasaka, although the bird at the top is pretty thick for them. The iron surface shows more "modulation" that is typical for Akasaka. It looks more like something from the Owari region. The seppa dai is not far from Akasaka shape. The guard reminds me a little of a third generation piece that I have:

http://home.earthlink.net/~jggilbert/akasaka.htm (second guard down from the top).

Looking inside the sukashi openings do you see any signs of three layer construction?

Tough one... it's bugging me already. Some Akasaka style, but a lot that is not. And I don't know what the "is not" part is. Thinking out loud...

Jim

Jim Gilbert <jggilbert@...> wrote:
Paul,
A thick, round rim is typical for early Akasaka guards. Later ones tend to be thinner. The early Akasaka really favored a very "high," rounded rim compared with other groups working at the same time.

Sometimes tekkotsu are seen in early examples. Senkotsu is more common, which is a sort of long linear bone that forms where the inner and outer plates are joined.

David,
I think that what we're seeing is the layering in the iron that has been delineated by etching or rusting. In the "pastry crust" effect I mentioned the whole edge looks like that with every thin layer clearly visible (I'd guess that those were intentionally etched to bring out a uniform effect).

Senkotsu can be wavy like that since it's also the result of forge welding, but it's higher carbon material actually coming out of the surface a little like a long, thin iron bone. It's not as pronounced as tekkotsu, but the effect is similar.

Maybe I'm not seeing your photos correctly, but it looks like something has gotten in to etch into the welds here, revealing the layers of metal.
Jim
