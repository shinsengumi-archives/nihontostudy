Ron, T, Peter, peanut gallery,

I'd love if it was Hirata Hikozo, but I think the
truth is closer to Peter's guess. What bugs me though
is I studied Andy's Nakane a good bit in San Fran, as
I thought several times about buying it.
The iron quality on this one is better than the
Nakane, and the gold/gilver workmanship is better, but
the tsuba does not feel pre-1800s to me. This one came
from an antiques shop, and previously was in someone's
collection, as it had an old collection tag on it
identifying it as "dating from the 1600s" and other
tsuba terminology. Obviously I think the tag is wrong
on the age.
Nakane can't be the only one working like this
around that time, can he? It is not a Kamiyoshi guy-
or at least not to my knowledge- as their iron usually
feels softer, redder, and more refined in the several
I have handled and own.

So I'm still not sure what to make of it. I'll
probably sell it before the 2006 New York shinsa, but
don't like selling something before I've learned what
it has to teach.

Curran
