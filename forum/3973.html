color of Momoyama Kanayama tsuba

by steven_88_2000

In reading Sasano, I see that he frequently speaks of the color of a
given tsuba as being indicative of its age/period, as well as of the
qualitative aspects of its iron. However, he tends not to elaborate
on this, and since the images are virtually entirely in black and
white in the two well-known English language texts on sukashi tsuba,
it is difficult to actually see what he means when he makes such
comments. A few times he does note that Momoyama Kanayama tsuba tend
to have a reddish cast to them (as well as being smaller) when
compared to Kanayama guards of earlier times. I was just wondering if
this is the consensus among those of you who have much experience with
Kanayama tsuba. If so, is it enough to conclude that such
characteristics are so consistent as to be understood as definitive of
the period?

Steven

Re: [nihontostudy] color of Momoyama Kanayama tsuba

by yamaimojg

Steven,
Color is probably the most difficult kantei datum. The color of iron tsuba has often been changed (passively or actively), it depends on the light used to view or photograph and depends very much on the perception of the observer. I would suggest concentrating on everything else in dating Kanayama tsuba before considering color.

With that caution, I think that early Kanayama do not tend to show a reddish tone. Not all Momoyama ones are reddish in my experience, but some are. So, the observation is valid, but it is not definitive in my opinion.

If you do try to evaluate iron color use direct but not dazzling sunlight.

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] color of Momoyama Kanayama tsuba

by Barry Thomas

Blokes,

Many years ago a man once offered me a number of iron tsuba in trade for a tanto
I had that he desired. One was iron of very nice work with gold inlay in
snowflake patterns, but the colour of the iron was odd. It just didn't "look
right". It was sort of greyish. I commented on this and the fellow freely
admitted he had cleaned and restored the tsuba with gun blue... GONG... I kept
the tanto. I just want to highlight Jim's advice that colour is probably the
most difficult kantei datum. This also applies to shakudo. I won't buy
anything in soft metal unless the colour is right, as there were so many grades
of quality - 15, if I recall correctly, for shakudo alone...

So for me it is a combination of the art level of a piece (quality of work) and
colour and condition of the metal. I offer for your inspection a pair of menuki
I have of Nio. Now, I know these are almost identical to repro menuki sold by
Fred Lohman, and I got a real shock to see these in his catalogue. However,
mine are real as I have an NBTHK old green paper attributing them to Mito kuni.
I sent them to the first USA NBTHK Shinsa way back when. Here is the link:

http://ph.groups.yahoo.com/group/nihontostudy/photos/browse/301b?c

I've put a couple of other menuki there as well. A pair of Yanagawa tigers
(papered) and a pair of Goto shishi (not papered - my guess as to school)

The NIO menuki I found in a sword that was otherwise a crapper in every respect.
The wakizashi blade had a humungously bad dragon carved to hide a flaw (it
didn't!!). The tsuba was a rank, and I mean RANK, very ordinaire piece of scrap
metal (but old, mates, old...). The fuchigashira were crudely made plain brass,
and the saya was a leather over wood WW2 'creation'. But I could easily see
these menuki were SUPERB. I could never understand why and how they came to be
there amongst the dross...

The Yanagawa tiger menuki were likewise in an otherwise very ordinary wakizashi
in gunto "civilian" koshirae.

I bought a katana just to get the Goto shishi menuki. The blade was signed
NOBUKUNI and dated to 1400-something. BUT - the hamon dropped off the cutting
edge and the nakago was savagely rusted. This was a severely tired old blade.
The tsuba was a Shonai iron piece with Shoki the demon queller. All the
patinated metal on the tsuba had been polished back to bright copper. Now, some
30 years later, it is looking quite decent. The shishi menuki, however, were
mint apart from some "lashings" of a solvent glue used to hold the tsukaito
together.

Best regards,

Barry Thomas
(Melbourne, Australia)
