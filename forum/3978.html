Hi All,
If some of the comments made could be fused and edited the
tekkotsu in tsuba may be explained as to why they appear or not.
When tsuba were in their infancy on the new curved single edged
sword they were made by the smiths who made the swords. It makes no
sense that separate steels would be used, so the tamahagane and old
steels being reused were the base metals used in the tsuba
manufacture. this would cause the same characteristics found in the
blades to appear in the tsuba. Inclusions, layers, and
nonhomogeneity in the steel itself. In the art sense this would be
the primitivist stage where form came before function. The times
were constant warfare and all parts must be robust.
Now come the times of peace and for the most part form supplants
function. Soft iron is a good plate for carving and inlay and soft
metals become more and more common. No more tekkotsu for the most
part except for the few schools that continue the tradition of
worked steel.
Shinshinto times bring the revival of the old ways. What
appeared on old tsuba was now a favourable thing to copy including
using steel again and emphasising attributes such as tekkotsu.
Appreciation of functional aspects were now artistically
appreciated. What was functional had now become raised to the
highest form. This happened in the Chanoyu, Kodo and was almost
decadent. I'll leave that argument for another day. John
