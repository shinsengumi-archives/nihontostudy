Kanesane swords do have a fairly good following for a showato in part thanks
to the Fuller and Gregory book and the fact that most were water quenched. A
broken tip unfortunately will require a foundation polish making it
difficult to get away with anything short of a costly polish an good polisher.

regards, Franco>>

Franco:

The amount of money I have invested in this blade is a whole $40.00 US. The
amount of material loss at the Kissaki having looked at it with my glasses
on is less than 3/64" to my tired old eyes.

I am thinking of sending it out, having it polished and remounted, now the
question is to whom do I send it to for the polish. Fed Lohman has a gentleman
who does polishing but I do not know of anyone who has used him and I do not
want this blade ruined as I already saved it for a horrible fate as a yard
work tool...

There is a lot of activity (due to the water/brine quenching) along the
Hamon that I can still see a bit though the very light surface oxidation.. I
could be wrong but I feel that it could benefit from restoration, even as a
simple "what the heck, let's do it" type of project.

I just don't want to be trying to make a silk purse out of a sow's ear if
you know what I mean... I will try to get some photos taken if I can get decent
ones out of my ancient camera.

Now it is my understanding from the research that I have done that Kanesane
did make quite a few blades personally, as well as supervising his production
company, how do I tell for sure if this is a production blade or one he
made himslef. I am leaning towards the former due to the Seki stamp on the
nakago.

by giuliettaspider_1959

Typically Kanezane's personally made swords will have a showa stamp and feature a kakihan (stamped personal seal) following the mei on the nakago. The outline of his kakihan resembles the shape of a turtle with the Zane character in the center. The fact that there is a seki stamp leaves little doubt that this is a production sword.
regards, Franco
