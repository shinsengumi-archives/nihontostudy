San Francisco Token Taikai

by soshuju

Gentle-readers and poor souls,
I say poor souls because I know how pitiable one feels when
he cannot get to the show. This was my first show in like five years
and I enjoyed every minute. From Thursday night check in to last
minute haggle on Sunday.
From this list I met Curran, Darcy and Rick for the first
time, and I became reacquainted with others like Ron Hartman etc...I
knew and remembered most everyone there, so spent a great many
pleasant hours just getting caught up. In the past I was just the new
kid or one more face in the crowd. Now that they had all seen my face
"in the paper" as it were I met many more people who expressed
interest in my articles. Frankly it was very nice to know that
someone had been reading them, and very kind of everyone to pay the
compliments.
I try to be scientific in my approach to the showroom but
depending on who I run into it doesn't always work. On Thursday I
covered the whole room as best I could returning to my table (really
a friends table), with two t-shirts, 9 DVDs and a mental list of
tables and people I would need to revisit.
As Dr. Gordon Robson DRE, and Okada Moriyoshi of the NTHK
were in attendance, I naturally did my best to make the distinguished
guests from Japan feel at home. Along with Jim Gilbert we had a fine
seafood dinner and shared talk of swords, kodogu, the London Shinsa
and the possibility of a SFO Shinsa in the future.
Friday opened early with more of the same, I put some books
out which sold quickly and thus had some coin to spend on the items
on my want list. Replacing books with books is always a danger with
me, I spotted a title I had been after and made note of price and
location. There were some supplies I needed and priced those at
several tables, while just perusing other things.
There wasn't much armour to be seen but quite a few good
swords. Especially right across the front of the room. Cary Condell,
Michael Krause, Ralph Bell and Fred Weisberg, Al Bardi, Robert Hughes
and Mark Robinson all had fine blades in polish and ready to move.
Michael Krause and Robert Hughes had good blades in nice koshirae,
Ralph and Fred mostly blades in shirasaya. Cary had a monster blade
at sanshaku in koshirae and a selection of fine kodogu. As promised
he showed me his Sa Yukihide Tanto, which rather than elicit "wows!"
gave me to utter a contented "MMM Good..." It looked and felt solid
in every respect.
Friday I was busy helping set up and monitor the lecture and
kansho-kai presented by the NTHK and Dr. Robson. The topic was
Bizen-den from Muromachi to Shishinto, with Sue-koto Bizen blades
most in evidence. The arc from Late nambokucho to bakumatsu was
illustrated clearly by the shapes and workmanship in each. As
mentioned the Kozori tachi was a rare treasure in any company, I
liked the Kiyomitsu ken as well. I was impressed that proper sword
etiquette was strictly followed by all, though for the life of me
cannot imagine what it was that kept people in line.
Afterwards back in the showroom the tables had almost filled
up, excepting those dealers from the East Coast who got caught in the
Blackout. While there were plenty of polished/papered blades
available there seemed to be a distinct lack of piles of junk. There
really weren't any tables stacked with rusty, chipped and broken
diamonds in the rough, waiting to be spotted by a discerning eye.
This might be explained by the abscence of Mr. Wahlberg, but it seems
that most everyone had just a few choice items to sell. Perhaps the
object was to travel light.
Friday ended like each night ended with groups of like minded
enthusiasts huddled around a warm Sam Adams, discussing the virtues
of a Kunimichi katana or the foible of seeing all Shinto works with
mitsumune as Hankei blades.
Saturday skipped breakfast and an initial foray into the
showroom to get in line at the Masamune Jutetsu exhibition. Avoiding
the long line opposite the Masamune, I jumped into the middle of the
table and found myself addressing a Naotsuna katana and then a Sa
Hiroyasu, then Okanemitsu, then Naoe Shizu, then Yamato Shizu
Kaneuji. At this point in Japan we would likely be done but there
were thirteen or more blades, all textbook examples of the artist.
Better education for the eye cannot be found, I'm thinking. That
Masamune was so popular that it was the one blade I didn't get to
see, by all accounts it or the first Norishige was the best blade on
the table. For me the Norishige was the best blade I saw all weekend.
Back up on the sales floor, more books and supplies. Made the
rounds with some iai friends looking for acquisitions. There was no
auction but we did have a special presentation at dinner. As part of
the preparation for the show we polled local politicians for letters
of support. We received letters from many club members, the Consulate
General of Japan, the Mayor of Burlingame and the Mayor of San
Francisco. In an only in San Francisco twist, it seems the office of
Mayor Willie Brown misunderstood just exactly what we were after.
Instead of a letter of support we received something better! A
proclamation from the Mayor making Saturday August 16th DR.GORDON
ROBSON DAY!! Our very own holiday celebrated with a pair of
suspenders, a pint of beer and a good cigar.
Sunday, with tables closing down here and there the last
minute wheeling and dealing began. I got a deal on some good books I
had been eyeing all weekend, and bought a tanto just because the
koshirae caught my fancy. Hataya Sensei was there along with Ando
San. While Hataya didn't seem to leave with but a small armful, Ando
san had to buy an extra guncase at the end to carry his purchases.
BTW I met the Ebay Monk he paid his daughter to wear kimono
and man his table. She generated a good deal of interest but it seems
he sold nothing. He may be reading this list because he brought
several daisho sets, and asked me "aren't daisho popular with
Americans?"
As always there were lots of things I wish I had the coin to
purchase and too many people I wish I had more time to sit down and
chat with. Overall the rumblings were that prices were high and sales
were slow but it was a successful show. If you are reading this and
not remembering it, then start making your plans for next year, these
shows are only getting better year after year.
-t
