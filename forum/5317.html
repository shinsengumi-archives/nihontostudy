Re: [nihontostudy] Nikuoki

by yamaimojg

Elliot,

Did I really say that? Nikuoki is the subtle variation in the treatment of the surface of a tsuba, particularly through hammer work. Nikuoki is a quality found in a good Owari or Kanayama tsuba, for example. It's definitely not present in something like Soten work. While I am not at all a fan of Soten tsuba, it wouldn't have been entirely fair of me to criticize it for inferior nikuoki since that's not at all what that type of work is about, but rather picturesque 3-D carving and inlay.

Unfortunately I'd say that Asahi lacks nikuoki as well.

Jim

Hi Barry, Hamaguriba is a particular type of sword construction that
has a 'thicker' or 'meatier' blade that has a more wedging action to
a cut, as I understand it. Niku, esp. in the hiraji, can refer to
presence or lack of this in blade construction but is commonly used
to describe the effects of polishing whereas hamaguriba does not. At
least this is how I've used the term conversationally, maybe
incorrectly, with others and the intent is generally understood. John

Hi John,

I was a bit slow in answering Barry, but my answer is based on the entry in the
Nihontô Yogo Jiten or Dictionary of Sword Terms. It gives hamaguri-ba as an
equivalent to ample nikuoki. The reference is to a cherry stone clam or hamaguri,
and means that the hiraji swells like a clam shell. One of my favorite things is to
take Japanese colleagues to Legal Seafood in Boston and turn them on to raw
cherry stones. Japanese don't eat raw clams, so it is always interesting to see their
faces light up when they try them for the first time.

Gordon

Hi John,

As I said, I do not use hamaguri-ba, so I looked it up in the Japanese Sword Terms
Dictionary by Mr. Kajihara, and, as he states, "When there is ample hira-niku,
because this shape looks like a clam shell, this term is used." In other words, if
there is ample niku between the shinogi and the cutting edge then hamaguri-ba is
used and used to mean the same thing as ample hira-niku or ample niku-oki. This
is not my definition because I never used this term nor heard it used in Japan.

Gordon

RE: [nihontostudy] Re: Japanese Sword Terms Dictionary

by robsongord

Mutsu is from alternative numbering system for six. Hitotsu, futatsu, mitsu
or 1, 2 and 3. These numbers are used for general counting and when you
don't know the usual counting word. For example, one block of tofu is tofu
itcho. The Dictionary of Sword Terms I mentioned has all the fittings terms
as well.



Gordon
