I have been aware for a few years that the deliberate rubbing of gold to look
worn is a well-established part of the metal workers' repetoire. I believe it
is meant to impart an "antique" look, or maybe it's that "wabi-sabi" serpent
rearing its ugly head again. Either way, if an item looks to be of good quality
and in good condition AND the gold looks worn, then it is more likely to be a
deliberate effect and original and NOT a "condition problem". I would sure like
to know more about this.

Hi Barry,
It's an interesting study to be sure although I'm not aware of
too much available literature to 'study'. The 'Bushido' magazines
from Robert Benson (1980's) had a lot of great information, and the
1976 Tai Kai from San Francisco book has a lot of information also (I
believe there's a copy at Bridge of Dreams). They show up on Ebay
from time to time also.
What I was referring to with the kozuka was the effect of
removing of gold revealing the substrate for effent, which you can
see better there than on the menuki where there is definatley wear
involved also. Ezo menuki were fabricated on a positive mould from
thin copper sheet, then the gold was applied using a mercury
technique (probably heat evaporation), then burnished with a needle
to get the effect. Uttori by definition is attachment of the inly
around the edges only, although I would imagine there is some form of
burnishing or other form of adhesion involved as well. As far as the
wear to the menuki, hell, I hope I look that good when I'm that old!

Pete

PS: I have a feeling they are older than Edo due to the posts both
being open -- possibly Jim would know?

Re: Post dated Post Update (LOL)

by klepet33319

Hi Alan and thanks for the information on Mrs. Schneidman. I will
have to look into finding the catalog.
I just came upon this information today and it coincides with your
question. It is from 'Kodogu' pg. 20, by Gunther Heckmann

'Until a few years ago it was generally agreed that sword decorations
of this sort were produced by the Ainu, the native inhabitants of
Japan's northern island Ezo, which is nowadays called Hokkaido. For
this reason these sort of objects are usually called Ezo koshirae.
Because of the fact that the Ainu did not have a deep knowledge of
metal processing, we should rather use the term Shoki Kinko, 'the
first kinko period', for this type of work. Other ideas about the
origin of these objects can be oriented from the beginnings of the
fire gilding technique in China, during the T'ang dynesty (618-906),
and the possible use of imported Chinese bronze for the production of
sword decoration.
The basic metal used for these objects is different from the metal
usually used for sword decoration. An examination of the metal
content of this pair of menuki was performed in April 1996, by Prof.
Dr. Richter of the Institut fur technologie der Malerei, in
Stuttgart, using LASER micro spectral analysis and atomic absorption
spectral anallysis. The main componenets of the metal in this
object are 70% copper, 25% silver, and the remaining 5% shows traces
of lead, gold, mercury iron, calcium magnesium, aluninum, and
silicon. The gold and mercury derive from the fire gilding. The
other contents can be taced to the partially refined copper. An
alloy of 25% silver and 75% copper is often used for Japanese sword
decoration and is called oborogin. With a higher percentage of
silver to copper the alloy is called rogin or more commonly
shibuichi. For the outome of the alloy, it is important to know that
when silver is added to copper in these quantities, it tends to rise
to the surface, which is the reason for the silvery appearance of the
Shoki Kinko pieces. It is not clear yet why especially this alloy
was used for sword decoration at such an early time. These objects
are mostly deated from the early to middle Muromachi period (1400 -
1450), sometimes even earlier. Shibuichi was always considered to be
a metal of the Edo period, but the date of origin of oborogin is
unknown. Further analyses of the so coalled Shoki Kinko decarations
are necessary before any conclusion can be made about the components
of these sword fittings. With this first analyses an important and
stimulating step was made towards further studies of this subject.

Buttweiler in his writing stated he felt the dark color under the
gilding may have been due to the effect of mercury on the copper in
combination with the gold creating a layer of 'shakudo' and this
effect may have led t it's discovery as an alloy. (This gilding was
post probably done by making a slurry paste of gold filings and
mercury and then heat evaporating the mercury leaving the gold layer).

Hi Pete,
I have just looked at your kozuka, this is not suri-hegashi gilding but uttori-zogan that has worn through showing the typical 'holes' in the inlay (or more properly 'overlay'), the last fruit is only just showing the wear, some leaves are worn also, uttori zogan would not have a hole in the top. The kozuka looks like 16th century Mino Goto, The early Goto did not use suri-hegashi gilding.
Alan

Hi Kenneth,
The tsuba is a Nagoya-mono from Owari, as the metal is brown and not true shakudo it is from the later period, one feature commonly found on Nagoya work are the 4 'chips', 2 each out of the sides of the nakago-ana at the top and bottom .

The tsuba might be from a yari blade mounted as a tanto or wakizashi.

Hi All, I am desperately seeking help to recover a sword that went
missing on Monday 06 June on a flight between London and New York (JFK).
The blade in question was appraised in London and given probable
attribution as an Oei-Bizen tachi in the form of the late Heian period,
possibly to one of the "Oei no San Mitsu"

The blade measures 66 cm with 2.7 cm koshi sori and ko-kissaki. The
hamon is a very narrow suguha which continues through the boshi and
finishes in a small kaeri. The blade is suriage with a mumei nakago
showing the sori of the original length and with three mekugiana. The
blade is not in full polish but good condition with no rust or fatal
flaws. It does have two very slight bends through the monouchi area.
