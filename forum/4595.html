Re: [nihontostudy] my photo album, pictures 19 and 20

by neobliviscar

Elliott,

The good:
There are some nice tsuba in your in your folder. I
like the Sukeyoshi and the one labeled Saotome.

The bad:
Photos #19 & #20 are of a shiiremono (mass produced)
tsuba. It might even be one of the better modern
chinese repros. For study's sake, look up Mito
Shiiremono.

The ugly:
There is no sharpness or crispness to the design. The
nanako (little beads) are crudely spaced. The figures
are poorly cut, and gold washed.


Curran

Elliott,

No matter how hard we may try, I think all of us make
an ass of ourselves at one time or another. I
certainly have. By sticking my neck out and giving an
opinion here, I've probably just ticked off a dealer
who now is going to send evil thoughts my way.

There are a lot of dealers out there who are very
honest and take small profit for their pains. Do them
credit.
Lordy knows it pains me to go through the process
of selling anything. Absolutely hate to even try! This
past year I picked up 2 lots of exceptionally good
tsuba and am only going to keep about 40% of them. I'm
in the process of papering them (slow process)and
deciding which to keep. I planned to let an auction
house sell them, but learned the auction houses get
their pound of flesh! Unless I give them over $100,000
of items... they take a greater % than any dealer I
know.
As the tsuba come back from Japan, I'll just put
them out at Tampa sword show and possibly Chicago--
but ultimately a few of them will probably end up with
a dealer.

So go easy on the dealers. Many are good people who
provide a service to buyers and sellers. There are 2
or 3 I call friend.

Curran

Ps. The 'Saotome' have a shakudo edge?

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: my photo album, pictures 19 and 20

by gaigin2000

Hi Elliott and Curran;

Elliott, I know how you feel and can sympathize with your frustration. Unfortunately it is a part of the learning curve associated with this hobby.

As far as the dealers go, many of the people who have tables at shows are not dealers. They are collectors who are upgrading their collections, just like you, in San Fran by selling pieces that have nothing left to teach them and buying new ones that they hope to learn from. The majority of these people have the same limited knowledge selling their items as you did buying it.

I doubt if they had any intent to mislead you, they just did not know any better. Unfortunately this is true in most instances. The seller has very little idea as to what he or she is selling. This in my experience this applies equally to blades that are gime, and fittings that are less than what they may be demonstrated as.

The dealers who make their living buying and selling swords and fittings know exactly what they are selling, and if they should make a mistake, they will look after you.

Its all part of the learning curve.

John
