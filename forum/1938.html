Jingo Tsuba

by matthewhoyle2000

Can anyone explain why Jingo tsuba are so sought after? They always strike me as being a bit crude and not very interesting, often decorated with an eagle or other beast that looks, well, kind of retarded. Is there any specific reason why these pieces have the status they do? After spending hours looking at pictures of them, I still fail to see where the (artistic) merit is.

I admit to not having handled any genuine examples personally (since they are pretty rare), so perhaps these tsuba convey something in the handling that they do not in the pix?

Re: [nihontostudy] Jingo Tsuba

by yamaimojg

Matt,
The qualities of the best Jingo pieces do not come through well in photographs. The first generation's iron plate is just outstanding, even if the decoration is not to your taste. I was really surprised when I first handled one in Japan because it was something I never "got" from the illustrations I had looked at.

Studying Jingo tsuba outside of Japan is tough since the overwhelming majority of pieces we have are later work that really isn't very good and does not give any sense of the quality of the first work.

The style of the brass suemon zogan decoration does take some getting used to. It's probably not for everyone, but definitely try to see a top example in person if you can. What do you think of Hirata Hikozo's work in copper, for example? Or Raku, Shino and Oribe pottery?

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Jingo Tsuba

by arnold frenzel

Hi:
I will take a stab at your very interesting question, one of many that
you seem to come up with. You have provided part of the answer yourself
by saying "rare" though of course that begs the question, why rare?
Jingo tsuba were made in Higo and the work of the first three
generations is considered particularly fine, thereafter as in most lines
following generations could not stop adding gold and silver effects,
becoming too symmetric, adopting features from other groups, etc.
Higo, being remote from Edo, tended to supply products in the kodogu
area, as with swords, that were somewhat outside the mainstream and they
tended to be somewhat aloof from fashion elsewhere and to maintain that
difference longer than other groups. Their market was more the samurai
and less the merchant than would be true for people supplying the market
in Edo and Osaka. The first three developed naturalistic designs never
seen before or since, and thereby reflected the wabi sabi aesthetic that
was very influential, particularly with the more severe samurai in the
early Edo period. Their designs are very three dimensional, tastefully
accented with gold and silver. Their most complementary counterpart
seems to be found in the work of the Kanayama which, while sukashi, have
the same rustic, subtle and unpretentious character. People also wonder
whey they tend to go for some serious money. Jingo tsuba are instantly
recognizable and fit well with any severe koshirae.
Just a stab.
Arnold
