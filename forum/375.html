kongo-bei

by bowman1955

Hi All,
this is a first message to the group so I am hoping I havent been beaten by technology.
having seen Carys beautifully mounted Kongo-bei katana I was wondering what the current view/ opinion of this school was. I understand that they were not particularly highly thought of 20+ years ago as they were regarded as ultra conservative and not conforming directly with one of the 5 traditions. More recently 1 was listed in Comptons 100 masterpieces and I have seen several others (well at least 1) with Juyo certification. I bought a O-suriage katana with hozon papers a couple of years ago and have to say I really like it, not least for the fact it is extremely healthy ( particularly for a blade that is between 5 and 700 years old) and a powerful blade. Originally it must have been 31+ inches long. Has the school become more widely appreciated in recent years or is it just us ultra conservative suguha fanatics that love them.
would value opinions
regards
paul

by robsongord

Hi Paul,

Nobody seems interested in answering this, so I might as well put my foot
into it. The school, as you note, is conservative, which is probably due to
their Yamato origins, another very conservative place. I read the school
name as Kongobyoe, which is the pronunciation typically heard in Japan,
although there seems to be an alternative to just about every possible
reading for a name. The blades are seen as "waki-mono" or outside the main
schools. There has long been a tendency to look down on "waki-mono" with
some exceptions. Part of the problem is that with this school and others
such as the Kanabo school, the Bungo Takada school, the Kai Mihara school,
they produced a lot of kazu-uchi, which were obviously popular because they
have been so well used -- or used up. There are many who think that this is
all these schools produced, which like all generalizations, doesn't hold
true for a small, but important body of very good work. Thus, you should
look at the individual blade for the quality it possesses and not the
"truths" distributed by opinion makers. The nice thing about Kongobyoe and
the other schools I have mentioned, is that due to these kinds of
prejudices, their prices remain affordable even in Japan. Hope this fogs the
issue up sufficiently.

Gordon
