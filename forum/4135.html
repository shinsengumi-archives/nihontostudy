by retalt

I do not think it's always about how life like the portrayal of the bird
is Milt, but about how well the work is executed. In the case of the
Jingo tsuba, the light wash of gold over the iron I think is the
underlying beauty. The bird probably has little to do with it in
essence. I mean by that, it could be anything other than a bird. It is
the iron first, then the shape and the mimi, then the layout of the
seppa dai and hitsu ana and the design. Then step back and look at the
thing as a whole. It is why kinko mono is held in low esteem. Take away
average or poor work on the top, and there is nothing underneath.

I remember a young collector Richard being educated by this bloke Milt
who spent days talking to him about Wabi-Sabi , it took him a while but
Richard finally sort of got it. The lesson was the aesthetic, the
underlying beauty, not what one thinks one sees on the surface. Sound
familiar ?.

Rich

by soshuju

Milt -
Along with the comments Richard made (I think it was Richard) about
the quality of the iron and the overall presentation, what I think
makes Jingo special is the character of the work. It is rustic, it is
rough, yes but all the materials are worked and presented just so.
Nothing more needs to be added, little or nothing could be removed
without destroying the presentation.
Was the first Jingo master a rustic who simply did things his way
naturally with no thought as to the outcome? or was he as Hagihara
has suggested a genius, who was repelled by the trends in kinko work
around him, and inspired by the tea culture of his time to create
works shockingly different from any other artist of his time?
I can't answer the question, but I agree that if you compare the
best of Jingo to other kinko works, it is shockingly different, it is
solid, quiet but powerful and very befitting a samurai. A pleasant
shock indeed, and I think genius as well...
-t

Hi Milt;

You realize I would be the last person who would ever disagree with you, BUT as I have a soft spot for good Ko-Kinko tsuba I must tell you that you are blowing smoke, or perhaps having one of those wacky ones, when you make such a comment.

Good Ko-Kinko has excellent nanako, very fine and even, painstakingly produced. If it is uneven, and looks like Nagoya mono then chances are that is what it is.

Good Ko-Kinko shakudo has a beautiful rich dark black purplish color not often found in later kinko works, good weight, and normally a floral or vines style of decoration, simple but elegant and very fine nanako, at least the ones I have.

Just my .02 cents worth of course.

Aizu Shoami from Torigoye, 'Tsuba, An Aesthetic Study', abridged:

"By the Genroku era, and after, we have ample evidence of this school
in the mass production of inferior quality tsuba. This work is
little better than shiiremono. The style of this period was a
combination of Shoami and later Umetada school styles. ... The poor
quality of Aizu Shoami tsuba is partially due to the fact that for
two hundred and twenty six years the Kato family governed this
province without giving their patronage to the Shoami artists who
lived there. They made no effort to raise the level of the work
produced, or take an interest in its production. The workers of the
province were forced, as independent artists, to fend for themselves,
seeking work anywhere it might be available. ... At the end of the
Edo age there were two wholesale dealers of 'saikumono' (metal
handiwork) living at Wakamatsu in Aizu. They employed many workers
to fill the orders for the mass productions of the Aizu Shoami
school. ... In Meiji/Taisho they produced all types of copies and
imitations for the Tokyo and foreign market. In addition to the Aizu
style they made Heianjo brass inlay and Heianjo openwork, as well as
Nobuiye, Kaneiye, shingen and famous late kinko artist forgeries and
imitations. In fact, they made any and all styles that had a ready
market with the foreigners of Europe and America. This was the final
fate of the Aizu Shoami school".

Personally I wouldn't draw a comparison with Bungo as they were a

Barry,

I just remembered that we never finished discussing that great Ko Kinko tsuba with the Kasugane motif.

I'm particularly fond of the deer. I've added to the photo on the web-page an illustration from a book on Paleolithic art, describing the same combination of perspectives we see in the deer on the tsuba. This very ancient approach to perspective is not naturalistic in the way we are normally accustomed to seeing, but I think, in the case of this tsuba, it very successfully communicates the kind of imperfect image of deer we receive, looking from a distance in less than optimum light at moving animals.

I think the imperfection of the perspective makes this tsuba more exciting than a it would be if it featured a much more literally modeled image. And the archaic quality, the comparative roughness of the representation of the deer, on the elegant kinko background is more dramatic by the virtue of the extreme contrast between image and background.


http://zincavage.org/KoKinkoKasugane.htm

creature on it that I always thought was an "over-endowed rabbit", but Bob
Haynes told me was a fox!! I wrote to Bob when I saw a similar tsuba in one of
his catalogues. I was amazed when he said this was a very early style of tsuba
ca 1300 - 1400. I had it for years, often looking at it and saying "Do I like
it, or do I not??" Same thing with this deer kinko. I have gazed at Pete
Klein's image on yahoo (I actually have the Lundren Collection book, but it's
out on loan to a 'kinko' mate) for a long time on this sleepless night, and I
ask myself the same question all over again. So David Z, if I could ask you a
question, when you say "I don't collect kinko at all, but this is one of a
small number of cases which, granted a opportunity, would lead me to alter my
policy" I have to ask you "WHY??" Please explain.

If I found this very tsuba I would think it was junk. I don't have to tell you
why. Or do I?? The design spilling into the seppadai I have only seen on tsuba
I didn't care for. The wobbly surface and incredibly bad nanako would kill it
for me. And the stiff deer doesn't do anything for me at all. However, I would
look at the larger than usual kozuka ana and opine "Hmmm, maybe it's early".
Back to my question to myself "Do I like it, or do I not". If I don't like it,
I don't collect it, no matter what the aesthetes say.

Now, this puts me into the rustic class, I know, but there is a point to this
that is not all derisory or derogatory. It is this. It is easy to pick
excellent work amongst Edo period tsuba , even in the iron "wabi-sabi" class,
but the archaic styles, the transition between (if we might say) conceptual
forerunners and the refined later schools is much, much more difficult. Or is
it?? Recall here Milt's hawk tsuba that lead to the present discussion. And I
can show you a tsuba that a well-educated (tsuba knowledge) mate of mine
dismissed as "late, possibly 1920s". It is a masterwork of late Edo/early Meiji
and belongs to a friend of mine. If he gives permission I'll put up some images
of it.

So, to bring this to a close, are there any others inhabiting the same closet as
I??? I know I'm lacking an ability, an aesthetic if you like, to appreciate the
stiff deer kinko in question and it worries me. It seems without thorough study
and training many tsuba in this time frame would indeed be considered junk and
sold off, possibly to an ignominious fate. The other kinko that Pete shows in
two images at the same link does "speak to me". It is from the same time frame
and I find it elegant and refined and would collect it. I think this discussion
parallels the Sue Koto kazuuchimono line of thinking with blades - I don't
collect those either...
