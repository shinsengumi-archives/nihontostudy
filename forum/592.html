Re: Gimei removal and Nakago Repatination

by davidzincavage

Personally, I think this is an area where American collectors are too ready
to follow the Japanese point of view.

If a collector of antique American furniture found a 150 year signature
falsely attributing a piece of furniture to the wrong maker, he would
certainly take the misattribution into account in valuing the piece, but in
no circumstances would he remove that old signature. In his eyes, the false
old signature had been part of the piece long enough to become part of its
history, not perhaps the best part, but a part of some interest providing a
piece of historical information which one would not simply discard or
destroy.

In Nihonto, we even have the case of gimei Muramasa blades, where signatures
were altered or changed when the legend of blades by that maker and his
school and their ill-luck for members of the Tokugawa family caused
possession of Muramasa swords to be banned. It seems a real crime against
history simply to eradicate a feature so historically evocative as this
particular sort of gimei, and I would dispense with papers myself rather
than remove such a false signature.

David

Re: Gimei removal and Nakago Repatination

by davidzincavage

Your rejoinder I think provides a good illustration of the manner in which
the Japanese perspective differs from the Occidental on matters of this
kind. In pretty much any area of antique collecting in the West where an
appraiser was writing up a piece featuring a several hundred year old false
signature, the Western appraiser would simply describe the piece and note
his rejection of the signature as invalid and proceed to make his own
attribution. His description would include note of the false signature, and
his assumption would be that a clear written description, especially one
accompanied by a picture, would be entirely adequate to remove the
possibility of anyone being misled. The false signature's perfidy, once
recognized in the light of contemporary scholarship would be considered
historic, and consequently, merely interesting and amusing, rather than a
continuing active offense.

If a modern American organization were evaluating Japanese swords, and the
case of an ancient gimei came along, what we would expect is papers to be
issued simply noting that the signature is false and making a different
attribution.

One significant difference between antique furniture and Japanese swords
does come to mind: it is a good deal easier distinquish American furniture
makers and schools than to appraise Nihonto. Yet, inevitably, mistakes
occur, and sometimes opinions and evaluations change. The Japanese approach
of gimei removal and nakago repatination is based on the assumption that
this shinsa committee's judgement this day is 100% correct and could never
ever be reversed. Now some gimei are doubtless unquestionably what they
are. I own a Mino-den tanto signed Masamune, which has no possibility
whatsoever of being a Masamune. But I'm sure not all cases are as clear.
In most areas of conservation and collecting, irreversible changes to a
piece would always be avoided.

> > > restored, yet looking as if the whole package had been
> > > sitting in someone's home as a family treasure,
> > > mounted just as you see it, for the last three or four
> > > hundred years.
> > > That's what I would call true "restoration". It's
> > > not just "mounting up" a blade (for practice or
> > > display) but is the process of creating an artistic
> > > whole which is demonstrably (the visual impact is
> > > basically "wow", particularly if you know how this
> > > piece started out) more than the sum of its parts.
> > > The last fifty years of sword collecting (if not
> > > much of the last five hundred years) seem to be
> > > characterized by a kind of dealers' reverse mentality
> > > (I had a blade at one time which had been messed with
> > > during the Edo period to look like a Heian piece, so I
> > > know from experience that this kind of messing with
> > > blades has been going on for long and long) where the
> > > parts are worth more broken up than they are as an
> > > artistic whole. While that might be economically true
> > > (and as often as not it's even a bad economic
> > > decision) it's an artistic travesty. Another thing I
> > > did this month was manage to re-unite a Dai-Sho tsuba
> > > set which had been separated at least a hundred years
> > > ago. One piece wound up in Canada and from there made
> > > its way to the U.S. The other piece was in an
> > > European collection. Neither owner even knew that
> > > their tsuba was part of a matched pair. I can only
> > > speculate about what the swords and the other fittings
> > > might have been like (pretty good, I imagine if they
> > > still exist).
> > > It is so easy in collecting to unknowingly and
> > > unintentionally destroy, it is rare and difficult to
> > > rejuvenate, recover and restore. And as Chris has
> > > been telling us for years, restoration means a whole
> > > lot more than sending that rusty "junker" to an
> > > amateur polisher and then cobbling together enough
> > > bits and pieces of tosogu to create a mounted piece.
> > > Admittedly, it might not matter to some people
> > > wether they are creating or destroying and they are
> > > more concerned about what they have (and how cheaply
> > > they got it) than they are about their piece having
> > > any kind of artistic integrity. I had a very
> > > frustrating experience of this sort a few years ago.
> > > I had acquired (and still have) a very good Mito
> > > school sentoku tsuba (at least Ogawa Morihiro sensei
> > > said it was a very good Mito piece) and while I was
> > > visiting David Hamilton in New Mexico, he was wrapping
> > > a tsuka for a local gun dealer, which happened to have
> > > the identical (we can talk microscopic level here
> > > folks, I mean identity as in exact congruence not
> > > relative similarity) fuchi kashira (pretty obviously
> > > all disassembled from the same sword). We tried
> > > everything we could think of to get those pieces back
> > > together - offered him the tsuba way below market -
> > > offered to by the fuchi-kashira way above market and
> > > pay for a new handle for him - all in vain. He just
> > > wanted his piece and that was it. Where it came from
> > > or where it was going was none of our business. Once
> > > humpty dumpty falls apart, reassembly is often
> > > difficult if not impossible. As Stephen King says,
> > > "you can't unring the bell".
> > > So if you have a blade you are thinking about
> > > mounting or re-mounting, I would urge you to do it
> > > right the first time. You have two guys easily to
> > > hand - Cary in San Francisco and Chris in Japan, both
> > > web accessible (http://www.ikkyudo.com and
> > > http://www.ejapaneseswords.com) who specialize in
> > > precisely what I've been describing - restoration
> > > which is an act of art and an act of creation.
> > > Between the two of them their specializations are
> > > different enough that I regard them as complementary
> > > rather than competitive. Of course, the older guys on
> > > the list know this already. If you know Brian
> > > Tsernaga (sp?) or John Tirado and can get into their
> > > schedule, they are active participants in the
> > > authentic restoration process. But for the rest of us,
> > > it's a lot easier to go to one guy who has built a
> > > career specializing in authentic blades and
> > > restoration than to chase down all the individual
> > > pieces. Different strokes. When I was collecting
> > > gunto, Fred Lohman's service was perfect for me, and
> > > if you're mounting an oil-tempered gunto he's still
> > > the guy to go to. If you want a traditional blade
> > > made with traditional materials and methods then go to
> > > Chris, who has modern blades covered, beginning with
> > > the opening of the Tatara in 1933 right through the

> > > specializes in older blades, Juyo Token and blades of
> > > Juyo quality which for relatively small reasons
> > > (sometimes nonsensically small from my point of view)
> > > didn't get a Juyo paper and so trade in the market at
> > > a quarter of the price of their indistinguishable
> > > counterparts.
> > > As Mike Blue, Bill Latham, Mutsyhr Yee and Keith
> > > Larman can tell you, there are other approaches you
> > > can take (and they really speak for what I would
> > > characterize as "schools of thought" beyond their own
> > > swordmaking endeavors). I happen to like old blades,
> > > so having a tired old 14th century blade brought back
> > > to life in stunning period koshirae does it for me.
> > > But I've seen what top notch shinsakuto look like and
> > > if you're not hung up on the historical dimension as I

by samurai@xhasc.com

<jdz@...> writes:

> If a modern American organization were evaluating Japanese swords, and
> the case of an ancient gimei came along, what we would expect is
> papers to be issued simply noting that the signature is false and
> making a different attribution.

This is one of the things that I too found lacking in the shinsa
process.

One open area for research is "how many smiths signed Kanesada" and even
that there may have been more than one signing "Nosada."

In this environment, how reliable is a decision of gimei to be in the
situation where a signature looks good but the workmanship doesn't match
"*the* Nidai Kanesada?" Certainly not 100% in all cases.

One can see the unreliability of the shinsa process in the stories of
multiple submittals to Hozon for a sword in attempts to get the "right"
paper for it and getting multiple results. That is not a criticism, just
an indication that it is a process linked to subjectivity and
differences of opinion.

As such, are we to make the irreversible and destructive action of
removing a signature in order to obtain whatever opinion has been
withheld because current scholarship would indicate the signature is
bad?

Certainly that too will be on a case by case basis, and while some are a
clear cut "hee hee this 'masamune' mei is bad, remove it" others may not
be so clear.

It would be nice if an avenue existed where one could obtain the best
official opinion without possibly destroying something that might be,
after all, correct and deemed so by future generations of scholars.

I have a sword that falls into these categories. I would never remove
the mei from it, and I am 90% sure that it will fail shinsa, and I am
60% sure that it is gimei.

I don't want to go and smash up something that existed for three hundred
years though, should it be correct in the end. I am not even sure how
good I feel about removing it if it is only grafitti as it is part of
the history of the sword.
