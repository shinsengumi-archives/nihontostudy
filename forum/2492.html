Re: [nihontostudy] Ko Akasaka

by yamaimojg

Rich,
The term Ko-Akasaka is usually applied to the work of the first three generations, who left no known signed tsuba. (BTW, there are only a few known guards signed by Tadamune by the 4th gen.)

The shodai seems to show more Owari influence than Kyo, but that is open to argument and I think that both influences are present.

The tsuba in the photo looks later than the nidai to me, but the design has some early elements.
Jim

p.s.
Sometimes I hear Ko-Akasaka used to describe tsuba with Akasaka-style motifs that otherwise appear to predate the Edo period, i.e. "proto-Akasaka." I don't think I've seen this usage in any Japanese sources though.

Re: [nihontostudy] Paulownia ?????

by yamaimojg

Rick,
The flower stalk at the left is pretty clearly drawn as coming from the same stem as the two aoi leaves (that are not part of a mon). My first thought was the same as yours, but as far as I've been able to find, the aoi that is depicted in the famous mon is Asarum caulescens, which is as you say a wild ginger and pretty similar to the European and North American varieties. None of them have flowers like that, let alone the vine-like habit suggested by the arabesques. Looking into the popular names for these plants is confusing -- I may be missing something essential, but I suspect that the lacquer artist was working backward from the mon and just mixed in parts from other plants to go with the aoi leaves.

Jim

My Japanese plant books show tachi-aoi as a species of trillium. The leaves are quite different from ones in an aoi mon and again the flowers don't match the ones on the bowl.
