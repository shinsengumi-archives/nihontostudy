Re: [nihontostudy] Jersey City sword show...

by robsongord

Had a good time. Enjoyed seeing lots of familiar faces and some good
swords. People are still submitting stuff they shouldn't, however. I
do hope the level of study continues to improve. A lot of the guys
who have been with this for some time now did very well with some
only getting one or two rejections. Nonetheless, the younger and less
experienced guys need some guidance. I think the clubs are best for
that. Still, when the front desk tells you not to submit your
numbered and stamped gunto and you do anyway, I guess there is not
much that can be done. We are trying to have a traveling shinsa every
year with Chicago up next. I hope we can continue to see fewer
rejections. Oh, the show also seems to have been well attended on
Saturday. I gave a short talk similar to the one in San Francisco,
and it was a pretty good turn out for a one off event.

Gordon

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Jersey City sword show...

by Mark Conneran

For what it's worth, as a potential "new-bee", I thought I'd be presumptuous
enough to make a few observations regarding the Jersey City show. I came up
to the show with a friend, hoping to take in a lecture or two. Obviously a
lot of work went into it. A few thoughts, for what it's worth, as
constructive criticism. We went to the taikai and skipped the shinsa
thinking that the latter would only be for those submitting swords to be
evaluated. The taikai was worthwhile for the budo demonstrations alone. The
swords in the sword display, however, were not labeled and we were, of
course, curious to know what it was we were viewing.

We are both members of JSS/US but haven't attended any of the NY club
meetings and really have little sword knowledge. As such I'm enormously
impressed by the knowledge of the members of this list. But from the
comments I've seen here earlier discussing the need to attract "new blood",
I think activities such as the "introduction to sword handling" lecture
might perhaps be expanded and publicized. Again, for what it's worth.

Mark Conneran

polishing. This blade is suriage and the Mei that is present

Dont agree Rich, If a novice (or indeed anyone else) submits something they have no clue about I think it would be useful at least to be told what the tsuba/ sword etc was trying to be. using a catch-all Bungo-Takada, echizen seki, or shiomi attribution does little to advance their knowledge. by yamaimojg

Milt,
With the hawk count shut down due to rainy weather I'm catching up on things post-shinsa.

The fittings shinsa team this time around took quite a lot of time scratching our heads to come up with informative attributions on the mumei stuff. There were quite a few attributions to schools and groups. The problem simply is that for many pieces there is nothing distinctive enough about the workmanship or quality to go beyond just Kyo, Edo or Mito Kinko. Throwing out something more specific isn't going to help anyone learn if there are multiple other groups turning out work of the same style.

Honestly I found my self thinking several times "why would someone submit this item to shinsa?" You want to give the customer his money's worth, but there's very little that can be said about the piece. It's kind of like being handed a T-shirt and being asked which fashion design house it came from.

Jim
