Re: tsuba boxes

by alchemyst

> >Is there a source of tsuba boxes which have secure centerpieces
which don't just fall off?


I've about every type of tsuba box known to mankind and ALL
of them have that problem. NEVER ship or transport tsuba in
them without protecting the tsuba from possible damage from
the $&*^%*$ little nails should centerpiece come loose. You
might also insist the those shipping tsuba to you do the same.

I have on some, removed the centerpiece and used a drop of
white glue and replaced the centerpiece. That seems to help
for normal use, but I still don't trust them for shipping
or transport.

by charles_granick

To all,
While I have only been collecting for a few years, I have had very good success by simply replacing the 2 small nails with 2 very small brass flathead wood screws. They hold exceedingly well -- it is almost impossible to manually create any rocking motion in the " centerpiece ". ( Is there a real name for these centerpieces ? ) In addition, the brass is much less likely to scratch the tsuba if they should somehow come loose.
If anyone is interested, I could find out the correct screw size and length that would be appropriate for the typical tsuba box.
Regards to all,
Chuck Granick

> I have on some, removed the centerpiece and used a drop of white glue

Try using a modern carpenter's yellow glue. It will be stronger than the
wood. Only use a small amount, and then either clamp or put some weight
on the center piece so that it stays absolutely flush while the glue
dries.

The stuff is extremely strong, you will tear the wood backing out before
breaking the joint.

P.S. Tsuba bags are easy just get yourself some
pliable felt cut it about an 8th of an inch wider than
the Tsuba on either side and 3 times the length. Fold
the tsuba up with the felt and sew up two sides. This
should give you a flap that you can either sew velcro
onto it or get some silk string to tie the bag shut.

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: tsuba boxes

by dhs1us

I'm a lurker on the list, but thought I'd chime in about this question. I work as a conservator
(artwork on paper) by day, so safe storage is one thing we're concerned about. I'd avoid using
any sort of curing latex or Styrofoam product. Prolonged off-gassing can be a problem. I'm not
sure that the vapors will affect metal, but they will definitely affect silk. I'd urge you to
consider getting in touch with an objects conservator at a major museum with tsuba in their
collection. Perhaps the Museum of Fine Arts Boston, with which I am familiar.

As for foam, there is a safe foam on the market called 'Ethafoam' made by Dow chemical. There is
a company called Gaylord (www.gaylord.com) which sells it but I am sure there are others. It is
archivally sound and can be cut with scissors/knife to any shape.

-Doug Sanders
