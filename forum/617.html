polarizing subject - part deux

by rkgatteleport

Hey guys,

the polarizing material arrived in the mail earlier in the week and I
had a chance to try
polarizing the light source this morning.

Looking at the screen I found that the glare and direct reflections
pretty much went
away(!) but, surprise! it completely fooled the light metering system of
the G2
(I haven't bothered to post a picture of the result - its pretty black).

So I went to manual and found I gotta get a bigger light (I went to a
100 watt halogen bulb
for the following pictures - I didn't use the second light because I was
a bit short
on time this morning). I re - did the color balance for the final photo
(which I shot first),
but this next photo (the tsuba with only the circular polarizer on)
isn't quite right (it was
shot last and I didn't redo the white balance after removing the
polarizing sheet for the
light source). You'll note the pics aren't up to my usual standard of
sharpness either - I
think this is due to me opening up the f-stop (which reduces the depth of
field) and perhaps this camera suffers from the same autofocus errors
that some
of the other canon cameras (notably the 10D) have...

That being said, this is a photo of the tsuba with only the circular
polarizer on - note
all the direct reflections:

http://home.teleport.com/~rkg/misc/kagamishi_control_small.JPG

this final photo is with the polarizing sheet in place:

http://home.teleport.com/~rkg/misc/kagamishi_all_polarized_small.JPG

the first thing to note is that most of those annoying reflections are
mostly gone (!)
the second thing to notice is the couple of purplish distortions I think
these can be minimized
or eliminated by moving the light further away and/or to a different
location (just get it further out
of the family of angles for the tsuba), or maybe a small gobo or
something. I think the white
flash at the bottom was due to some light getting around the polarizing
sheet.

I also think its still a little underexposed (again, I was in a hurry,
and kind of doinked the exposure/fstop until it looked about right in
the viewscreen...)
(it would be really useful if Canon had actually allowed the user to
control all the camera's
features remotely with the software they provide).

This can be compared to a pic just using a polarizing filter and careful
light setup (and letting
the camera (which is usually smarter than I am) do the exposure stuff):

http://home.teleport.com/~rkg/photos/tsuba/kagamishi_front_polarized_small.JPG

and again, the above pic with the circular polarizer set to minimum
glare reduction:

http://home.teleport.com/~rkg/misc/kagamishi_front_non_polarized_small.JPG

My conclusion would be that the use of a circular polarizer to cut glare
is a no-brainer,
and that it appears that polarizing the source light has promise as
well, though I suspect
the big winner would be for trying to photograph swords, where its
almost impossible
not to get a big bloom of direct reflection off of something. I'll
probably try that later
this weekend on my new tanto (its a wicked looking osoraku piece for
those who care...).

rkg
