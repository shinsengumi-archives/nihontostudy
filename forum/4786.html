Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Fwd: Can anyone ID this tsuba?

by neobliviscar

Becky,

I posted a scan in your folder. It should answer most
of your question. According to Gary Murtha's Sword
Fittings book pg 44, it is probably a tsuba by Kyoto
Kiyonori or one of his students.

I've seen this tsuba a few times over the years. If
you (carefully) remove the pin holding on the tsuka
and look at the face of the tsuba that was under the
tsuka, you will (maybe 50/50 %) see a gold 'KAO' mark
like that on the tsuba images uploaded to your folder.
The Kao will probably allow us to attribute it to a
specific person, althought the KAO shown by Murtha and
that recorded by Haynes are somewhat different for
these guys. Still, we should have a good idea.

According to Murtha, Kiyonori was "A Kyoto artist.
Signed with gold inlaid kao. His favorite design being
crickets in grass. One of his students was Atsuiye who
changed his name to Noriiye."

They are nice tsubas. One of the fellow listmembers
had one a few years back. If he sold it, he could
probably tell you the value best.
I did not look closely, but do the fuchi-kashira
match the tsuba in design? That would be nice.

Curran

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Fwd: Can anyone ID this tsuba?

by neobliviscar

Becky,

I would agree with Barry that your fuchi-kashira look
like late Higo work. The Higo school is a nice school,
and did good work. Their style spread and was often
copied. I too think the design is of a very stylized
dragon. It might be what is known as Edo Higo (Higo
style manufactured in Edo). The sharp shape of the
kashira is uncommon.

On the sword, I cannot say much. It is unsigned. It
doesn't seem any of us recognize that mark. I was
hoping someone would.
Without a signature and with the sword blade "out
of polish" it will be hard for us to say what school
or person made the blade. Many very old blades (of
high art value) are not signed, but also many 1920s to
1945 blades (of low art value) were unsigned. Someone
with experience in assessing out of polish
"closet-finds" like yours should take a serious look
at it. You sword may be a diamond,- or it may be a
dud. Given that it has some fair fittings of older
origin (not the military issue of WW2), I would say it
has a fair chance at being a decent blade. That is no
guarantee, -just my gut feeling.

Curran
