by yamaimojg

Matt,
I've wondered if those swords made for export to China were mounted in Japan. If they were shipped with koshirae, would they have bothered with kozuka and kogai? If they were mounted in China, would there be any reason for them to be fitted out like a Japanese sword? The signed guard in the Boston museum collection has kozuka and kogai ana, so it was presumably made for a Japanese-style mount, or perhaps just copied from a Japanese tsuba treating the ana as part of the decoration rather than for use.

Jim
-


Jim. that would be interesting, as at the start of the Kangxi period,
the whole of China was effectively still at war since the Qing were
taking over from the earlier Ming dynasty rulers.

When dating Chinese porcelain, we make use of a term called the
"Transitional Period" that lasts from 1620 - 1683 approx to indicate
that these pieces were made during a time of great turmoil, when the
imperial porcealain factories were not functioning (they had been
ransacked)............ however I digress.

We also know that hundreds of thousands of Japanese swords were exported
to the Chinese Court from the Kamakura period onwards into the 15th
century. However I have no literature stating that this continued into
the 17th cent, does anyone know anything about that ?

One can just imagine some metal worker in Nanking making a tsuba for his
Great Grandfathers treasured Japanese sword to take it to war once
again........

I might add it is extremely unusual for any Chinese artisan to sign
their work with the exception of perhaps paintings.

Matt

by matthewhoyle2000

Good point Jim, that would be fascinating to find out. I do know that
the swords were nearly all exported to the Chinese Court and the
aristocracy, and they (the Chinese upper class, especially the Emperor)
had a kind of tendency to "embelish" items of great value. The 18th cent
Qianlong emperor for example had his own poems engraved onto priceless
12th cent Song Dynasty works or art that were handed down through the
generations. I could quite well imagine them replacing a Japanese tsuba
on a Japanese sword with one of their own if they thought it was an
improvement.

My guess would be that the swords were exported with koshirae, but I
would love to know for sure how this exactly went down. Another
interesting point is that in 12 years I have never ever seen a Japanese
sword found at the Imperial Chinese court. Since so many of them were
exported all those years ago, you would think there must still be one or
two lying around. Very strange !

- Matt
