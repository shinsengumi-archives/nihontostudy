I'm sure Milt means the Kanzan books.

It's not surprising that Kanzan used these sources, as almost all
Japanese scholarship is simply repeating what the previous authorities
had to say. Anything else would be disrespectful, right?

I have a couple of these kinds of books. The fact that they were
printed means they were not "secret," even 150 years ago. Much of the
information gets repeated by Wada in his scarce 1913 companion volume
to the Furukawa collection book, which is currently being serialized
for us in the NCJSC newletter (Joly's translation, edited and
annotated by Alan Harvie.)

Akiyama and Torigoye are two that did not follow the conventional line
of thought, and if you've been reading what Haynes - who's in that
"lineage" - has been writing on the subject of Japanese scholarship,
you know what I'm talking about. Sasano was another who colored
outside the lines, but his work has already been discounted by the
powers that be.
