My mouth is open to receive boots of any size... This dipstick has just seen
the second picture. Sighhh... I plead alcoholic indulgence after a long and
exciting day...

The second picture reinforces my earlier expressed opinion - note the bad ware
at the yokote (a crack, heaven forbid), while the poorly polished kissaki
suggests that the boshi "runs out". The only change I would make to my earlier
expressions is that the sword could be (could be!!) Sue Koto. However, from
what I can see the sword is still "no chop"and the nakago suggests this sword is
o-suriage mumei!!! Which reinforces the observation that a poor sword is a poor
sword whenever it was made. Here I must say that I'm talking about the Sword as
Art, not whether it is a good cutter on the Sengoku Jidai battlefield. Given
all this, IMHO the red-lacquered inscription is of academic interest only...

Regards again,

Barry Thomas.

Katana/nagamaki and blade inscriptiion - Harry..where are you?

by sigp210

Barry,
I appreciate the comments. My photography was horrible on this one..I
had 5 minutes in bad light. The boshi is intact and healthy. No hagire
anywhere. The polish is a nasty semi-amateur job that makes it look
worse then it is. The sword is actually very nice. In person, the
hamon is clear and has some activity. Lines are sharp and clean.
Yes..I agree it looks o-surige. Some opinions have been that it is a
nagamaki naoshi shortened and reshaped in shinshinto or shinto times,
and the inscriptions were done at that time.
They seem to be in the Chinese style of writing, and boast about the
sharpness of the sword. Would be very interested in a translation from
Harry, as I am told he is familiar with this type of language.
This one you have to hold in hand to appreciate. Nothing fantastic,
but has a nice sugata and nice lines. Fairly thick. I will try next
time to do better shots of the whole blade, and give dimensions.
Thanks for the comments and views. Much appreciated.

Brian
