Kinko Meikan question

by musashibobenkei

I have a question from a customer about the editions of the Kinko Meikan.
The original edition published in 1974 has four mei photos per page, and
there is a wonderfully handy translation published by the NCJSC that was
done by John Yumoto and Alan Harvie, which is still available from the club.

Obviously the translation makes this edition far more desirable to
non-readers of Japanese than the later edition(s) which show six mei photos
per page, and of course do not match up with the translation at all.

My question has to do with my recollection - it was perhaps even a
discussion on one of the lists long ago - that the problem with the first
edition was the significant number of gimei signatures illustrated, and
perhaps other errors. These were then corrected in the later editions.

I've searched what archives I have to verify this bit of information kicking
around my aging and untrustworthy brain without success. Can anyone
corroborate or refute this or shed any light on the subject?

And before anyone asks, I'm out of stock on the first edition, but you can
add your name to the want list. Second edition orders will be filled right
away!

Craig-
I haven't heard this, not that I am the endall, but I think
you'll find with a simple knowledge of kanji and kana the second
edition and the Yumoto translation can be used together. The same
names and photos appear in both with a number of additions in the
second. A serious study of the 2nd using the translation would allow
you to suss out the info in many of the additional entries and would
be a great way to reinforce ones Nihongo lessons...

by musashibobenkei

Tom and Arnold, thanks for your replies, you both made excellent
observations.

I spoke with Robert Haynes yesterday and raised the issue with him.

To summarize, he was not aware of any particular problem with gimei pieces,
in fact he said that compared to other references (like Wakayama) that were
produced from submitted photographic examples (which Arnold pointed out,)
Kinko Meikan was done mostly from examples in hand and was a more reliable
reference.

He of course mentioned, as Tom pointed out, there is not really a problem
using the translation with the later edition if one is willing to work a
little at reading the kanji and kana, and certainly one will learn more by
doing so.

Bob said that he had started to make a comparison list between the first and
later editions, as some examples in the first were dropped, most stayed, and
of course many were added. His conclusion has been that the later edition is
more complete and accurate and therefore the better reference.

Of course that still leaves me wondering *which* book it was that supposedly
had many gimei in the first edition ... or if my memory is manufacturing
things.
