Re: [nihontostudy] Tsuba // Cupped Tsuba

by Peter A Bleed

Friends,
I have become rather interested in Namban tsuba that are made to look like
rapier guards. They are pretty rare and I own only a couple and have seen
only a few others. In that regard, I have liked looking at other Namban
tsuba and cupped guards. All that to say this is an interesting thread.
John, I'd love to see your rapier style guard if you can post a pic.
Let me say, however, that I am not certain about the link between cupped
tsuba and European swords. I have seen a number of cupped tsuba that I
suppose could be inspired by European cuphilts, but may of these look like
they are Hizen style rather than "Namban" (whatever that is). Furthermore,
in the case of the tsuba that got this conversation started, it looks to me
like the cup would have pointed TOWARD the blade! Pretty different from the
European cuphilt style.
Peter

Curran,
I sure won't promise any "depth" on this issue. I also don't know which
book you have in mind, but I happen to have Yoshimura's "Namban Tsuba:
Akogare No Dezain" with me here. It shows NO cupped guards at all... which
merely means they don't fit his classification.
I suppose that cupped guards could be reflective of European designs, but
it seems to be no more than a very general reference. Furthermore, I have
become convince by John Lissenden that most Namban "rapier" guards date to
the 18th century. In John's view the classic "rapier" tsuba appear to be
copies not of rapiers but of the later smallsword form - which is a
development of the 18th century. Cuphilt rapiers, on the other hand, are
(supposedly!) Spanish and 17th century. Given how things were in Japan at
that time, I wonder how common they could have been in Japan. Maybe, of
course, that could explain how the samurai could have got the cup pointing
in the wrong direction!
Peter

Re: [nihontostudy] Tsuba // Cupped Tsuba

by soshuju

Phil et al-
Remember that the tsuba is a hand stop and not a handguard.
Thus the more ornate of the two sides is worn "OUT" when the sword is
in the scabbard. Once the sword is drawn it is just a hand stop (or
guard) but all decoration ceases to have any meaning. With few
exceptions the most work is applied to outside of the "cup", which if
you think about it is most aesthetically pleasing...
-t

by neobliviscar

Peter,

I do not know if I have met Mr. Lissenden. By chance,
is this person a fencing coach on the West Coast?

I've also had similar thoughts about some of rapier
tsuba being more the smallsword form copies of the
18th century. I was not sure how far back such
smallswords were made and used. From mental trivia, I
remebered record of one used in a duel c. 1715.

My knowledge of western sword types is erratic, so I
don't put up much arguement about what was first made
when.
Sailors of all types (English, Dutch, Portugese,
Spanish... southern Spanish and Portugese of mixed
Moorish ancestry) carried weapons of all types. I
wondered about cutlasses and other weapons. Shipdeck
weapons made for hacking with little or no knowledge
of fencing.

The study tsuba I have was worn carved face towards
the blade, with the cupping toward the hand. Most
interesting to me, the INSIDE of the cup was once
entirely covered in zogan of gold or silver. It is a
fist sized tsuba, so that was a rather flashy display
to anyone looking at the samurai who carried it.
I did not realize this when I first received it.
Only when it was pointed out to me by Jim Gilbert. I
can only wonder if it were patterned, and what the
entire rig must have looked like.
Someone quite intentionally dug most of the gold or
silver out of the cup, leaving small scars. A shame.

Curran
