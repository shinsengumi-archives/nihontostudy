Re: [nihontostudy] clear lacquer

by shirogitsune99

Hi All:
 
  Heianjo are complex.  For a start, most Heianjo (real Heianjo and not Edo period copies) are Momoyama period or earlier.  They were mainly made just for a hundred years or so, basically the 16th century.  The earlier pieces are sometimes called Onin, but even if you include this group, you still only get the period around 1450-1600.  That means that they are old.  Many were lacquered.  Some were well preserved some were not.  The lacquer is part of the overall gestalt.  If you see a hundred Heianjo tsuba, 25 or so will be lacquered, of which 5 will be in excellent condition, 10 will be decently preserved, 5 will be in need of varying degrees of restoration and 5 will be dead.

Cheers,

Shirogitsune

Re: [nihontostudy] clear lacquer

by sworddad

I've always enjoyed Heianjo (never owning an Onin work that I know of) and Yoshiro mon-sukashi works. Always like brass inlay on good iron!

To my surprise, I just received what I thought looked like a nice early iron with brass inlay tsuba, recently purchased of Ebay, my first Ebay purchase in ages. After 40-odd years I thought I had a good "eye" for such things; just bought a repro! Yikes! Hope to reverse the deal but must admit that "pictures are deceiving" at times. Looks like an honest mistake on the seller's part, but it points up the importance of in-hand study. (I think others spotted this since my bid was much too low if authentic. Just mentioning this to point out how good (in pictures only) these repros are today.

Enjoyed this topic of lacquer on Heianjo works. I once owned a very similar (or same one?) tsuba that was pointed out here. I need to sit down and check some of my other early tsuba for lacquer.

Ron
