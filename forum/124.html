Re: [nihontostudy] Re: O-dachi Koshirae

by yamaimojg

Rich,
These were made for use. What makes Oyamazumi so interesting is that the early swords and armor preserved there were practical items that were in use before being given to the shrine. Many of the blades are full of chips and pits and most of the koshirae are very simple and/or in degraded condition. These are pieces are rare remainders of what was for the most part used up and discarded long ago.

Matt & Richard,

Some very long blades were made for devotional reasons, however others in Nanbokucho and early Muromachi were intended for use. Obviously they were not drawn from the saya like katana. I suspect that they may have been carried more for intimidation value than for effectiveness as a battlefield weapon, but I'll have to defer to Tom and the other swordsmen on that. I suppose that they are not so different from a long pole arm.

Re o-dachi,I remember a piece in the National Geo about fifteen years
ago about the amazing disappearing Pacific tuna, including the sushi
favorite, bluefin. A number of big bluefin were shown kayoed on a
Japanese distributor's floor with the bidding about to start, with
the note that they could go for up, to $5k a pop. A sidebar story
had to do with bluefin cutting katana, about five foot nagasa, made
in semi-traditional manner (seems they were quench hardened
or "tempered", though possibly not fold forged, and likely not of
tamahagane), the heat treatment which resulted in the "loss" of one
every now and then. So it seems that even now, o-dachi live. "The
right tool for the right job."
