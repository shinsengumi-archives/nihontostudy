Nunome-zogan

by Peter A Bleed

List-eners,
Has there been a synthetic study of nunome zogan? This is the
Japanese term for the overlay techniques that places gold or silver leaf on
steel by rubbing it into/onto very fine cross hatched striations. When well
done it is very beautiful, but often it is either entirely gone or survives
only as traces. Do we know when and where it originated in Japan? And where
else is it practiced?
Peter Bleed

Re: [nihontostudy] Nunome-zogan

by yamaimojg

Peter,
I'm not aware of such a study. It would be a great topic for an article in the Bulletin of the Metals Museum. My impression is that nunome zogan first appears on Japanese sword fittings in the late Muromachi period. Where it originates is an even harder question, but nunome zogan is strongly associated with Ko-Shoami work and some sources place the origin of the Ko-Shoami in Kyoto.

I have seen some very sophisticated gold and silver inlay on iron from North China ca. 5th-4th C BC, and of course complex Tang metalwork, but I can't recall nunome type inlay from that time. As Rich mentioned "nunome" is seen elsewhere in the Middle East and Asia. I have seen nunome-style inlay on old European material as well. There is an "Anglo-Scandinavian" iron stirrup in the Metropolitan Museum from the 10-11th C with brass inlay that is affixed with a nunome-type technique.

I have a few pages of notes from Bob Haynes where he records various nunome cross hatching styles seen on tsuba and attempts to correlate them to their various makers.

Jim

Jim and Others,
Thanks for the replies. I too have noted that the technique we call
nunome turns up in lots of premodern metal working traditions. In addition
to those noted, I have seen it on Tibetan and "Chinese" stuff, most notably
in our area of interest on Chinese "Namban" tsuba, for example. The
explicit item that triggered my question is an iron six-sided vase I have
with heavy (but not very attractive) silver overlay that I think is Korean.
Does anyone know the Korean word for such stuff, by the way?
I have also noted that the earliest apparent occurrences of this
technique on Japanese stuff all seem to have a Muromachi sort of age. Given
its apparent world-wide distribution of the technique, it must have either
been introduced or invented in Japan at that time, OR, older stuff has not
survived. I tend to favor the later idea.
It is possible, I betcha, that the fine iron striations that hold the
gold/silver on, simply won't survive more than about 500 years. If they
largely oxidize away in that kind of time frame, all older examples would
be gone so it would look like nunome suddenly appeared.
Let me add, in closing, that virtually all of the techniques Japanese
kinko use do show up in other parts of the world and other artistic
traditions. The Japanese like to see themselves as innovators in this
regard, but what they really did, IMHO, is bring basic techniques to high
levels of virtuosity. This weekend I bought a couple of swords. One is a
rather ordinary nippon-to, but the other a really nice cuphilt (Spanish?)
rapier that is embellished with light repousse, good horidashi engraving,
and a wonderfully even "nanako-ishime" finish that is as nice as any on an
iron tsuba. Craftsmanship is where we find it, mebbe.
