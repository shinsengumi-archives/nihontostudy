Hi:
No, the images you attached are all photographs of well known and often
illustrated Nobuiye. Akiyama, who died in 1936, was the foremost
researcher on tsuba, particularly iron. of his day, and has left a
lasting impression on the categorization of tsuba to this day. He
collected dozens and dozens of oshigata of Nobuiye, some very
unassuming, as was the found one I refereed to, and they were published
by a Mr. Nakayama, or Nakamura or something close to that (only my poor
brain as a reference here). When you see one like the example you asked
about, what makes me suspicious is the "pulled out of the fire" look it
has and the lack of depth and sharpness to the chisel work. Those done
by the Norisuke, I and II, often look quite good, but they must be
examined in hand. They often are deficient on the part of the mimi you
don't see from a photograph, as it is said they tended to work from
books that did not illustrate the sides, or that is one story I have
heard.
There are many many gimei Owari Nobuiye out there as his work was very
much in vogue during the early 19th Cent. revival period. One the other
hand there were other, entirely legitimate Nobuiye working elsewhere,
and they are entirely worthy collectors' pieces in many cases.
The dealer, that should probably remain confidential to him.
I hope this helps.
Arnold
