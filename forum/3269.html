> David et al-
> O'neill and Nelson are about all you need. Sword books and
> kodogu references tend to follow simple rules and if you learn to
> recognize a handful of "signpost" kanji you'll be able to navigate
> them and glean much of the info.
> With sword signatures saku or tsukuru are usually preceeded
> by the two characters of the smiths name (you'll need O'neill).
> Kuni,ju and (no)Kami are preceeded by place names, usually two
> characters (in the Nelson indecies). The character shu is usually
> part of the "Chinese" name of a province (also found in the Nelson
> indecies). nen, tsuki,hi(jitsu) and dai are usually preceeded by
> numbers, the year, month, day and generation respectively. Of course
> nen can follow a zodaiacal character like year of the rat, year of
> the tiger, (these characters are also found in the indecies).
> Most descriptions follow sugata, jigane, hamon, nakago and
> background. It is in the background that one can get lost as these
> are usually given in regular Japanese, not "swordspeak." Even then if
> you can learn to recognize dai, jidai, nen, kuni, den, (ryu)ha,
> deshi, and mon, you can get a lot out of what seem undecipherable
> paragraphs. Mon by the way means gate, Masatsune mon would translate
> "Masatsune school", as in "he studied within the gate of Masatsune"
> or "in the Masatsune school". Often rendered as "student of" it can
> mean that he may not have been a direct student but was a product of
> that group, if it was a large school or the relationship isn't clear.
> For tsuba the signatures follow similar rules though one
> encounters a wider use of GO or art names. Regular dictionaries don't
> really help there, I suggest Haynes or the Kinko Meikan. The
> descriptions usually follow Subject, shape, plate, engraving and
> inlay. So look for zu,sugata,ita,hori and zogan, knowing the terms
> from glossaries you'll quickly start to recognize the kanji as they
> are repeated in many listings.
> In general I think reading swordspeak, learning to read
> swordspeak is easy, learn the hiragana and a handful of kanji and you
> can get a lot out of those books. Please bear in mind I type all this
> not for you as I am sure you know much of this already, rather it is
> intended for those the list that might be having similar troubles...
