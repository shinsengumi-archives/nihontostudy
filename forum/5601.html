Sword Ringing

by deanwayland

Hello All,

This is going to sound like a somewhat unusual question for this group,
but it is one that I am often faced with, and I need some expert input.

I teach a sword sport, not a martial art, and run a tiny historical
society here in the United kingdom, dedicated to the era of first
contact between Europeans and the Japanese.

I do not own any genuine nihonto, nor do I "collect" replicas.

Over almost the last thirty years, I have commissioned quite a number
of totally custom made replica swords manufactured with durability, not
sharpness in mind. Meaning they are not as hard as one intended to take
an edge. I have noted that symmetrically design swords "sing" with a
clear ringing tone when impacted, even when done gently, and that
asymmetrical ones like our katana and wakizashi do not. These instead
produce a flat "clat" like note.

Non of the ten Japanese weapons we have had made "sing", much the same
as iaito and I presumed, that this was due to their asymmetry, which is
what I have been giving in answer to the question at our public shows
"why don't your swords ring like in the movies?" I've always thought
that the ringing tones in Japanese movies was a dubbed in sound effect,
rather than a true representation of the weapon's own nature.

For a very long time now I have been wondering if this is incorrect, and
it is due to our weapons lack of hardness not their asymmetry. Our
current Japanese weapons tend to be in the Rockwell scale between 52-54.

As collectors of true nihonto, can you confirm or deny this point: do
nihonto "sing" when struck? And no, I'm not expecting anyone to actually
try this on a historical weapon:-)

For our next project, a moderately sized no-dachi, I am approaching a
new sword smith (our old one recently passed away), and I am considering
raising the hardness to circa 56-58 or maybe 60, to improve resistance
to damage, and possibly gaining a sweeter note from the blade, assuming
that my former hypothesis is in fact incorrect.

It would be good to have a definitive answer to this question, as it
would be better than having to say that "currently, we don't know".
Filling in gaps in one's knowledge is always a good thing.

Any help, observations etc., would be most warmly received.

With much thanks

Dean
