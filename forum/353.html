been a little quite

by retalt

Greetings all, I thought I would run this by the group since its been a
bit ( read lot ) quite out there. A mate and I were talkin swod tok the
other night and we were discussing tanto. He mentioned and sent pics of
a very small blade that measures app 22cm in total length. As it turns
out, I have one almost identical in size. Mine, as is his is also quite
thick, both about 7 or 8 mm at the munemachi, very much like a
yorio-doshi only far too short.

Paul mentioned that he was told his was made as a gift for the wife of a
samurai as a wedding present. He believes his to be mid/late edo and
traditionally made. MIne is out of polish and hard to see hada or hamon,
nie, nioi etc.. but the rest of it looks pretty similar.

here is a link to the two tanto Tantos

I believe blades were common gifts for samurai wife's and I would expect
them to be smaller than usual but 22 cm is pretty small and more like a
child's size, though I guess weilded properly, could inflict just as
much damage as a 30cm tanto.

looking forward to all opinions.

cheers

Rich

Probably talking outta my hat as usual but I think we should

Ron,
Koshirae with that kind of spring clip are usually (maybe always) fitted with a ring rather than a traditional kurikata. I don't know what the difference in use was, but there probably was a practical reason. I don't have a firm understanding of toppei koshirae, but at least some of these examples with the external spring clip were apparently made for wear with Western-style clothing. Some toppei koshirae without a spring clip still have a ring-swivel fitting instead of a kurikata. Others have a fixed or swivel ring mounted on the "edge" rather than the side of the saya. I think that the sageo used was more like a string rather than a flat braid. I suspect that they were worn at a different angle to the body and possibly there was a greater risk of the sword falling out of the saya, hence the clip. I don't know much about them. Tom, any ideas?

Etched soft metal isn't a very common technique. Good lacquer and an interesting wrap, too. It would fit right in the Edo no Tanto Koshirae picture book.

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: been a little quite

by soshuju

Jim et al-
While I dig this style of Koshirae very much, I can't say I
know all I would like to about them. Toppei I believe refers to the
shape of the kashira and kojiri IE; tapering. I agree with all that
Jim has said, I believe these were popular at the tail end of the Edo
period when Western weapons and tactics were being fervently studied
by the Japanese.
This style of koshirae could be used with hakama or European
trousers, hence the swivel often seen. The swivelling "kurikata"
allowed the sword to be thrust thru the Obi or slung "backward" in
western fashion when dressed in pants.
The gunto koshirae is an extension of this idea and one can
see how many different ways were invented to allow quick conversion
from saber to uchigatana...
More often than not these are of superior workmanship. My
belief is that the new officers of the newly formed rifle or
artillery units went for this style and most of them with the money
to get a special set up like this were likely sons of the Bakufu.
There is one memorable example of this style which is decidedly
common, showing rough simple work suitable more for a foot soldier
than an officer; That would be Saigo Takamoris' shuzaya toppei
goshirae which carried a gimei Muramasa blade.
I think this little tanto exhibits all the features of a
quality koshirae in this style. There is one more feature not yet
mentioned and that is that Toppei koshirae typically feature iron
fittings with silver inlay (sometimes gold).
That really is all I know (or have been taught). Thirsting for more,
Tom
