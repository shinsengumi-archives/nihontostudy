kokuin (stamped seals)

by sworddad

Hi all -

Somebody asked me about how "kokuin" were put onto nakago. I've always refered to these as "hot stamped" but really, I wonder how the smith applied these stamps. The nakago being soft (not hardened), were they punched in after the sword was finished? It would seem illogical to think the smith "heated the nakago" enough to "hot stamp" their seal. Can anyone explain how this was done?

A related question is about Masahide who did apply a "kokuin" to his nakago. Does anyone know if Masahide ever signed his nakago with his "kokuin" only? (Not signing his full mei with it.)

Ron

Re: [nihontostudy] kokuin (stamped seals)

by sworddad

Matt, Rich, all -

Yes, the Gassan did this thing, too. The few times I've looked at these stamps they really didn't look like they were "cold stamped." They don't appear "sharply" cut nor do they appear "pushed out" around their edges (so to speak). But I can't understand how you could "hot stamp" a nakago. It would seem the nakago would have to be at a "red" temperature for hot stamping, and that would seem to need to be done before the hamon treatment is done. These stamps seem to be nicely done and in keeping with the rest of a "finished" nakago, not done way ahead of finishing the nakago. Honestly, I have no knowledge of how this is done, other than what has been passed on over the decades. It would be interesting to hear from somebody (swordsmith?) who has first-hand experience on this. Let's see what others can offer...

Ron

Re: [nihontostudy] kokuin (stamped seals)

by Barry Thomas

Dear Blokes,

Trickie, if the stamp was white hot it would simply be like a piece of
plasticine when hit with a 10 kilo hammer!!! A friend of mine has a Naotane
wakizashi with a kokuin that reads in katakana "KOROMO", the town where
Naotane made the sword. This stamp has been struck slightly off true. I'm
not in metallurgy either, but I would speculate that when a hardened stamp
is placed against a tang resting on a very heavy anvil then struck very hard
with a 10 kilo hammer, that the softer tang metal truly flows like caramel
fudge - somewhat like the phenomenon of explosive plastic deformation. I
remember reading of a swordsmith in Japan who (in an interview) said that
metal can be pushed around from place to place. So all in all, considering
what little I do know of metallurgy I think it is done as I have outlined.

The Gassan with the stamp is more likely to be by Sadakazu 1st than
Sadakatsu. I have a monograph written by Benjamin Hazard of a Gassan I
tanto in the MMA that has a kokuin (from a failing memory...).

Regards,

Barry Thomas.
