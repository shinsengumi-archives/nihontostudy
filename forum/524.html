Re: [nihontostudy] tsuba value ?

by Arnold Frenzel

Hi:
What an interesting question! I think that as a general rule the notion
of size and value, other things being equal, except in the restricted
situation of a daisho perfectly matched, but now separated pair, is really
not an operational comparison. Tsuba size choices of the artisan were
determined by the parameters of the blade and koshirae appropriate to the
period of initial manufacture. What make tsuba interesting and desirable
is how close the fit is what it ought to be for the time of manufacture.
If one follows Sasano sensei, for example, with respect to the earliest
tosho tsuba, the early ones were small and the later ones as a rule were
larger. Even though the designs might be quite similar, the criterion of
originality and probable scarcity ought to yield a significantly higher
price for the older but smaller piece. Mutatis mutandis the same argument
holds for tsuba of different types and periods. How close the fit between
the piece at hand and the ideal type is probably the main determinant of
value. For a short answer I do not believe that there is a correlation
merely between size and market value. My two cents worth anyway.

Arnold Frenzel
afrenzel@...

Re: [nihontostudy] papering tsuba

by arnold frenzel

Hi:
That is any excellent set of questions. It is useful to remember that
while sword knowledge and what should be designated as what goes back at
least to the time of the first Ashikaga Shogun, knowledge of tosogu,
particularly iron tsuba, goes back no early than the late Bakumatsu era,
particularly with the writings of Akiyama and
Kawaguchi. The knowledge paradigm is a still expanding one and the
boundaries are somewhat indistinct and evolving. There can be a great
deal of controversy as to what group something falls into and wildly
different opinions as to age of manufacture, that of course hinging on
the date of the adoption and substantial spread of the use of
uchi-gatana. All one needs to do is to watch the moving time line in the
writings of the late Sasano Masayuki.
My advice would be to be conservative in what you submit unless it is a
piece about which you have a great deal of curiosity and it being of
good workmanship, preservation and aesthetic content. On the other hand
one can not sell short the impact that papers have on the value of
fittings and the certainty with which they can be sold at a predictable
price, if that is of relevance to you.
Arnold

Re: [nihontostudy] papering tsuba

by davidzincavage

Thanks, Arnold. Not too long ago, I came across a reprint of a tsuba book
written in 1736. I can even supply a picture, because I put photos whenever
possible of Japanese language titles into my bibliography, which I need to
avoid duplicate acquisitions. Koshoyama found me a copy.

Kanzan, Matsumiya. Tsuba shokan koketsu, 7.5" x 10.5", 210 pages. Estimated
to be authored in 1736, "Tsuba shokan koketsu" is said to be the oldest
monograph on the appreciation of the tsuba. The hand-written original has
long been lost, but over the years numerous hand-copies were made among
tsuba and sword enthusiasts. Since the extant copies were becoming scarce
and out of reach of average tsuba enthusiasts, Shibata Mitsuo undertook the
project of publishing the book in a modern format in 1967. This book is the
result of that effort. Original text and illustrations (taken from several
reliable copies in existence) are duplicated in large pages, and modern
typesetting was added to enable today's audience to read the cursive
classical writing. The direct translation of the title is "Orally
transmitted secret of the appreciation of the tsuba," but "How to Appreciate
the Tsuba" would be much more appropriate for the content.

Kanzan, Matsumiya. Tsuba shokan koketsu, 7.5" x 10.5", 210 pages.
Estimated
to be authored in 1736, "Tsuba shokan koketsu" is said to be the oldest
monograph on the appreciation of the tsuba. The hand-written original
has
long been lost, but over the years numerous hand-copies were made among
tsuba and sword enthusiasts. Since the extant copies were becoming
scarce
and out of reach of average tsuba enthusiasts, Shibata Mitsuo undertook
the
project of publishing the book in a modern format in 1967. This book is
the
result of that effort. Original text and illustrations (taken from
several
reliable copies in existence) are duplicated in large pages, and modern
typesetting was added to enable today's audience to read the cursive
classical writing. The direct translation of the title is "Orally
transmitted secret of the appreciation of the tsuba," but "How to
Appreciate
the Tsuba" would be much more appropriate for the content.

I have not submitted many (any) tsuba, but was faced with the same

I would think:

1. if there is a possibility that it is old and rare vs. the more common
pieces and you want to assure yourself or a buyer of this

papered... I'd say generally avoid it as not being fiscally sound.
