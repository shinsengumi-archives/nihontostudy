Elliott et al-
Despite light sales activity no one I talked to was disappointed
with this years show. The tables were all full, unlike laste year
when storms back east prevented some from attending. There were
several Japanese with tables set up that had not been here in the
recent past, Most notably Aoi Arts. There were exceptional swords,
and lots of average blades though maybe not as many swords overall
compared to last year. two things of note were the number of Gassan
works for sale, there were a half dozen polished and papered pieces
scattered through out the show room and a real chance to study
Sadakazu, Sadakatsu and others. Also there were many more koshirae of
exceptional quality and interest and I enjoyed quite a bit of my time
pouring over these.
The NTHK presented the Swords and Fittings of Mino Province, the
Naginata seen below is from that exhibit. The NBTHK did a
presentation of Tanto, 13 total as I recall, from Awataguchi Hisakuni
to Gassan Sadakazu. The Soshu Yukimitsu tanti which was number two on
the table was my pick for best of show. There was a Soshu Hiromasa
Otanto (kowakizashi) in the middle of the pack that was in an older
polish which was a bit scuffed up but this did not prevent one from
seeing the quality, a fine example of the period and not the kind of
koto we usually see around here.
There was an NBTHK Kantei-kai as well. With a Yosozaemon Suksada
yoroidoshi and a Kawai Hisayuki katana being the stand-outs. The
Kawai blade was presented by the First Japanese Ambassadors to the US
to their American chaperone and it is still held in the same family.
A real treasure.
I bought some books as one might expect, sold very few that I
brought with me but I learned lots and enjoyed good company all
weekend. I am sure we'll here more from others as they get back to
their homes from way out west...
-t

Mas et al -
It would be interesting to find a connection. Kawai Hisayuki was a
Tokugawa vassal who made swords for pleasure. His given name was
Kametaro, his Go was Shoseishi, he had a son, Kawai Hisataka who
succeeded to the name of Hisayuki. The Shodai Hisayuki died in Tokyo
in Sept. 1868, his son died in Tokyo in 1886. I believe the temple
they are buried in is called the Ryuyoji.
Any of this fit your genealogy? would be cool if he was a long lost
cousin...
-t
