Re: [nihontostudy] anyone for a piece of history

by yamaimojg

Yes, it's mine. Thanks. There are much better examples including some that show areas of what appear to be the original surface and very nicely done hirazogan (no doubt revealed only after very careful cleaning).

Some years ago John Badenhop had a museum journal in Japanese that showed these guards being cleaned with a plasma etching technique. The chemistry was presumably chosen to etch the rust while having some reasonable selectivity to the unoxidized iron and the inlay. I only got a quick look, so I don't know the details.

Here's a soft metal example, too: http://home.earthlink.net/~jggilbert/irogane.htm

and some more fuzzy photos from pieces in museums:

http://home.earthlink.net/~jggilbert/kofun2.htm
http://home.earthlink.net/~jggilbert/kofun.htm

Some of the blades of this period also show extensive inlay in silver and sometimes gold. Some of these sat unrecognized for years before they were cleaned sufficiently to reveal the inlay. "Windows on the Japanese Past: Studies in Archaeology and Prehistory" has an article or two on these swords as I recall. There's even a piece there by some guy named Bleed, although not sword related.

Jim

Hi Jim,

You're welcome. Maybe soon we can have katakana on our fittings
kanteisho!! Since this information didn't go out on this list, let me
add that kenkyu-in is the first step to becoming a member of the shinsa
team. All of the members of the present team went through the ranks.
The next step is shinsa-in hosa, which means an assistant to the shinsa
team. Assistants should fill out the working papers -- in Japanese. I
could somewhat manage this, but in the end, I was given a break. The
third stage is full shinsa team member. This all typically takes about
four years or so from kenkyu-in to full shinsa team member.

I will also try and scan a photo of Choshu-ya's koshirae. I don't think
it sold.
