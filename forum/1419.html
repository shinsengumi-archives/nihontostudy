Re: [nihontostudy] tsuba designs?

by yamaimojg

Matt,
Yes in answer to all questions. There are references to some of the top kinko working with an eminent contemporary painter to develop their designs. I don't know if there is hard proof for these claims, but they are frequently made. It seems plausible, since a good draughtsman and metalworker isn't necessarily an inventive designer.

I'm not aware of any specific mention of customer-designed themes, but it'd be hard to imagine that it didn't happen. There are stories about some of the famous early chado (tea) masters having ceramic work made to their specifications by others. Come to think of it, I suppose that the Yagyu tsuba are probably a good example of customer design that was fabricated by a number of different makers both known and unknown. I haven't seen an original Yagyu design book, but I have a photocopy of a later hand-drawn copy of one.

I have seen some original design books for shiiremono tsuba. They are full of generic sorts of Nara-esq designs. Someone had broken one of these up and was selling individual pages at one of the sword shows years ago. They seem to be about the equivalent of tattoo parlor "flash."

There are at least several fittings books that show illustrations taken from surviving design books from known makers. One that happens to come to mind is Futagoyama Norisuke Ko, where about half of the book is illustrations of detailed sketches and oshigata by the Norisuke. Some are original designs but quite a few are classic Yagyu, Yamakichibei, etc., that were presumably used as guides for their forgery work.

I suspect that most if not all makers would have had documents like these. Basically sketch books for working out designs and oshigata to record finished works or works by others. Probably well more than half of the fittings makers that ever worked made their living by simply turning out copies of standard designs that they did not create. The number of real innovators isn't large, but these are of course the big names.

Many fittings motifs are elaborations of things that have been in the culture for many years and it would be hard to credit a given designer. Even some of the famous innovators' work was in coming up with a novel twist on an old theme. Most of them then proceeded to repeat themselves for the remainder of their careers, and their followers mostly did the same.

The first Yasuchika is one maker who really stands out for me in not doing this. Going through a book of his work, it's remarkable how many times he would develop something really good and then leave it to go on to something different and also great. His fabrication skill in both iron and copper alloy is incredible and he was either a great designer or knew the right people to work with (or steal from).

I think that Graham is talking about putting the final texture on the guard. The forging and shaping of the plate would be done with a normal hammer. Matt,
I doubt that the finish of early Akasaka guards was produced by the same sort of tooling. They do have a unique surface texture that is very uniform and fine grained. They are known for using a sandwiched construction with a hard skin layer, but I do not know how that layer was finished.

A common metal working technique is to make a texturing punch from annealed steel by driving it into nicely grained stone and then hardening it. It's possible that Ko-Akasaka guards were finished with this sort of tool, but the surface doesn't look beaten down and the effect is very uniform. My guess is that it was done another way.

RE: [nihontostudy] Ko-Katchushi tsuba.....

by Peter A Bleed

Matt,
You have engendered an interesting discussion and elicited Jim's usually
worthwhile insights. Thanks
In response to your comments, I took a look again and some of my katchushi
guards last night (they used to be so common and cheap that I couldn't
avoid becoming a 'collector'). I have always loved the finish of these
tsuba which looks old and natural but which is also obviously the creation
of human intention. I am not familiar with Gemmell's book, but - like Jim -
I am sceptical about the use of a pointed hammer to created tsuba surfaces.
I can believe that a kinko might make and use a hammer with a network of
very fine points. As I think about it, use of irregular hammer and anvil
surfaces during the forging of the initial plate might be a means of
setting up the internal irregularities we call bones etc.
I agree with Jim, too, that whatever means was used to created a surface
finish, it probably involved a chisel or punch rather than direct pounding
with a hammer.
In that regard, let me recall a conversation with Hagawa-san, the Sendai
kinko I have mentioned to the list before. He makes frequent use of
"ishime" which is a smooth but 'irregular' surface of small dots that looks
like a stone (ishi means stone). He told me that ishime is as hard to do as
nanako. It is done with specially crested punches ('tagane') that are
struck with a hammer - one dot at a time. Hagawa-san said that once
started, an entire surface had to be done in one setting to assure a
uniform texture. If the work was interrupted, the piece will be ruined.
Thus, before he starts an ishime surface, he tells his wife and family that
they should not interrupt him til the tapping stopped. Maybe the point here
is that even if special tools are used, it is still the craftsman's skill
and intensity that matters.

Peter Bleed

Matt,
Yes in answer to all questions. There are references to some of the top
kinko working with an eminent contemporary painter to develop their designs.
I don't know if there is hard proof for these claims, but they are
frequently made. It seems plausible, since a good draughtsman and
metalworker isn't necessarily an inventive designer.

I'm not aware of any specific mention of customer-designed themes, but it'd
be hard to imagine that it didn't happen. There are stories about some of
the famous early chado (tea) masters having ceramic work made to their
specifications by others. Come to think of it, I suppose that the Yagyu
tsuba are probably a good example of customer design that was fabricated by
a number of different makers both known and unknown. I haven't seen an
original Yagyu design book, but I have a photocopy of a later hand-drawn
copy of one.

I have seen some original design books for shiiremono tsuba. They are full
of generic sorts of Nara-esq designs. Someone had broken one of these up
and was selling individual pages at one of the sword shows years ago. They
seem to be about the equivalent of tattoo parlor "flash."

There are at least several fittings books that show illustrations taken from
surviving design books from known makers. One that happens to come to mind
is Futagoyama Norisuke Ko, where about half of the book is illustrations of
detailed sketches and oshigata by the Norisuke. Some are original designs
but quite a few are classic Yagyu, Yamakichibei, etc., that were presumably
used as guides for their forgery work.

I suspect that most if not all makers would have had documents like these.
Basically sketch books for working out designs and oshigata to record
finished works or works by others. Probably well more than half of the
fittings makers that ever worked made their living by simply turning out
copies of standard designs that they did not create. The number of real
innovators isn't large, but these are of course the big names.

Many fittings motifs are elaborations of things that have been in the
culture for many years and it would be hard to credit a given designer.
Even some of the famous innovators' work was in coming up with a novel twist
on an old theme. Most of them then proceeded to repeat themselves for the
remainder of their careers, and their followers mostly did the same.

The first Yasuchika is one maker who really stands out for me in not doing
this. Going through a book of his work, it's remarkable how many times he
would develop something really good and then leave it to go on to something
different and also great. His fabrication skill in both iron and copper
alloy is incredible and he was either a great designer or knew the right
people to work with (or steal from).

Jim

Matt,
The earliest use of "cat scratch" filework on the back of kozuka and kogai that I am aware of is from the middle Muromachi period on Goto Yujo's work (the first generation). I haven't seen it on any older Ko-Kinko, etc., but expect that it could have existed. It's quite common in the Goto school on gold. I have seen illustrations of a couple of Goto examples with shakudo backs with this treatment, but it is rather unusual and might not have been original. You'll note that when the Goto used shakudo and gold together in the decorative patterns you mention, the gold will be filed and the shakudo will be polished.

My guess is that this technique was a way to put a uniform matte finish on the gold so that it wasn't too showy. I doubt that it was for friction, since if it was why not treat the shakudo pieces the same way? Maybe it also helps to make small scratches and dings in the gold surface from wear less obvious. Other ideas?

Lots of machibori (non-Goto) kinko also used this technique on a wide variety of metals, but even there it's most commonly seen on gold. I don't think that it can be used as a definitive kantei point, but it is certainly something to observe and consider. The machibori groups also used those decorative patterns of gold and other metals. Of course some late kinko makers also made some fittings of polished gold, and yes, they are showy indeed.
