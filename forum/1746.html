The joy of tosho tsuba

by Peter A Bleed

Dear Friends,
Let me share a share a minor but satisfying bit of collecting and
what might be a worthwhile tip for tsuba preservation.
Saturday morning I "picked up" a sorry little tosho tsuba. It was
thin - 3 mm - and rather small, but showed a wonderful delamination flaw
and a very sabishii set of 4 'arrow fletch' cut-outs. It clearly is an old
soldier that I'll bet you dates from the later Muromachi period. I rather
like these things, but this one was in "as found condition" which meant it
was terribly dirty and seemingly covered with red rust and some kind of
really noxious pucky. At first I thought that I should just throw it in the
junk box, but decided to give it a bit of 'treatment.'
After a good boiling, it was clean enough to show the laminations,
but it was also entirely bright. In that condition It wouldn't look good
even in the junk box, so I invited it to have a bit of color. It responded
nicely, but it was now rather fresh. Here's the tip I discovered. I used a
bit of boar tusk to "bone" the surface. I used this in the usual way, to
burnish the flat surfaces and clean out the sukashi and the rim. I was VERY
impresses that the tusk seemed to work better than the antler does. It was
not hard enough to scratch the surface so that it left no bright lines. It
also was hard enough to leave not dusty residue on the surface. But it did
a nice job of finishing the surface and quickly turned it a nice dark
blue/black. With a dab of oil, and in less than an hour, this tsuba went
from a questionable buy to a presentable piece I was pleased to hang with
my other swordsmith guards.
I could never have done this sort of thing with a finer or more
valuable tsuba, but in this case I felt I not getting beyond my
capabilities and also that I was preserving an old guard that was 'at
risk.' From now one, I am going to use the harder tusk rather than antler.
Ahhh the joys of collecting


Peter Bleed

Re: [nihontostudy] The joy of tosho tsuba

by gaigin2000

Hi Peter and Ron;

The method of heating the tsuba in a solution of some sort, water being the most common is an old and accepted method of cleaning IRON tsuba. This normally does not change the color but removes the grease and grime that collects on tsuba over time.

I personally prefer to use a soft soap and a toothbrush with warm/hot water to clean the plate.

Unless you over cook it and give the plate a dry look and feel, either is quite an acceptable treatment and cleaning method for iron tsuba.

The "home grown family secrets" of various compounds and tea solutions is a deferent kettle of fish however. Most tsuba collectors have their own methods for this treatment.

Cleaning the active red rust by "boning" the plate and then oiling and carrying in your pocket or rubbing with a soft cloth is also an old accepted method of rejuvenating and healing the iron plate that has been neglected to restore the patina to the plate, and repair the color of the plate.

I have seen a number of over cleaned iron tsuba however. Over cleaning can removed the patina and leave a "shiny" surface to the plate that takes a long time to repatinate unless chemical solutions are used to age the plate. These chemically treated tsuba, that seem to go from a shoe polish black to a brown in color to my eyes never look as good as the original patina that develops with age and use.

These methods are NOT to be tried on kinko or soft metal tsuba, it will destroy the finish of the soft metal plate.

John
