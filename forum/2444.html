Re: [nihontostudy] Musashi's sword

by drjohnplissenden

To inject a slightly more serious note into the smugness of your 'leftie' members ...

Do any members KNOW if left-handedness was extant among early Japanese swordsmen. I have never, personally, seen illustrations of samurai wearing their swords on the right side, and it is not inconceivable that they were trained at an early age to use their swords in the conventional manner. The only advantage in being left handed is, surely, its comparative rarity and strangeness to a right-handed opponent.

Interestingly, I have in my collection (WAY 386.27.ex Gardiner collection) an unsigned, shakudo fuchi-gashira, attributed to Hirata Harunari This has an ishime surface, with takaramono in a shippo of coloured enamels within gold cloissons. The ura surface of the fuchi has had an additional takaramono added in gold lacquer, and this may possibly be to facilitate its wearing on the right side by presenting a more decorative surface. I can think of no other possible explanation for this modification.

John L.

RE: [nihontostudy] Musashi's sword

by robsongord

Hi John,



If you refer to the Tokyo National Museum's publication on Uchigatana
Koshirae, you will note several Tensho period mounts arranged for wear on
the right side. There is some question as to whether or not they were for
wear with the cutting edge down. I don't know of any illustrations showing
right handed wear. I might add that to the best of my knowledge there is no
such thing as an entirely left-handed person. Left-handed individuals either
due to nurture or nature seem to do some things with the right-hand and
others with the left and sometimes able to use both for the same thing.



Gordon

started they were forced to change. FWIW BOb Elder
