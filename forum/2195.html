Re: [nihontostudy] tsuba, waxing, etc...

by rkgatteleport

steven,

first,

DON"T MESS WITH THE RUST INSIDE THE SUKASHI CUTOUTS!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!

Now that I've said that...

I've seen all sorts of tsuba abuse ("brown" wax, various attempts at
repatination, people who have
decided they can do a better job of patination than the original maker
and strip top quality
pieces down and start over, people who just wax them with some magic
compound, oil them, etc.,.) - don't do it -
basically, Jinsoo is absolutely correct - if the surface patina is good,
just store 'em somewhere where they
won't get condensed on and rub them periodically with your hands/a soft
cloth. If its got problems, see below...

the deadly enemy of iron tsuba is red rust - if your piece has this on
it, its a problem. somehow you have to
get this off - some people use horn, but I've found that ivory seems to
be really good for use to scrape this off
while not harming the the brown rust (I think Jim Gilbert has an
excellent article on this in a couple of
places on line...). - often you have to work at it quite a bit - I
sometimes alternate scraping sessions with
washing the piece in warm water with a mild soap and a soft
toothbrush... And again, you only want to
do this ONLY on the outside surfaces of the tsuba, not down in the sukashi.

After you've done that, the revealed old rust regions are often pretty
gnarly, or if the piece is just plain "rough",
you can "pocket polish" the piece - just carry it around
in your pocket for a few weeks (NOT where you carry your keys/change) -
this gives the piece a nice rubbed look that
is hard to get by just marathon rubbing sessions with a cloth...

When you're buying tsuba, always be on the lookout for pieces that have
been "primped" like this - you'll see
people that hide a multitude of sins with various waxes, oils, PAINT (I
got a couple like this - the guy color
matched the patina and jammed the tsuba (!!) - looked real darned good,
too - I was quite happy until I tried to clean
up a rough spot I thought was oil, used some enamel thinner, and -
surprise!...), etc.,. I'd like to think they
just wanted the pieces to look better/didn't know any better and really
weren't thinking fraudulent thoughts, but....

Good Luck,

rkg

Re: tsuba, waxing, etc...

by frkirk

Hi Steven and All
I am fairly new to the group but throught I would throw my 2 cents
in.
On the latter part of your question- if a tsuba has Hozon (or other
respected) papers I woud leave it be- even be cautious about how
much you "pocket polish" it. I have also heard tell of great tsubas
that were made too glossy my over zealous cloth rubbers. As you
say, leave well enough alone and err on the side of caution.
If the tsuba is un-certified and you are unsure as to the
authenticity of its patina, I beleive that dunking it in boiling
water cannot harm it and may remove grime and wax at least. Please
use only plain water for this and make sure the piece is dry ASAP
once it is removed. What you may get from this process will be a
UGLY lookin tsuba since any false patina will be stripped and red
rust, pits, etc will be exposed. While you might be saying to
yourself, "It looked better before" you now can start from ground
zero and nurse it back to health.

Hope this helps
Kirk

steven,

First of all, even papered pieces may have stuff that might need to be
taken care of on
them (red rust, etc.), and I'd guess even some of these end up getting
"primped"
by later owners. Sorry - Papers are a good start, but...

I had alluded to Jim's article about tsuba care - he recommends boiling
water, which works
pretty well.

On paint, I should have been more clear (that's what I get for typing
late at night) - often older
tsubas were lacquered - remmnants of old paint on a tsuba should be
handled with the greatest
care/respect (on really old tsuba you'll see the paint form this
funky/cool amalgum with the surface
patina) - you have to proceed carefully (or just leave well enough
alone) when you have old
paint like that - I had a tosho once I nicknamed "rusty crusty" because
there was a fairly thick
uncared for patina on it - BUT there was also a fair amount of what
appeared to be original
lacquer, so I left it well enough alone - while the tsuba would probably
have looked a whole
lot better/sold for more if I had just attacked it a little with the
ivory/rubbing, it didn't seem
right since it would have nuked the lacquer as well... The real answer
is experience... You need
to go and look at and study (and take good pictures of if you're
motivated) a bunch of good tsuba...

On "primped" tsuba, an amazing number of tsuba in the US appear to have
gotten the "treatment" -
I've pretty much stopped buying pieces from people in the US who I don't
know because of this -
I've been burned enough so I can usually recognize its been jacked with,
but I get tired of sending
pieces back - but you'll even get stuff from Japan occasionally that has
been "helped"... (comments
about how many people don't seem to understand white balance on digital
cameras deleted).

Gotta run,

rkg

Steve,

-What he said.-
(I concur).

Read Gilbert article (on his website) on cleaning
tsuba. If/when you first try to clean a tsuba, go
slow. I overdid one of my first ones a bit, and have
spent 2 years promoting healthy repatination & the
occassional rub. On a good dense Tosho tsuba, this is
an agonizingly slow process. Many people don't have
the patience.
When doing the horn/ivory cleaning, but to practice
with a $50 tsuba off ebay that has just a little rust.
It will get you accustomed to the feel of doint the
work.

Re: [nihontostudy] tsuba layers

by yamaimojg

Rich,
What you're seeing inside the nakago ana appears to be the result of the hammering to change the size of the nakago ana. That's fairly common. Always try to confirm any "layering" in the other sukashi openings and rim. This iron looks quite homogeneous in the other openings in the photos.

Shodai Nishigaki Kanshiro can indeed show some loose laminations in his plate that can be visible in the sukashi or even as delaminations on the surface. I can't think of any other Higo makers that have that. Your guard is in Nishigaki style, but is pretty late. "Kodai Higo."


Jim
