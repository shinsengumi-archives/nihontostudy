by pbleed

Enore,
I want to second Gordon's advice. I would urge you not to alter your sword
in the way you are describing. If you want a grooved blade order one or
find one. At the very least you should not do anything until you have had
your blade well shinsa'd.
Still, your question does point up a basic feature of the world of
nippon-to. The values and standards of the various people involved in
Japanese swords are diverse. What is absolutely "wrong" to some is "just
fine" to others. Most of us base our actions on our views of aesthetics and
a sense of historical preservation. There are other values out there and
knowing that helps to understand some of the things we see about swords.
When I was first in Japan I happened to meet a number of sword craftsmen
rather than collectors. Of course this was interesting (heck, it was
wonderful), but it may have given me a skewed view.
1. The first "smith" I met ( and he was an important artist-and a
wonderful person) was re-tempering some old swords when I visited his
forge.
2. An important kinko proudly showed me his portfolio that included some
very excellent "umegane" work he had done. He assured me that "no one could
see" the fillings he had put into some seriously flawed blades.
3. During a road trip with a fellow who had recently completed his
polishing apprenticeship, we took a long extra trip to let his teacher see
the grooves he was carving on a tanto. He was proud of his work and his
master approved. When I asked why he was doing it, he said "the smith
should have put the grooves in."
These men are not crooks or villains. Indeed, I think they are great
artists and skilled students, but they operate with a different set of
values.
You certainly can find craftsmen who will do what you ask of them, but one
thing is clear. You will only be able to cut grooves in your sword once. I
urge you to proceed with caution.
Peter

Yes and no. It was originally made to be used. If it survives a
certain passage of time, and if of sufficient quality to begin with,
its primary identity passes from "tool" to "art object". One might
quibble over what this time might be. In my opinion, any good nihonto

with or put at any substantial risk. Others may disagree, but as
strongly as I (and I suspect others) feel about this, and as is
typical of conflicting basic values, discussion is prone to get very
heated very quickly, and is not something I desire to pursue beyond
this stated opinion.

Hello Enore, To place bohi to lighten an old sword means the sword is
too heavy for you. It was done often with conversion of tachi to
uchigatana or katana, to balance the sword. Hearing about the koto
being carved makes me shudder. To carve bohi for better whistling
sounds is a shallow reason to alter something that can not be undone.
You will never use this sword for what it was intended (battle) so
use an iaito with any horimono you wish to make whistles and leave
historical irreplaceable objects alone. John

Re: Bo-hi

by peonies_waves

Hello Enore,

A few more thoughts to consider. If your sword is a shinto sword, it
was almost for certain carried by a samurai. The question then
becomes, if this sword was good enough for a real samurai without a
bo-hi, how is it that it is suddenly not good enough without a bo-hi
now?

Additionally, isn't cutting a 'striking event', and if it is, isn't
final momentum a critical factor in addition to cutting motion? And,
isn't momentum made up of mass X velocity? Therefore, by removing
metal for a bo-hi won't that reduce the mass, thereby reduce the
striking force of the cut? While you could possibly increase the
velocity with a lighter sword to compensate some, the best result
would come from a combination of both.

Historically, the reason for bo-hi is to reduce battle fatigue
(lactic acid build up) from making cut after cut for an extended
period of time.


regards,
Franco

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Bo-hi

by soshuju

Enore -
Your passion for authentic practice is comendable. Your teacher it
seems has done something similar in pursuit of her idea of perfect
form. Most anyone here would not put in a bohi for practice or to
remove a flaw without serious study and long consideration. A skilled
man could probably put in a hi that was difficult to detect as ato-
bori. However many of us here have seen good hi cut on old swords and
have learned to look for them as a sign of age or worse; hidden
flaws. If the sword came in shirasaya someone thought enough of it to
try to preserve it as is.
You asked for opinions and we would recommend that you not alter the
sword without more study, more study of swords, and more study of
swordsmanship. Batto-do is destructive no matter how good the
swordsman one should never cut with antique swords. Iaido believe it
or not is destructive, constant practice dulls blades, scuffs
polishes and eats up koshirae. Trust me I know. Restoring a sword
that has been practised with can be a tricky process, one where fatal
flaws may come to light. Someone restored it once, polished it once
and put it in shirasaya for protection once, long ago. The hand of
our elders is apparent in that saya listen to what it is telling you.
In two years time you may find you prefer a longer sword or a
heavier sword or one with deeper curve. Some folks take ten years or
more before they settle on the style of blade that best suits their
practise. Practise hard but proceed with caution you'll be glad you did.
Thomas C Helm
6 Dan Muso Jiki Den Eishin Ryu

Enore,

As pointed out in other posts, this is not a personal attack, but you
did ask for opinions and I think they have been unanimous.
If you need a blade for cutting, then why not commision a modern
shinsakuto made to your specifications?
These swords were made that way for a reason, and I feel that saying
it would be better with a hi, is like saying that you think a Picasso
needs more red, and then considering adding it. Even if it would be
better..it is not how the artist intended it to be. The swords were
once made for battle...but now they are universally considered art.
We collectors are always striving to get swords that are as close as
possible to how they were originally manufactured. Yes...suriage is
tolerated...but not prefered. It is accepted because it cannot be
undone. No-one here would have an old sword shortened because they
think it would look better that way.
Adding hi may not be noticable, but it is not the chosen action by
anyone except yourself..and since we are preserving these swords for
future generations...I just feel it is selfish to change it. The next
generation may want it shortened..the next will add a horimono..and
after a few generations what was a nice Shinto sword becomes another
back-of-the-cupboard relic. None of this can be undone, and the fact
that others may do it does not make it the correct action.

Just my 2c on behapf of future generations looking for original Nihonto.

Brian

Hi Enore,

I rarely post here, but your message makes me feel obliged to respond.

I can agree (to some extent) with an opinion that swords were made to
be used rather than for appreciation only. However, as you seem to
practice sword arts, think about that: various ancient swordsmanship
schools, methods, techniques were also created for active use
(particularly - in battle), not for any sort of appreciation or
samurai-style fitness exercises. However it is considered a horrible
misconduct (among serious practitioners) to change the authentic
techniques for a reason of 'adapting it for oneself' or even thinking
that 'certain technique is meant to be this way, not the way old
teachers were doing it (for centuries)'. I would say this principle
can well be applied to old swords: while created to be used, they are
not for change (unless in damaged condition) or unless your skills
become equal or higher than skills of old masters.

Practice sword supplies are plentiful at the moment. You may even
order one to be made _exactly_ how you want it to be. I would agree
with others: please please please leave the sword unchanged. If it's
too heavy/too long/too short - it's just not for you (for your
practice). If you feel like training with old nihonto (to be frank I
don't support this idea and neither did different sensei I met), find
the sword which fits your hands as is, without change. I personally
do sometimes unsheath one of my swords to do a couple of slow cuts in
the air to 'get this feeling back' ;-) But I would never consider
using it on a regular basis (or any basis) in training - well made
iaito is enough.

Another reason it's not recommended is safety. Regardless how well
preserved it looks, you simply don't know the history of this sword.
As somebody mentioned earlier it could have been treated with
some 'cosmetic surgery' or even just have a natural flaw which didn't
appear (yet) because the sword was _never_ used. By the way, adding
bo-hi would only increase that risk.

Kind Regards,
Stan

Dear Enore,

John Yumato wrote; " The Samurai Sword has long been considered the
symbol of the spirit of old Japan. It has been said to be the
embodiment of the Samurai's code, the expression of his steel
discipline, unswerving devotion, and peerless skill. It is, in
addition, one of the most outstanding examples of Japan's traditon of
highly skilled craftsmanship. The tireless efforts of hereditary
artisans, who's sole efforts in life was the achievement of perfection
in their craft, the Samurai sword is indeed a beautiful work of art as
well as a formidable weapon."

Your Shin-to sword may very well be as much as 400 yrs. old. It has
remained just as the Swordsmith intended it to be through "Many"
owners. All of whom did not feel the need to alter it. Yes, you are
the present owner, to do with it as you wish. However, "All" of us
are only temporary "Custodian's" of these artifacts. I would
encourage you leave it "as is." Allow the many future owners to study
and enjoy it in original unalterd form.

I myself am even reluctant to have a sword polished which could be
considered "sorely in need." The two deflected blade marks in the
mune would be gone. The two "knicks" in the ha which may have been
retaliatory strokes would also be gone.....

Good luck,

Dave
