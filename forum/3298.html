Tsuba care question

by neobliviscar

List-

Between intervals of carrying old iron tsuba in the
back jeans pocket to give them a slow polish, it has
been traditionally advised to give them a rub with
flannel. Over lots and lots of time, the flannel thing
works... very slowly.

_My main question_: what are the pros and cons of
using non-dyed (aka homespun) denim /instead of
flannel/ to rub the faces of the tsuba?

_Secondary question_: In jeans pocket, it seems the
mimi takes on too much polish while the face gets only
slightly polished. What are the opinions of aesthetic
about this? Should degree of Mimi polish and face
polish be equal, or would Hagihara and other Japanese
authorities recommend more polish on the mimi and less
wear on the face especially the seppa dai area, as
would happen to a mounted tsuba over time as it rubbed
against the silk/cotton of its owner's kimono?

Curran

Boy, you are getting specific here. When I think about these concerns I sometimes wonder about us modern concervators. I think we "think" too much. We know what should "not" be done to iron tsuba and as for what "should be done," well, I think it is just common sense to "handle" them to enhance their "aging" and otherwise, let them age without making such a fuss over them. Re: Tsuba care question

by nihontochicken

--- In nihontostudy@yahoogroups.com, "Mark Jaillet" wrote:
> On a slightly serious note, are any of you aware of a book that
will tell
> how to actually pronounce nihonto related words? Or anywhere's on
the web?

Vowels are fairly simple, as follows:

"a" = "ah" as in bob, got, malt, paw, shawl, taught, thought.
"e" = "eh" as in bed, get, melt, peg, shell, threat.
"i" = "ee" as in bead, greet, me, pea, she, think.
"o" = "oh" as in bode, goat, mow, pour, sew, though.
"u" = "oo" as in boot, goof, moot, new, pool, sue, shoe, suit, through.
"ai" = "uy" as in bite, buy, eye, height, my, pie, rye, sign, thigh.
"ei" = "ay" as in bait, bay, gate, may, pail, shale, they.

"g" is always hard, as in "get", never soft, as in "germ".

There are some trickier parts, like "ko" + "katana" = "kogatana", and
"Matsushita" is pronounced "Mat-soosh-ta" (no shit, er, "sheet"!), but
the above general rules will get you through 90%+ of the battle.
Actually quite simple - look again at the various English spellings
that yield the same sound, depending on the word. Note that in
English, for instance, "dead" is not pronounced the same as "meat".
Many English pronunciations have to be individually learned, unlike
most Japanese words. Good fortune!

NC
