by robsongord

Hi Ron,



The method of attaching menuki to the handle went from the male-female posts
or inyôkon that passed through the handle and the nakago fastening the
menuki to each other to chikara-gane. These inyôkon posts continued until
the late Muromachi period into the Momoyama period. They are also seen on
generally high class works for the mid to late Edo period; however, they no
longer went all the way through the tsuka and basically worked as the newer
chikara-gane do. As noted, strength and metal is the literal translation,
but more accurately I would call them reinforced/strengthened tabs of
typically copper. On an added note, at least the first three generations of
the main line Goto masters are said to have only used inyôkon posts and
menuki without them will typically be attributed to Ko-Goto rather than one
of the main guys. I expect we will be catching up with many on the list in
San Francisco next month. I am certainly looking forward to it.



Gordon

Hi Ron,



Grapes are a motif that was brought in on the silk road and are of Greek
origin, I would suspect. The famous set of Asuka period bronze statues of
Yakushi nyorai and Gakko and Nikko at Yakushi-ji in Nara have an early
example of the grape motif on the base of the Yakushi nyorai statue, so we
are talking middle 500s or so. Squirrels may be included in the motif, but I
cant recall right off hand. Thus, there is a definite Buddhist connection.
The motif shows up in sword fittings rather early and I believe there are
pieces by Joshin or at least around that time with the motif. Ko-Kinko
workers produced it, and there are also some Ko-Mino examples, if memory
serves. As you move into the Edo period, it seems to me that the motif was
not as popular. Certainly from the mid to late Edo period you do not see it
very much. If I can remember, I will see what some of my other sources have
to say. I did see your earlier message about this, but just didnt jump at
it.



Sorry we will miss you in SF, but we can catch up at the Daitoken-ishi.



Gordon
