Re: [nihontostudy] Followup on Owari Kinko Tsuba and Norisuke

by neobliviscar

Alan,

The tsuba is very thick and rich shakudo. It is very
heavy. The gold wire used is also very heavy guage,
set extremely deep into channels. It has retained
about 99.9% of the inlay, but there is a small spot on
the mimi where I can see how deep the channels run and
the gold wire extends all the way into it.

According to Haynes and one or two other sources, the
Norisuke line did work in Kinko sometimes.

I am not saying with 100% certainty that this is a
Norisuke, but there is a similar tsuba out there by
Norisuke and the more that I look at other tsuba by
the Norisuke family, the more I see the same design
elements over and over again. Leaf shape. Flower petal
engraving. Flower engraving. General composition. Very
much like a painting, the great number of details
evident in a soft metal heavily carved piece like this
seem to me a signature. There is some variation, like
there is a very similar 1st gen Norisuke example with
near identical leaves, but the main vein of the leaf
is done in a single track of gold vs the double vein I
have on this leaf.
I like to figure things out before I submit them to
shinsa, and sometimes if I am happy enough with an
answer and intend to keep a piece- I don't submit to
shinsa. I'd be surprised if I actually got a
'Norisuke' call on this, but as a newbie-intermediate
having something like this seem to click into place
has been very satisfying. Yet I may be absolutely
wrong! So if I can stand on someone's shoulder and see
further into this.... I welcome the view.

If you want to take a crack at the other tsuba that
has been a challenge for me, I will dig that photo out
too and post it. This 'Norisuke' has been the more
interesting piece and horribly difficult to photograph
given the highly reflective shakudo and gold.
Photography is not a strong skill for me, yet the
devil of a good identification is also in the detail
of the other one.

Curran

Alan,

The tsuba is very thick and rich shakudo. It is very
heavy. The gold wire used is also very heavy guage,
set extremely deep into channels. It has retained
about 99.9% of the inlay, but there is a small spot on
the mimi where I can see how deep the channels run and
the gold wire extends all the way into it.

According to Haynes and one or two other sources, the
Norisuke line did work in Kinko sometimes.

I am not saying with 100% certainty that this is a
Norisuke, but there is a similar tsuba out there by
Norisuke and the more that I look at other tsuba by
the Norisuke family, the more I see the same design
elements over and over again. Leaf shape. Flower petal
engraving. Flower engraving. General composition. Very
much like a painting, the great number of details
evident in a soft metal heavily carved piece like this
seem to me a signature. There is some variation, like
there is a very similar 1st gen Norisuke example with
near identical leaves, but the main vein of the leaf
is done in a single track of gold vs the double vein I
have on this leaf.
I like to figure things out before I submit them to
shinsa, and sometimes if I am happy enough with an
answer and intend to keep a piece- I don't submit to
shinsa. I'd be surprised if I actually got a
'Norisuke' call on this, but as a newbie-intermediate
having something like this seem to click into place
has been very satisfying. Yet I may be absolutely
wrong! So if I can stand on someone's shoulder and see
further into this.... I welcome the view.

If you want to take a crack at the other tsuba that
has been a challenge for me, I will dig that photo out
too and post it. This 'Norisuke' has been the more
interesting piece and horribly difficult to photograph
given the highly reflective shakudo and gold.
Photography is not a strong skill for me, yet the
devil of a good identification is also in the detail
of the other one.
