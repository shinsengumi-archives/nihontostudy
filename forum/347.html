First, I want to thank Jim for posting these pictures for me! i just
figured out how to get signed on! {:) As I mentioned to Jim, the
miscellaneous attachments on this section of pole (14 inches long) in
my opinion are all functional. I believe as Jim mentioned that the
barbs on the "quadraspike" (how's that for a word!) are for breaking
the blade of an opponent. I also think that the tightly woven fiber
bands on both sides of it would act as shock absorbers..they would
flex under impact and then spring back to their original position. I
imagine the impact of a blade against another weapon would be
considerable. I think also that with this heavy a blade, the pole
would need quite a bit of reinforcement and all these fittings would
definately supply that. I believe the section behind the large tsuba
would have had same' panels and tsukamaki wrap for the left (forward)
hand. This nagamaki is very plain in all respects and in my opinion
is probably a Shinshinto copy of an older fighting piece not some
late ceremonial decoration. The Shinshinto period was a time
of "experimental archaeology" in that the Japanese were trying to
rediscover their earlier sword making styles and abilities and I
think this may be one outcome of that study.
