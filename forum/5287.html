Re: [nihontostudy] Kyo-kinko

by yamaimojg

Kyo-kinko is non-Goto (non-Mino, etc.) kinko work from after the Momoyama period. It is often applied to work that is in Goto style but is not Goto main or sideline work. Edo-kinko used to be used similarly for work in the style of Edo region machibori kinko schools, but lately this is generally lumped as "Kyo-kinko" as well. So it truly is a grab bag, but the problem (especially with menuki) is that there is so much Edo period kinko work with no unique character to it that makes it possible to give a more specific attribution.

BTW, generic Edo period kinko work of lower quality than Kyo-kinko is attributed to Kyo-kanagushi.

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Kyo-kinko

by yamaimojg

Ron,
I didn't say it was hard to date Kyo-kinko, just impossible to assign to a group. There aren't different attributions for the different parts of the Edo period. The NTHK does assign a period within Edo for every fitting or koshirae paper:

初期 Shoki (the beginning)

前期 Zenki (the first part)

中期 Chuki (the middle)

後期 Koki (late)

末期 Baki (the end)


The NBTHK does not.

I can't even guess at a group on the tombo menuki, and that is the way it is with the Kyo-kinko attribution. I didn't see them in Tampa, but based on the photos they look like late Edo work.

Agreed with Gordon's earlier comment that there are plenty of early solid gold menuki, including Ko-mino and Ko-Kinko work in addition to Goto. The recent NBTHK AB display and lecture at Tampa included several pairs of solid gold mainline Goto menuki from the Muromachi and early Edo periods.

Jim

Kyo-kinko is non-Goto (non-Mino, etc.) kinko work from after the Momoyama
period. It is often applied to work that is in Goto style but is not Goto
main or sideline work. Edo-kinko used to be used similarly for work in the
style of Edo region machibori kinko schools, but lately this is generally
lumped as "Kyo-kinko" as well. So it truly is a grab bag, but the problem
(especially with menuki) is that there is so much Edo period kinko work with
no unique character to it that makes it possible to give a more specific
attribution.

BTW, generic Edo period kinko work of lower quality than Kyo-kinko is
attributed to Kyo-kanagushi.

Jim

Milt,
As I've explained before, while the motif is the same, the workmanship of your tsuba is not identical. Given that it was unsigned and had no unique characteristics, there was no more justification to assign it to a "den" than to assign every dragon or shishi Goto copy to den Goto. Good workmanship and materials will get a slight point bonus even if the work is not distinctive enough to get a group attribution.

In Edo period kinko and also iron sukashi there are many very similar works with signatures from unrelated groups. Fittings makers were turning out what would sell.
