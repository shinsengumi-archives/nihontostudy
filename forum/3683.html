Re: [nihontostudy] Nobuiye Mei

by Barry Thomas

Try Robert Burawoy's little book:

"The Picture Book of Old Tsuba"
500 copies printed in 1983

Up the back, under "SIGNATURES, PROBLEMS, HEADACHES" he begins with the comment:

"We are now going to examine the first signatures, and people with immense reputation too..."

He then examines case by case the main people. Of Nobuiye he notes:

"NOBUIYE: Here we are on a little sounder ground. The accepted notion is that there are two REAL Nobuiye, and a bunch of later provincial artists using genuinely the same name (plus a LOT of fakes). They lived in Momoyama period. The two examples shown here if not the best, seem real, I do not know of any other acceptable in the West. The first Nobuiye is credited with one great quality, which the tsuba reproduced here has but probably does not show on the picture: An extraordinary surface reminding the glaze of a Raku pottery."

A nice little book for one so ignorant as I...

Best regards,

Barry Thomas
(Melbourne, Australia)

Re: [nihontostudy] Nobuiye Mei

by yamaimojg

Pete,
I can't think of anything in English better than the Tsuba Geijutsu-Ko by Toigoye and Haynes. I'm sure that there is further commentary in Joly's translations of Akiyama republished in the NCJSC newsletters some years ago, but I don't have them organized or indexed.

Nobuie Tsuba by Nakamura is as good a picture sources as any, at least for having large numbers of examples to compare. Each mei is attributed to a type in the text of the oshigata section. The attribution is usually at or near the end of the text (lower left portion).
放れ銘 hanare-mei, 太字銘 futoji- mei, 詰り銘 sumari-mei, 左傾銘 sakei-mei and 甲胃銘 katchu-mei are ones I saw frequently when I flipped through the book.

In the past there were something like 8 or more categories of mei in use. Most signatures these days are lumped into futoji-mei and hanare-mei. There's quite a bit of variability among accepted examples in each category though, so things are still not simple. Wakayama shows some details about the different signatures in the first volume of his Toso Kodogu Koza. No doubt there are other comparisons in the various survey books.

It's a difficult area.

Re: [nihontostudy] Nobuiye Mei

by yamaimojg

It's a major deviation. One of these wide-spaced mei guards was submitted to shinsa in Chicago and failed as gimei. I don't know if any examples have been accepted as valid. Personally, I don't see why anyone would sign like that if attempting to deceive. The workmanship of these looks early Edo period, so it may be the signature of another "other" Nobuie working then.

That kind of wide spaced signature is rare for tsuba in general. I have seen an Umetada signature like that, but that's about it.

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Nobuiye Mei

by davidzincavage

Another reference:



AKIYAMA, KYUSAKU, Nakamura Kakudaifu Nobuie Tsuba shu [Collection of Rubbings of Nobuie's Sword Guards Belonging to Kakudaifu Nakamura], Tôkyô:Nanjinsha, 1926 (First edition)
-- 150 pp. printed on double fold paper. Text in Japanese.
Rubbings of Nobuie tsuba from the Nakamura collection, many of which were lost or destroyed during WWII. Akiyama, the pre-eminent scholar of tsuba of the early part of the 20th century, assembled this collection of rubbings.

--reprinted with supplemental material, Tôkyô:Token Shunju Shinbunsha, 1981.

David Zincavage
