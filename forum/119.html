Onderwerp: Re: hizento skins


Hi Matt,



The question is "Why do hizento have such thin skins ?"
- Matt


They all don't.
Some do - the kobuse construction (lower quality) where better steel is
used on the outer edge, but only in a thin layer as this was cheaper.
They can certainly have a very thin outer edge.

The sanmai construction are the better swords (Tadayoshi 1, 2, 3, 4
(maybe), 8, Masahiro 1 etc. and they have thicker steel. The better
steel is used as the core.

I am of the opinion that the Tadayoshi used imported steel as it was
readily available. That maybe why the hada is so tight. Now, how
expensive this was I have no idea - but if it was expensive, then they
could have used it in a thin outer layer (kobuse) - saving costs. The
other factor to consider is that because the hada is so tight and
flawless in Hizen blades, when you polish through it into the core
steel, it then looks awful. In other Schools you probably wouldn't
notice the difference as the outer steel hada can be coarse as well.
There might only be a slight colour variation.

Re: [nihontostudy] Iron Sand

by Rick Bliss

Bob and Folk -

As far as exactly where in Japan the black sand beaches and stream beds are
found, I'll have to punt. But generically, the source for tamahagane is
just that, a type of black sand. Most all beach sands contain some black
sand consisting of magnetite, chemically Fe3O4, containing iron in both the
+2 and +3 oxidation states. As the name implies, it is magnetic, and simply
by running a magnet through beach sand, you will collect the magnetite
grains. A great concentrating method that was likely not available to the
Japanese before cheap magnets became available in the modern age. They used
a water flow ripple method instead (sluice box), not as efficient. I
imagine that the traditional Japanese used sand from the few places where
magnetite was a major fraction of the sand total, giving the bulk sand an
overall "black" look. But where those beaches and stream beds were exactly,
I'll have to leave to someone else to tell. Kapp does mention Shimane
Province as the major source of tamahagane (80% of total) from the late
Muromachi onward, but doesn't specify the koto era sources, except that they
were scattered. Note: Kapp also indicates the source of tamahagane as
Fe2O3, but this is hematite, a red color as the name implies. It is indeed
a good iron source, but not what was available to the Japanese, I believe.

Re: Iron Sand

by nihontochicken

Bob (and Folk) -

I don't know whether the Kalapana Black Sand Beach is magnetite or
not (or whether it's even still there - it was supposedly run over by
a fresh magma flow a few years back - I was there about thirty years
ago, but wasn't interested in its magnetic properties at the time).
But a typical US beach has quite a bit of magnetite in the sand. A
kid's magnet will net you a small vial full in a few tens of
minutes. A serious Edmund Scientific magnet should get you enough in
an hour to make a sword, if you could figure out how to smelt it.
I've thought about making an electromagnet to collect it (better than
a permanent magnet because you can turn it off and drop the magnetite
you've picked up, rather than try to pull it off the magnet by
hand). But, again, what would I do with it? It has to be heated in
a reducing atmosphere to get iron/steel out of it. Anyone have an
idea for a mini-tartara? BTW, an amateur US swordsmith I spoke with
years ago (since lost contact) did make his own tamahagane, and
forged a small blade from it, and showed same to Yoshihara at the
Solvang Knife Show. He told me that Yoshindo immediately recognized
the difference in tetsu, and asked the US smith where he got his
steel. I have heard from other sources that the source of the steel
(tamahagane, mill, electrolytic, wootz, etc.) does indeed result in
differences in the tetsu visible to the experienced viewer.
