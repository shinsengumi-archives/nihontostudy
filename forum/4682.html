Re: Masahisa

by drjohnplissenden

Dear Elliott

Your general question re the use of casting tecniques for the
production of tsuba, although probably asked in all innocence, opens a
whole 'can of worms'.

There is no doubt that casting was accepted as a skilled form of
metalworking and was used to produce cast bronze vessels and mirrors,
and iron kettles. Indeed, a cast kagami is one of the Three Sacred
Regalia of Japan. Kagami-shi tsuba, supposedly made in the pre-Edo
period by mirror makers, may also be of extremely high quality and it
is not illogical to suppose that many soft-metal tsuba may have
initially been cast.

But the casting of iron tsuba is, for some reason, very frowned upon by
collectors. I am personally convinced that the vast majority of Namban
tsuba are cast and the poor quality of many of these makes this very
easy to detect with the use, simply, of a bright light and x30
magnification. The ease with which casting can be identified clearly
depends upon the amount of hand finishing which was utilised afterwards.

Many people will justify the casting of Manban tsuba as being expected
because of their poor quality, but I believe that casting may have been
used far more generally than is accepted. It should be a simple matter
to confirm this, but such examinations would need to be carried out on
tsuba of accepted high quality in order to avoid the a priori excuse
of 'poor quality'.

White cast iron is not a suitable metal for casting, but the
carburisation of grey cast iron to produce a malleable cast iron,
which is ideal for casting small artefacts such as tsuba, is a process
that has been known in Japan for several hundred years.

As to whether the tsuba illustrated in your photo album is cast or not,
I would hesitate to offer a firm opinion without a hands-on
examination. But my first impression is the absence of any clean,
sharp chisel-work - I have my suspicions!

Regards, John L.

Dear Rick

You ask for evidence that the 'lost wax' method of casting was known to
the Japanese and you are, of course, quite correct in your assertion
that the typical Namban tsuba could only have been cast by using this
tecnique. Evidence exists in Japan of the extensive use of both
the 'closed form' and the 'lost form' methods of casting - the former
being known in China as early as the Zhou dynasty (1122-249 BC) and the
latter being introduced there 'relatively late'.

Many Japanese artefacts could only have been produced by the use of
this tecnique, and kagami are but one example of this. The detail of
many cast objects, especially in bronze, could not have been acheived
any other way.

Thus my belif in the casting of Namban tsuba presumes the use of
a 'lost form' method. The naked-eye evidence of casting; the knowledge
that this form as casting was widely known in Japan; and the acceptance
that no other contemporanerous method of casting could have produced
these artefacts seem to support this presumption.

I would be interested to hear your further comments.

Regards, John L.

Re: Masahisa

by drjohnplissenden

How encouraging that the noticeboard has several members who are not
blindly prejudiced against Namban tsuba as a group, even if many of
these are lacking in both design and execution. I remember my
distress some years ago when, upon being informed of my research on
the group, a senior member of the To-ken scene in GB replied Oh Dear,
but he supposed that somebody had to do it!

The tsuba that Ron describes as being 'made in two pieces' is
probably one of many tsuba which were made in the mid-C19 for sale to
foreigners in Nagasaki. These were made in two non-contiguous halves
that, when joined together gave the impression of undercutting. In
the better of these they were joined by a mimi; in the others they
were simply soldered together.

The Hozon Namban tsuba posted by Richard is very nice. The gold
sleeves that he comments upon appear to be inserts intended to tidy
up the irregularities in the design left by the later addition (or
should I say subtraction) of the ryo-hitsu.

Please keep them coming.

Regards, John L.
