Re: [nihontostudy] tsuba balance

by moses_becerra

Jim and all,

The fact that a tsuba is larger or smaller may play some small role in the use of a sword, but not in cutting. I really can not imagine samurai sitting around discussing the weight of a tsuba and how it affects cutting, unless it is as heavy as a brick. A tsuba offers some protection for your hand. The only exception that I know about is that a very long sword is extremely difficult to draw if you have a large tsuba. The tsuba makes too much contact with your wrist. So a small tsuba is best. Now, what I have seen is a handle that has metal inlayed at the end of the tsuka past the nakago. I have a shin shinto katana that is 32" long. It sounds very awkward to use but actually it is made in a interesting way. The blade is slender and has fumbari and a strong curvature, also the stuba is leather with black lacquer over it and the tsuka has some kind of steel inlayed it at the end. It is very noticeable. The fact that the tsuba has almost no weight and the tsuka has extra weight added in the at the end of it makes this blade very nice to cut with. At 32" long it actually feel light. I hope to write an article on this sword with pictures in the future when I have more time.

Regards,
Moses Becerra

Re: [nihontostudy] tsuba balance

by soshuju

Jim-
In my mind Edo period tameshi giri was a very ritualized
activity. A whole series of fees had to be paid, divination done, the
officials involved had to agree on a schedule, lunch had to be
ordered and an agenda that wouldn't allow for any embarrassment set
up. It wasn't like a carwash, where you drop off your sword and come
back later. It had to be done in just such a way. My understanding
has always been that the testers used special "testing tsuka" and no
tsuba, and probably made a big show of putting it on. I am inclined
to see thick tsuba as fashion and personal taste and not practical
(though surely they were sturdier than say kyosukashi pieces).
I am curious about your thoughts on the use of and changes in
the tsuba. Early kenukigata tachi and ito maki tachi have thick soft
metal or kawa tsuba, many of these are seen in the catalogs. What
mounts do we see the big perfectly circular Kokatchushi and Kotosho
tsuba on? In your minds eye what king of mount do you see these on?I
could see these large rather thin plates being useful say on
horseback, when blade to blade combat would have occurred less often.
But as Richard says once we move from tachi to katana, don't the
tsuba have smaller diameters and become a bit thicker? what is the
progression as you see it?
Kyo-sukashi ostensibly was the rage in the capitol, where
artistic merit surely held sway, these pieces made for the elite may
have seen battle but were seen as worthy of preservation early on. I
am thinking the workingmans' sword guard would be more along the
lines of a Hoan, very simple iron and were used up or even tossed out
in favor of the latest fad.
-t

by yamaimojg

Tom,
Thanks for the reply on tameshi giri. I have an old friend staying with me and haven't had much time to devote to the list.

The only koshirae mounted with Ko Katchushi or Ko Tosho tsuba that I have seen in person or in illustrations were typical mid to late Muromachi katana or wakizashi. I have not seen an early tachi mounted with these guards.

Most of the early tachi koshirae that have been preserved seem to have been for formal wear and these are fitted with relatively elaborate soft metal guards. There are many practical tachi preserved at Oyamazumi, and some others in existence elsewhere, and they are mounted with nerikawa tsuba. It seems that early tosho and katchushi guards were intended for practical use, but it is not clear to me that they were ever mounted on tachi despite some references claiming this. I'll try to get back to the possible use of some of these tsuba on naginata or nagimaki, but I don't think that the majority were mounted on pole arms.

Reading ahead through the email list, Gordon is of course correct about Sasano's interpretation of the picture scrolls and his construction of a chronological sequence from extant iron guards. I don't think that any of these very early katana or koshirae still exist. I have to wonder why the tsuba still exist and why so many of the supposed early examples have wakizashi-style kozuka ana that are considered to have been added later.

It's late and I'm out of steam again... more to follow when time permits.
Jim

Ko katchushi and ko tosho on tachi

by Arnold Frenzel

Hi.
When one considers the design orientation of some of those tsuba it would
seems that must have been carried on tachi. On the issue of the lack of
confirmatory evidence, I used to point out to students, when the lecture
got to methodology, that the absence of evidence is not the same thing as
the evidence of absence. Further there are many katchushi and tosho that
would seem from the design orientation to be intended to be carried with
katana koshirae, and undoubtedly most were carried that way, however some
of them, perhaps the largest ones, were probably used on no-dachi for
which a katana orientation would probably make sense if they were used for
over the shoulder carry most of the time.

Re: [nihontostudy] Ko katchushi and ko tosho on tachi

by yamaimojg

I happened to visit the Metropolitan Museum of Art today to see their new show "Great Waves, Chinese Themes in the Art of Korea and Japan." While not a particularly successful show, there are some very good paintings, including an interesting set of newly acquired handscrolls from the Nanbokucho period on display that included some apparently accurate battle sequences.

Titled "Aki no yonagawa monogatari emaki" (Illustrated tale for a long autumn night), there are clear depictions of wakizashi-sized swords being worn edge-upward under the sash. These are worn by unarmored and otherwise unarmed monks, lightly armored footsoldiers also armed with naginata, and even by a few elaborately armored mounted and unmounted warriors sporting tachi and bow or naginata.

These "uchigatana" have no no tsuba. The tachi appear to be mounted with typical mokko-gata nerikawa tsuba of the period. The lower status individuals carry naginata without tsuba and the higher carry naginata with rather small guards. There are no "O-dachi" depicted.

The drawing of the arms and armor appears to be accurate enough to be worth study. It is of course only one data point, but it is one that I haven't seen exhibited before.

Jim
