Re: [nihontostudy] cleaned tsuba

by dirkamarshall

I buy the bottom keys for cleaning large spaces of flat stuff. the long skinny pieces that I think are next to the black keys for detail work, the black keys to make cool little racks to show guards off. I start with rusty crappy rusty iron tsubas to lean technique worked on better rusty guards as I learned the routine, then went to inlaid tsubas, its fun and to me rewarding. You can go thru a bunch of keys cleaning a tsubas as they split often though. It also takes many hours of work.

Dirk

Re: [nihontostudy] cleaned tsuba

by Barry Thomas

Dirk,

Congrats, that looks incredible!! You've also made the patina uniform - how did
you do that?? I have a restoration experience too that is now 30 years old. I
grit my teeth every time I look at it. I'm happy to put these forward as (a)
what not to do and to tell my story and (b) make a comment about restoring iron
tsuba. Dirk, if I send you some images can you put them up on the same
website???

The story. I had a daisho tsuba (now elsewhere) that had "crusty rust" on the
iron surface. I had read Captain E Johns account (name?? - a very early article
in English) about cleaning and "had a go". I used a scalpel very carefully (I
thought) to shave the rust off. I also 'picked' rust out of the pits left. An
American collector visiting Australia suggested it would have been better to
clean the rust off by, rather than dig rust out of the pits, to LEAVE THE
SURFACE OF THE RUST LEVEL WITH THE SURFACE OF THE UNRUSTED IRON SURFACE. I now
realise how important this is artistically when looking at rusty tsuba. They
are already compromised in condition, price and quality by the rust. Now,
Dirk's restoration has restored an already wrecked piece to something with a
very credible appearance, thereby preserving the marvellous zogan work. It sure
looks a treat, but is it kosher to suggest that this tsuba is less valuable than
a similar one in perfect (I mean PERFECT) condition??? I know money isn't the
issue, I mean this from an artistic perspective, but in the end it all boils
down to monetary value (apart from the fact that in 5 billion years we will all
be cosmic dust!!!).

Dirk, I have seen this tsuba before and have an image stored from recent weeks.
Ebay??? Po-edo??? I guess it is the same one as I remember looking at the
crusty, red rust and gritting my teeth. It is a joy to see it in your restored
condition and I thank you for putting it forward.

Best regards,

Barry Thomas
(aka Batman)
