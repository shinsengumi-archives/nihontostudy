Re: the state of things What's up

by soshuju

All-
I am a member of most of the organizations concerned and I have to say
I have had a very different experience. The very first person I went to
was a dealer, who as Harry has pointed out, risked the safety and
security of his family and collection by welcoming into his home a
complete stranger. I didn't get cookies and milk but did get the honest
appraisal I had asked for and an introduction to people who could
further me in my areas of interest. He didn't know it then but it was a
life-changing meeting for me.
For some years I was reluctant to join the clubs because I was
concerned that a newbie wouldn't be able to keep up and might be
unwelcome. Not the case however, I have learned a great deal from every
one of the members of my club and we are always happy to see new faces.
New faces with or without stuff in hand I might add. You are all
invited to join us at the NCJSC anytime you are in town and your
schedule permits.
I write travelogues and grade school level essays on subjects of
interest to me for our newsletter. It was several years after I joined
that I took up the challenge to contribute. I didn't know much then,
still don't now but I love a good tale and have a hard time resisting
an empty pulpit.I am also unafraid of being wrong. Not sure this is a
talent but we all pitch in with with those skills we bring with us to
make the club the best we can. I have seen some great changes in the
last ten years and fully expect more as some of us newbies start to
flex our muscles.
The question was what is wrong and what can we do to fix those
problems. Having studied briefly in Japan I find little wrong with what
is happening there, education, study, and publication continue. There
is a world of new info published each month, there are kantei-kai and
taikai providing unparalleled opportunities for growth. With the birth
of the American Branch of the NBTHK alot of that will now be available
to us unlucky enough to live here in the land of the 9.99 pizza. Ok
membership could be cheaper, but would be worth it at double the cost.
We don't do enough here to spread the word and bring in new members.
Visits to local schools ( as a list member suggested awhile back), a
table at Martial arts tournaments, more advertising and other ways of
reaching out are all possible. I for one would like to see more
translations, but we have the NBTHK and the NTHK and soon translations
from Dr. Robson from the JSSUS, what isn't being transmitted? Thats
gist for another thread perhaps, but it seems the material is being
made available and each publication/organization has their area. It
ain't coming out fast enough, I agree, afterall I went to Japan to try
to learn the lan-gauge and accelerate the learning process but it is
coming out.
I also want to see older material rehashed, wouldn't it be great if we
could do an oshigata issue, going back to the original "masters" I
would love to see biographies of long time collectors, taped interviews
and a history of our clubs. We have a "convention", it is in San
Francisco. If there is a beginner topic you want to learn about the
club is always looking for monthly topics tell us what areas you want
to explore.
Mentoring? certainly can't argue with that, wish I had one myself.
Since I have a voice, thanks to this medium, I always try to answer
newerbie questions and always help folks who contact me off line and I
have received lots of help from other listeros myself. Again my
experience has been a positive one. This medium you ask? Yes lots to
say on that but will make that a separate thread.
Anyway what's up is,is that interest is down, membership is down and
the time is ripe for a small group of motivated individuals to
revolutionize these clubs and this study. Maybe all we need is you, I
am here already and I am ready to create the future...
-t

e-mail new user IDs on ebay that are bidding on swords and fittings. I've had a

what so ever). I think there is potential to recruit from the ranks of ebay. My
2 cents.

Re: the state of things What's Next?

by soshuju

All-
Forgive me if you have heard this rant before, but I think this the
most important challenge that faces us;
I can't agree with anyone who dismisses computer and internet
technology as quick fixes or spoon food for Gen X-ers. John Yumoto
himself said the computer was the future of nihonto study and no matter
how much info we make available here, the hard work will still have to
be done in hand one blade at a time.
There is honestly no reason we cannot have all our references
downloaded and indexed in an online library. What I wouldn't give to
have just three books on my PDA! Electronic paper? I'll take a sheaf!
Seeing what others have done with Tosho and what Darcy has done with
his database I wonder if I shouldn't have studied "computer" instead of
Japanese.
Whats missing? What would do the most good? Nagayama without a doubt.
I use the Japanese but even when I use the Eigo I have days when I
can't find a particular smith even though I KNOW he is in there.
Computer would solve that. The Meikan? absolutely if it is just a
hobby, and you are not obsessed like me, then you want a quick
reference to names and dates. This is the book and with minimal
Japanese it's secrets can be unlocked. I would venture a great deal of
useful info is already in print, from the JSSUS and other US clubs. I
expect it would be easier to get them on board with a project to
update, collate and disseminate all the info contained in those old
newsletters.
Authentication? Here's a big complaint. Newbiles have to visit dealers
and silverbacks or even Japan to get an appraisal. Even were everything
we now have translated on line, the eye of an expert could never be
replaced. The clubs that do shinsa now, could save time and effort
offering internet appraisals. Charging a nominal fee for reviewing
scans and rubbings, this could be credited against a full shinsa
submission. You'll have to deal with more junk on the whole, but a lot
less in person and the whole process would feel more accessible to the
hobbyist.
The tricks, scams, flaws and pitfalls could all be compiled on a
single website. There could be a known fake database, to help prevent
future frauds. There will still be people who don't bother to visit it
and some people won't listen no matter how many times or ways you
explain "fatal flaw" or "fake signature". So again the internet will be
a big help but it won't be able to save everyone.
Lectures and study groups. Martial arts dojo bring "experts" over for
seminars all the time. There is no reason we cannot. We have Dr Robson
of the NTHK making annual presentations of the kind and quality seen in
Japan, others have come in the past, why not invite them over? The
argument against seems to be the money. Not enough members willing to
pay for an evening with Fukunaga Suiken? Well Rock stars do online
interviews why not the stars of nihonto? Hire a translator, sit at a
terminal and rock out. Better yet set up a video conference, don't
allow questions just broadcast the kantei kai, or let Sensei talk for
an hour each week on whatever he likes. However as Clive has mentioned
without your alls support...
I have talked about virtual swords one could hold in a sensor-gloved
hand and examine to your hearts desire and a slew of virtual Sensei
giving lectures on each of these. Check out e-kokuho at the Tokyo
National Museum, virtual swords presented at various magnifications and
in different light, excellent photography. We aren't so very far from
this but can't we digitize the old lectures of John Yumoto? Put them up
as audio files that play with subtitles? Digitize what film we have of
him and others, add updated graphics, background notes, subtitles,
oshigata and still photos?
Shijo kantei! Why not a for fee monthly kantei? The same swords that
are in the magazine. This way overseas members don't have to fool with
postage and have more time to crunch the problem before the deadline.
Create a new category of e-member, $15 or $20 bucks a year for a
passcode to access the members only pages allowing them to try their
hand at kantei . Virtual shinsa could be a priveledge for only those
with a full or e-membership and passcode. Member profiles could be part
of the site. Imagine your picture and list of wants or area of
expertise listed for any and all interested parties to see. Friendships
and business contacts could be made, you might even place that name
with a face you remember from the taikai.This is where we are headed,
we only have to wait to see who does it first.
Wanting to see all these advances, I can barely afford to pay all the
dues, but then cannot afford to let any of these great clubs fade away.
If it is not your club it can be, join up now. Waiting for the reform
and withholding your dues will likely only slow the change. Plunk down
the money and be active and we can have all the things on our wish
list. Answer the door Neo!
-t

by yamaimojg

Creating a distribution channel for this stuff is important. There is a lot of material that is in circulation among friends and at clubs. For example, our own Tom Helm made a nice videotape of an NTHK group visit to a swordsmith in Kamakura. I got a VHS copy from Tom and have shown the tape at our NY shibu meeting and loaned it out to friends. That's all very good, but the material hasn't traveled as far or freely as it could.

An online or disc-based way to distribute this stuff would not only make more of the existing material, but would also create an incentive to make the effort to record more. We haven't taped Gordon's lectures for example. If the tape is only going to circulate between a few friends, it's not too motivating to take the time to do it. OTOH, if we know that there's a place for it on the web there's a much bigger motivation to capture it.

I don't think that the old model of selling a video for $39.95 (or a lot more) will go anywhere. If it can be put online or sold for a few bucks or as part of membership dues on disc it'll have an audience. Not a money maker, but a way to distribute information.
