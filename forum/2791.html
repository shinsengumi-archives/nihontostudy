Hirata School Tsuba Question

by neobliviscar

List-

I have recently been looking at a (non cloissone)
Hirata style tsuba:

-It is not iron, but rather seems copper.
-It is plain, unmarked, with the plate being covered
in dark lacquer.
-The mimi edge is done in a sort of silver or other
metal crimped onto the place and fashioned to look
slightly like the edges of an abalone shell or other
mollusk .

---All in all, sort of a common plain-jane Hirata
style design. The seppa dai area does have those big
pock marks I see on Hirata tsuba.

I am wondering if this is just a very late Hirata, a
Meiji era export, or an attempt to imply Hirata recently discussed a gi-taganamei Higo Kamiyoshi tsuba
with Jim G., in that the unsigned tsuba had
hammermarks to imply it was the work of second
generation Kamiyoshi "Fukanobu">.
On what little I have seen and very little I know
of such Hirata tsuba, they always have radial marks on
the plate to add to the design impression of a shell.
This one been flat and black lacquered, seems very
odd.

Thank you in advance to any replies,

Curran

Re: [nihontostudy] Hirata School Tsuba Question

by yamaimojg

Curran,
The cloisonné Hirata people worked in Kyoto. You're talking about Hirata in Higo. I'm not aware of any connection between the groups other than the shared name.

Hirata Hikozo did a lot of work in copper alloys. Most plates are yamagane or copper, but some are shinchu or shakudo. Many of them are lacquered. The mimi edge you're talking about is a fukurin (rim cover) and Hikozo often used them, usually in a contrasting metal. I think that you're describing an Odawara fukurin, which was a particular specialty of Hikozo. Many Hirata guards have the concentric circle file marking you mention, but others have amida yasurime, irregular or polished surfaces. Many do have a distinctive taganemei of heavy, domed punch marks along the sides of the nakago ana.

I can't say what you have without seeing it. There have been contemporary copies of Hikozo's work for sale at US sword shows recently. I don't think that they were made to deceive since they are very obviously brand new, shiny and not at all in the taste of the original time and place. On the other hand, a couple of them turned up at the Chicago shinsa, so someone may have gotten a little confused. I have no idea how the sellers are representing these guards.

Jim

by sworddad

Hi Rich, all -

Not to be a wet blanket here but--referring to the first tsuba with chidori--I suspect this would be designated Tosa Myochin made in Akasaka style. I say this because of the distinctive tagane (kakushi-tagane) around the nakago-ana, a Tosa Myochin trait according the Sasano and others. (Sasano points this out in his original English sukashi tsuba book, pages 218-220.)

Not to muddy up the waters, but an odd experience with a like-tsuba was experienced by me back in the early 1980s. In 1979 during a Chicago NTHK shinsa, Sasano papered a friend's mumei tsuba as (I believe) 2nd master Akasaka work. Later, the tsuba went to a Haynes auction where I purchased it. Bob Haynes was adamant about the tsuba "looking like" Akasaka, but that in reality it was Tosa Myochi. His argument was based largely on these "wisker-like" kakushi-tagane that were place around the tsuba's nakago-ana. I sold that tsuba a year or two ago and passed on this story along with the original NTHK paper declaring it Akasaka. Either way, it remains a fine tsuba!

Ron
STL

Hi Ron, a nice observation. and possibly correct. I went right through
the Akasaka books and cannot find a single tsuba with the kakushi-tagane

have not heard of them earlier than that, has anyone else ?. I kind of
think from the images, this tsuba of mine looks older than the 1800's,
but then again, as I a said, when I have it in hand, it may be easier to
tell.

Anyway, food for thought, I like Tosa Myochin also as a school and would
not be upset if it turned out that way.

best wishes

Re: [nihontostudy] Akasaka tsuba

by yamaimojg

I also think that it's too old for Tosa Myochin and that the tagane are not as clear as the ones they used. But, to be an even wetter blanket, I'm not convinced that it's Akasaka either. The Akasaka weren't the only group to use this design. The edges of the sukashi show some rounding and beveling, where Akasaka is usually quite sharp and vertical. The texture of the iron looks a bit softer and grainier than Akasaka. The shape of the design elements is also softer and looser than typical for Akasaka, compare the details with the signed example in the Sano catalog. Note also that the seppa dai is wider than that one. Some things to consider.

Jim

by yamaimojg

David,
The same motif often appears through groups spread around the country. Since we were talking Akasaka, many of the later Akasaka guards (made in Edo) were lifted directly from work originating in Higo (Kyushu). Of course there are also general motifs that persist across long spans of time and space. That's culture.

There are extant pattern books for later iroe shiiremono tsuba. I don't think that I've seen one for sukashi guards, but they might have existed. But, think about our own culture... there are many common and persistent motifs that aren't set forth in a pattern book.

The doubled elements at 12:00 and 6:00 are myoga - ginger shoots. At 3:00 and 9:00 are of course tea whisks. On the diagonals are folding fans and linking them all are birds. The inside of the rim is a very loose kiri. The groups of three dots that you mention as problematic are a standard part of the depiction of myoga in that period.

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] More on the Akasaka tsuba question

by yamaimojg

Akasaka looks like a reasonable call for this, although the poor surface condition confuses things.

I think that a lot of the punch marks on the guards being posted are things done later in fitting them to blades. Not all Tosa Myochin guards show the characteristic kakushi-tagane, but the ones that do are distinctive. It's not just any diagonal marking at the top of the nakago ana. For examples take a look at numbers 166 and 167 in Sasano's "Early Japanese Sword Guards."

Jim
