Blokes,

The story I remember is that menuki are vestigial ornaments from pre-Heian times
when the tsuka was fixed to the nakago with a rivet that was not removable. The
heads of the rivet were decorated and eventually became the menuki we know
today. What I did not know until these emails is that some menuki have
"interlocking pins" to become mekugi. Sure, I've seen these on cheap Meiji
pieces, especially tanto, but didn't know early Goto menuki had the same
feature. We can clearly see that as the Nihonto developed from earliest times
the disadvantage of a fixed rivet became evident, but the early menuki discussed
appear to have still functioned as a mekugi, only later becoming separate with a
bamboo mekugi, or mekugi of other material (iron??). I also remember that in
the Momoyama period a single menuki was used, accounting for some of the
"orphans" we see. Looking back over my collecting career I think I once had a
single (i.e., never a pair) shakudo menuki of a pomegranate. It was of that
really dark black shakudo. Sometimes knowledge comes too late to save us from
ourselves!!!

Ken, I do have somewhere a late 19th century English treatise on the Goto
mainline (Shirobei 17 generations) with photos, but it doesn't go into the
"secret marks"... Does anyone know the title of the Japanese one that Ken
mentions??

Regards,

Barry Thomas.

by sworddad

Hi -

Honestly, I could never get too excited about the premise that menuki posts were ever made to serve as mekugi except for what you wrote, Barry, about early, early tsuba being rivited with the heads decorated possibly being forerunners of menuki as we know them today. But of course, I have no "other" explanation for the "male/female" posts. I do find it interesting to note that the "primary" menuki usually has the "male/solid" post whereas the "secondary" menuki has the "female/hollow" post. (Primary one would be fitted to the omote/outside of the tsuka; the secondary would be on the ura side.) Make one wonder if perhaps there is some "other" meaning behind the use of male/female menuki posts.

As for the "secret marks" of Goto, I'm not positive but it seems Jim Gilbert remarked about this section of the book awhile back, saying -- something like -- the idea of secret marks is not exactly true. Hope I'm not saying this incorrectly, but something like that. I too, have always wondered about that section of the book in hopes of "learning the key to identifying Goto works! Maybe that section just presents this idea in an overly simplified, general way?

Ron

Blokes,

The story I remember is that menuki are vestigial ornaments from pre-Heian times
when the tsuka was fixed to the nakago with a rivet that was not removable. The
heads of the rivet were decorated and eventually became the menuki we know
today. What I did not know until these emails is that some menuki have
"interlocking pins" to become mekugi. Sure, I've seen these on cheap Meiji
pieces, especially tanto, but didn't know early Goto menuki had the same
feature. We can clearly see that as the Nihonto developed from earliest times
the disadvantage of a fixed rivet became evident, but the early menuki discussed
appear to have still functioned as a mekugi, only later becoming separate with a
bamboo mekugi, or mekugi of other material (iron??). I also remember that in
the Momoyama period a single menuki was used, accounting for some of the
"orphans" we see. Looking back over my collecting career I think I once had a
single (i.e., never a pair) shakudo menuki of a pomegranate. It was of that
really dark black shakudo. Sometimes knowledge comes too late to save us from
ourselves!!!

Ken, I do have somewhere a late 19th century English treatise on the Goto
mainline (Shirobei 17 generations) with photos, but it doesn't go into the
"secret marks"... Does anyone know the title of the Japanese one that Ken
mentions??

Regards,

Barry Thomas.

Ron et al -
I have to say that I too have heard that the male-female post was a
residual from the original "decorated mekugi head". In the late-Edo
when they started reproducing old koshirae styles this type of mekugi
was seen again but most often with a screw in feature.
The section on secret marks is nothing of the sort. As I understand
it, it is a detailed explanation of the chisel work seen in
individual artists. It shows close ups of fine detail on toes,
feathers, scales and claws, showing how some of the masters put the
finishing touches on their work. If these marks are present and done
in a certain way you have evidence that it may be the work of a
certain master. If they are not present then maybe you do not. They
are all visible however, not hidden in any way, one just has to
recognize the workmanship.
and before anybody asks, I haven't done more than browse thru it, I
don't want to translate it and I don't know any secrets...
-t
