sanmai tsuba

by elliottshogun

HELP! I need some clarification;

Sanmai tsuba are tsuba made using three layers of metal held together by a fukurin. The word 'sanmai' does not designate a school or group, correct?
Kinko (Kinku) means metal worker. Ko-kinko is 'early' metal worker? Tsubako is tsuba maker.
Kagamishi - Is this a school (Ha) or metal worker? Do they or him make Sanmai tsuba? Is this related to the Kaga Ha?

I have Ko-kinko tsuba, Sanmai tsuba, and one Kagamishi made tsuba. They all are made of three layers, held by fukurin. There is zogan on some, and all have some design formed by a substance under the outside layer of metal. How do you tell one from the other????

I appreciate the time and education that you (all) give me.

Elliott

Re: [nihontostudy] sanmai tsuba

by retalt

Hi Elliot, yes sanmei refers to the technique, not a group. Ko Kinko are
the main culprits. Many of them are held together with two little
rivets, the fukurin is more to finish off the the tsuba aesthetically.
See here (thanks Baz)

http://www.nihonto-bijutsubu.com/personal/ko-kinko.jpg
http://www.nihonto-bijutsubu.com/personal/ko-kinko-sanmai.jpg

The tsuba you are referring to as Sanmai, probably is Ko Kinko, but I
guess we would need to see it to be able to make a call like that.
Kagami shi (kagami = mirror, shi = maker) usually made tsuba that were
cast from bronze or yamagane. I have not seen any sanmei kagamishi
tsuba but that does not mean there were not any. I will have a look in
Sasano's little book tonight at home and see if there is any reference.

This is a typical Kagami-shi tsuba
http://richardturner.wordpress.com/2009/01/17/130/

Most likely, all 3 tsuba you have are Ko Kinko.

Cheers

Richard

Elliot, and all,

Richard is spot-on in his observations, of course.

I'm thinking that it's worth noting that "Ko-Kinko" can tend to be a big box
in which to sort anything old (Ko) made of soft metal (Kinko) rather than
iron. So the materials can cover the gamut from brass, bronze, yamagane
("impure" copper), more refined copper, and even shakudo, and the techniques
can range from cast, forged, sanmai, various surface treatments, zogan, etc.
Some pieces can be easily classified by "school" in the roughest sense of
the word - cast guards are invariably "Kagamishi" - and somewhat more
decorated guards like your sanmai get called "Ko-Kinko" like they are some
pre-Kinko (fancy) guards. In fact some get further classified as Ko-Goto or
Ko-Mino when they look like the antecedent of those well defined schools
that fully flower as we move closer to Edo period.

My own recent area of interest has been "tachikanagushi" guards which are
soft metal thought (by some) to be made by tachi makers who also made
fittings to go with the blades they made, which is (I think) an interesting
speculation without much empirical evidence. In any case you can see much of
that collection on Boris Markhasin's website, on the consignment page, at
yamabushiantiques.com.

Sometimes I think the semantics and classification get in the way of simple
enjoyment of beautiful art objects, but I approach this study more from an
art angle.

Regards,

Craig
