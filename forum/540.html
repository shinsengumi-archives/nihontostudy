Gomoku zogan tsuba

by alchemyst

We've been having a discussion on gomoku zogan tsuba on the
other yahoo group. I noted that there have been a lot of
minty ones on ebay lately and wondering if they are being
repro-ed.

Also as to their age. One seller on ebay said Muromachi.
All the books that I recall say late Edo. One Torigoye
books says they are poor tsuba and to stay away from from
The Shinchu Zogan Tsuba book that covers Onin, Heianjo (mostly),
Yoshiro and Ko-Shoami shinchu zogan doesn't mention them nor
picture anything like them.

What's the story here? Age? etc. Are these like the "Mito
Shoami" tsuba, that really aren't any particular school.
I've a daisho in gomoku zogan with Saotome type hammered
plates. I like them regardless. Just wondering what they
are all about.

by yamaimojg

Rich,
I haven't been following eBay, but if you want to email me some auction links offline I will take a look. I think that you are correct that gomoku zogan is quite late, I think perhaps even later than late Edo. However, the plates that it is applied to can be as old as Muromachi period. I have seen some fairly nice early tsuba (including Saotome work) covered with gomoku zogan and wondered about what the condition of the plate would be after removing the stuff. I haven't tried it, though.

The decoration was probably added to jazz up older or plain iron tsuba. I don't know if there was a domestic market for them or if they were only made for sale to foreigners. It's not a particularly desirable type, and I doubt that there was any school associated with their production. I haven't given up on Mito Shoami yet, BTW, but haven't had any time to work on them. Tsuba Taikan is sitting on my desk with a book mark on page 246.

Jim

Rich,
I think that's basically correct. Of course everything needs to be considered when looking at a tsuba, but the plate is most important with iron guards and probably the hardest to understand.

Note that when a mixed period tsuba (later decoration or modification of an older plate) is submitted to shinsa, it will usually be dated to its youngest element. Some mixed period pieces won't get kanteisho -- I've seen a tosho type tsuba that appeared to have sukashi added later than failed. I've never seen a gomoku zogan tsuba submitted, but if it passed at all, I don't think that an old-plate example would draw an attribution to the maker of the original plate.
