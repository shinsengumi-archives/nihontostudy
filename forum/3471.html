by davidzincavage

You can find a lot of good motif discussions in:

Baird, Merrily. Symbols of Japan -Thematic Motifs in Art and Design, New York:

Rizzoli International Publications Inc., 2001.



My whole reference shell includes:



An Illustrated Encyclopedia of Japanese Family Crests, Tokyo: Graphic-Sha Publishing, 2001.



Baird, Merrily. Symbols of Japan -Thematic Motifs in Art and Design, New York:

Rizzoli International Publications Inc., 2001.



Dower, John W. The Elements of Japanese Design - A Handbook of Family Crests,

Heraldry & Symbolism, New York: Weatherhill, (1971) 1979.



Edmunds, Will H. Pointers and Clues to the Subjects of Chinese and Japanese Art,

Chicago: Art Media Resources, Ltd., (1934) N.D.



Joly, Henri I. Legend in Japanese Art - A Description of Historical Episodes, Legendary

Characters, Folk-Lore, Myths, Religious Symbolism, Illustrated in the Arts of Old

Japan, Rutland, Vermont: Charles E. Tuttle Company, (1908), 1967.



Matsuya Piece-Goods Store, translated by Fumie Adachi,. Japanese Design Motifs, New

York: Dover, 1972.

-published originally in Japan in 1913.



Motoji, Niwa and Sadao, Hibi translated by Jay W. Thomas. Snow, Wave, Pine -

Traditional Patterns in Japanese Design, New York: Kodansha

International Ltd., 2001.


Papinot, E., Historical and Geographical Dictionary of Japan, Rutland, Vermont:
Charles E. Tuttle Company, 1972.



Turnbull, Stephen, Samurai Heraldry, Oxford: Osprey Publishing, 2002.



Williams, C.A.S. Outlines of Chinese Symbolism & Art Motives, Shanghai: Kelly and

Walsh, 1941, New York: Dover, 1976.

Ok-, I figured someone more knowledgable would speak
up on this rather straighforward topic. I know little

armchair commentary, so everyone feel free to correct
me....

Your clockwise swastika: Nazi symbol
Your counter-clockwise symbol: Occurs both in
Buddhism and
Southwest American
Indian symbol

You will see it on the neck of some Budda statues ,
right in the little divet between the collar bones.
Meaning? I am not sure. Memory says to me something
about the 10,000 lives or 10,000 incarnations of
Budda? Someone with a copy of Joly want to help?
Memory also says it showed up in Southwest
American items. I can't name the tribes or give you
much more than that. I also believe it was adopted by
our US Military from the Indians and used by troops
until it became associated with the Nazi party and was
'discontinued' by the US. This last bit may be
(hystorical myth). I believe most of this is from a
documentary or TV show about Southwest Indian culture
(cliff/cave dwelling?) I saw about 15 years ago. Some
of those historical shows stretch the truth a good
bit.

As for examples, there are many.

See tsuba G0016
http://www.nona.dti.ne.jp/~sword/tsuba.htm

Another off the top of my head is the complete black
and silver Daisho koshirae in the back of the Hahn Bin
Siong book. Lovely koshirae. Swastikas galore over
everything on them.

In short, it is a buddhist thing as far as I have
bothered learning to date. If someone has a copy of
Joly or is more familiar with Buddhist imagery than
me, please add.

by retalt

All, I have an Akasaka guard ( mumei ) I always attributed to the 6th (
or there abouts ) Tadatoki. It has some pitting in the mimi and
recently, in better light, I noticed for the first time, unmistakable
layers in these pitted area. Clearly 3 layers are visible.

Reading Masayuki Sasano, he talks about a famous tsuba by the shodai
Tadamasa that was burnt in a fire in the early 20th century, and how the
layers were revealed with this damage. He also mentions that if you can
see the layers with the tsuba in good condition, then it is not a great
tsuba.

As the layers on mine are visible only in the pitting damage and no
where else on the tsuba, I was wondering if this guard maybe older than
I originally thought ?. When did the 3 layer construction finish/taper
off in Akasaka tsuba production?, was it still in the first few Tadatoki
or mostly with the first 4 masters.

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Tsuba Mystery/svastika

by mvinehout

I couldn't find much in Joly's books, I did see on page 476; "On their white
paper sails are written the soul names (kaimyo) of the dead. Upon their
deck is placed a small cup of fresh water, besides an incense cup and some
banners bearing the manju (svastika, fylfot), with the cross arms turning
towards the right, or sometimes, but rarely turning towards the left
(suvastika). They are used on the Festival of the Dead (Ura bon ye)." Also
I found this info:
The svastika is a symbol of auspiciousness (svast = auspiciousness). It has
been used as a symbol of the sun or of visnu or even of ganesa. It also
represents the world-wheel, the eternally changing world, round a fixed,
unchanging centre or god.

Svastika marks depicted on doors or walls of buildings or an animals, are
believed to protect them from the wrath of evil spirits or furies of nature.

I have seen this mark on old maps, marking the place of temples in Japan, so
I know the Japanese used this symbol to represent more than what I found out
tonight. I'm sure Jim or Tom probably knows...
Mike

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Tsuba Mystery

by Barry Thomas

Andres and All,

Thank you for the Field Museum link. I've had a good trawl and was very
interested to see the tsuba by Sonobe Yoshihide in the Boone Collection. Is
this a Shupa (day-night) tsuba??

In the link itself as given by Andres I was particularly interested to see that
Rob Nalewajk's commentary inluded the note that shakudo was an exclusively
Japanese alloy. I believe this is now considered not to be so. The quote from
the link is:

"Shakudo (literally "red copper") is a metal alloy that was invented and used
exclusively by the Japanese."

I had also always believed shakudo to be an exclusively Japanese alloy, probably
having read it in the same quoted sources. A friend of mine disagreed with my
thought and pointed me to an article, which I will briefly recap here for the
listeros interest (I last did this on Ichi's Nihonto list in March 1996!!). He
gave me a copy of the article he had read and, voila, it does seem shakudo is
far from being a uniquely Japanese alloy. The article is:

"Secrets of Achilles' shield", by Paul Craddock, in New Scientist, 22 January
1994, pages 32-35.

Paul Craddock is head of metals research at the British Museum, London. The
article gives further reading for those interested:

Metal Plating and Patination, edited by S. La Niece and P.T. Craddock
(Butterworth-Heinemann, 1993).

OK, OK, "What's it about", I hear you cry. I hope a short quote from the
article will suffice:

"It now turns out that some of the most prestigious metals from the ancient West
are almost identical to a sophisticated metal, known as shakudo, that is still
made in Japan. The trail led from Egyptian hieroglyphics of 3500 years ago to
clues contained in Homer's Iliad, on to the famous and highly prized Corinthian
bronze of the Roman Empire, eastwards with more clues from the great epics of
Indian and Tibetan literature, then to the work of barely remembered Chinese
craftsmen before culminating in 19th century Japan.

"The detective work took place at the British Museum in London, where we had
suspected that some of the processes briefly described by ancient alchemists for
making a special type of bronze with a black patina were similar to those for
making shakudo. By a happy combination of detailed examination of metalwork,
careful study of ancient literature, some intuition and a little luck we were
able to turn promising hypotheses into fact, and build up a cultural and
technical trail stretching over half the globe through more than 3000 years."

I found this to be fascinating reading, and I hope it is of interest to the
list.

Regards,

Barry Thomas
(Melbourne, Australia).

by retalt

greetings all, last week I was rummaging through my tsuba collection and
pulled out a Higo ( Nishigaki ) tsuba i have had for some time. I
decided it could do with an extended vacation in the pocket, wrapped in
a nice soft cotton cloth I have. I got to thinking about what was
actually required here as I do not do this very often, just preferring
to leave well enough alone. A visit to Jim G's site and a read through
his article "Cleaning Iron Tsuba
<http://home.earthlink.net/%7Ejggilbert/Cleaning.htm>" was required to
see if there was anything I needed to know besides the basics.

Reading through, I came across the following sentence, "Lots of patience
and tired fingers is the only way."

This got me to thinking and asking the following questions.

1. How much pressure is applied generally ?, A: only a soft touch,
lightly brushing the surface with the cloth. B: a firm touch, bring a
little pressure to the surface. C: hard pressure, rubbing like a
lunatic. The last I think would be a big no no as my main concern is
over polishing, making the tsuba shiny and this is the last thing we
want to achieve.

2: Is there any sort of preferred cloth. ie. cotton, silk, flannel,
gingham ???? ( just joking on the flannel ). Has anyone tried all types
of cloth and had varying result.

3: Do people use oils ?. I have heard of Choji, skin oil ( i.e forehead,
neck etc ) and gun oil add to lustre but I find this makes the tsuba
look a little artificial. Ok, to stop any active rust I understand but
if the Hira is just a little dull and neglected then.................

Ok, so just something to generate some thought and discussion.

Cheers all.
