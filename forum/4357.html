Re: Antique katana blade - showa stamp

by nihontochicken

Yes, many ancestral blades were remounted in gunto koshirae and
carried off to the Pacific War. But they were not stamped with the
Showa cherry blossom. A Showa stamp, also the nearly as common Seki
stamp, indicates the blade is not completely traditionally made. In
some better examples that look very traditional, displaying both hada
and water tempered hamon, the stamp indicates it was made with western
style steel instead of the traditional tamahagane.

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Antique katana blade - showa stamp

by bobgriffis

I'm asking because I have a blade which has all the hallmarks of a
traditionally made blade--Masame hada, a well defined and unusual hamon,
an 8.5" nakago with taka no ha yasurime and signature. It is longer
than most WWII blades (ha is 26.75"). It also has a Showa cherry
blossom stamp. The polisher said it was a folded blade (19 folds),
confirmed by CAT scan, and was one of the hardest blades to polish he
had come across. Lacking the Showa stamp, they believed its
craftsmanship indicated a pre 1860s blade

Its matching saya is also unusual--it is covered in a tan-colored same
which is 3 skins expertly applied, but the gunto mount looks hacked in
as if retrofitted later.

So, absent the Showa stamp, both blade and saya suggest an ancestoral
blade that was stamped and refitted as gunto for use in WWII. What else
might I look for to authenticate it?

Bob,

I've seen several pieces that were nontraditionally made that had hada,
interesting hamons, etc.,. There were some interesting comments in
one of Kapp's books about how some of these were made with wrought iron
from train rails.

One other thing to look for is nie - no nie usually means not
traditionally made.
