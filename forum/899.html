one heck of a tanto

by ashigaru1

While described as being slightly "tired" this tanto has much going
for it. Soshu style -- not all that common; ubu; student
[atribution, school of] of not only a good smith, but a student of
Masamune -- considered the O-Kaji -- if i can make up a word -- the
master of master smiths. His work is almost never signed, and it is
not surpising his student's swords were not either. He worked for
the Shogun of the Kamakera Bankafu thus would not usually sign his
name as a relatively indendent smith might. It has good paper. In
good polish. And since they do not say, I assume it is delivered in
shirasaya. Being a newcomer to Nihon-to myself, that is about all I
got. I am sure the more Knowledgable members can expand upon, or
correct, my comments.

One question for the group: Why the three mekugie ana?

Doug

by samurai@xhasc.com

Hi Doug,

The tanto is mine. It is indeed in shirasaya, and also has Tanobe
sensei's sayagaki on it.

You hit on a lot of items why it is a collectable piece. There are a lot
of activities in the hamon too, making it a good study sword, and of
course it is also representative of its times.

The horimono are not etched as was previously suggested by the question
asker. Horimono like this are carved. Naotsuna are often found with
bohi, but horimono like this are quite rare on his works, and there are
very few tanto by this smith (none are signed).

There are three mekugi ana most likely on this tanto because it has gone
into three different sets of fittings (or more) over its lifetime. That,
and the frequent polishing, would likely point to this as being a well
used weapon, rather than something that spent most of its life as an
object of adoration. Those are my guesses at it.

For the original poster, in terms of the high end Soshu smiths, this
represents entry-level price, so the question is more one of what is
holding this one back from higher prices I would think. The hamon is
still lively on this tanto, but it is at the point where I wouldn't want
to put one, or two more polishes on it. That is pretty much the only
thing against it in my book, but then there are others who have
different tastes in school, smith, period, and type.

- darcy

Doug ,

Ooooh - that's a question that usually starts a jihad... Most "real"
dealers will make comments
about how buying a new sword is like buying a new car - you lose money
the moment you
drive it off the lot and/or will make disparaging comments about how
they don't consider them
"real" swords and don't look at them much or... And a lot of "real"
sword collectors are also
of this opinion....

On the other hand, I've heard it said that the better modern stuff is as
good as or better than
all but the best of the shinto era pieces. (I personally think this is
closer to correct, but then
again, I'm crazy enough to actually have a couple modern pieces, so
YMMV...).

If you wish to buy one, its a real minefield - there are a LOT of extant
pieces out there, often
at prices far below what the person who originally
purchased/commissioned the piece
probably paid, but then again, an object is really only worth what the
next guy is willing to pay
for it, but I digress... Because of this, I'm not sure I'd consider
having one commissioned - just
look for a extant piece in the style you like by the maker you like with
the activities you like to pop up
(it sounds nuts, but it does seem to eventually happen...).
You also have to realize that you probably will end up selling it at a
loss if you ever decide to.

There's a whole market built up around mukansa and mukansa wannabee
level stuff versus guys
who don't enter the competitions/don't win, yada yada - perhaps a trip
to the shinsakuto site
listed on rich's site would help you learn more about this...


Good Luck,

rkg
