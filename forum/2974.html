by retalt

Hi Fredrik, very nice tsuba and not it is not a Chinese rip-off I can
assure you. Akasaka would fit, as would Tosa Myochin. I notice there is
no carving in the hira ( surface ) usually the tea whisks ( 3 and 9
o'clock ) have the brush ( whisk ) lines carved in them and the Ginger
leaves ( 12 and 6 o'clock ) have usually 3 dots ( see this image
http://www.nihontomessageboard.com/tsuba_collection/akasaka_tadatoki_tea.jpg
)
and 3 lines on them as well. The surface does not look like Akasaka
plate to me, I may be wrong, it's quite rough and pitted up close. It
could point to a late students work I guess, or a late copy, none the
less, I think it is a late tsuba, as in the end of the Edo period sometime.

Just some ramblings LOL, they may be all wrong.

Rich

Re: [nihontostudy] Interesting painting (sword related)

by davidzincavage

The Noh play is:

Kokaji (a blacksmith, an official title of Waki)
Shite(Mae) a boy, an incarnation of Inari Myohjin mask doh-ji, jidoh
Shite(Nochi) a fox, Inari Myohjin mask ko-tobide
Waki Sanjoh no Kokaji Munechika place Inari shrine (Kyoto city)
Waki-Tsure Tachibana-no-Michinari place a house of Munechika (Kyoto city)
Ai-kyohgen a servant of Waki dance hataraki
? ? time 1.4 h
Category 5
story Mae The Emperor saw a wonderful dream, and orders Munechika to make a sword, a messenger is Michinari. But he doesn't know what to do because he doesn't have a partner. So he notices there is no way except for miraculous power and prays at Inari shrine. Then a boy appears and says he'll succeed, and tells ancient myths.
Ai Ai tells about the story.
Nochi Munechika is waiting the miracle on the altar. And the fox comes and forges with Munechika, and hands it to Michinari and goes home.

Dear Patrick

Yes indeed - one of the Namban group and probably made by a casting
process in the early-mid C19. This group is characterised by its
openwork and undercutting; the common presence of affronted dragons
with the tama jewel; and the atypically shaped seppa-dai. All of
these are present on your tsuba.

I would have expected a tsuba of this type to be of iron, but some
were cast in bronze. Interestingly these are unexpectedly attracted
by a magnet, but the admixture of up to 30% of iron to bronze in
order to reduce the cost of the expensive alloy was not unknown.
Also, iron was a common inclusion in naturally occurring copper ore

Re: Tsuba help

by drjohnplissenden

Dear Ron

Thank you for posting a photograph of your beautiful, papered Namban
tsuba. At 84 cms in width it is unusually large for the group - only
some 3 per cent measure that or more, and the largest one I have seen
was 93 cms. It is also unusual in that it has two contemporaneous
ryo-hitsu, and that these are both hangetsu-gata. Does the smaller
one have sekigane?

The iron appears to be good, although the extensive carving on these
tsubas meant that the iron was never of very good quality. The
carving of the undercut karakusa and the surface chisel-work are
sharp and excellent. The plain, squared rim; the absence of OTT gold
overlay; the conventionally shaped seppa-dai and ryo-hitsu; and the
asymmetrical design all combine to impart a dignity that is seldom
associated with tsuba of this group.

This is a beautiful tsuba, and well-deserving of its tokubetsu hozon
status. I am hesitant to give a firm time period. On the one hand,
because of the introduction of better metal-working tools, those
Namban tsuba of a later period are generally the more skillfully
made. Conversely, those with conventional seppa-dai were often
earlier. I imagine that the kanteisho says 'Edo period'? Shall we
say mid-late C18?

Regards, John L.

P.S. I hope that you enjoy the book.

by drjohnplissenden

Dear Barry

I am sorry, but I don't recall seeing the images of your Namban tsuba
before and I am unable to find them in my 'in box'. Is a green paper
from the NBTHK 35 years ago the equivalent of a tokubetsu kicho paper
(or perhaps it has just turned green with age!)

Namban tsuba are incredibly difficult to photograph, and this
difficulty is compounded when the metal is dark. A tsuba of this
group with a plano-convex profile is very unusual - I have certainly
never seen one. It has the ubiquitous, symmetrical design of
affrontes dragons facing the tama jewel (or is it a lotus blossom?,
with lashings of gold overlay. The alternating, perforated design
inside the mimi is a commonly found one, as is the chryanthemoid
mimi. Interestingly, the seppa-dai is only gilded on the omote
surface - I sometimes wonder how well would the seppa sit on a seppa-
dai such as this. The kodzuka-hitsu is clearly a later addition.

The photographs are much too dark to enable any deatailed assessment
of the carving and undercutting, but it appears to be of quite good
quality.

Date? Who am I to argue with 1670, but that seems to be a pretty
exact attribution. Perhaps I would put it rather later - what does
the kanteisho say?

Regards, John.
