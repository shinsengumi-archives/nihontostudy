Re: [nihontostudy] Re: more shinsa question for Jim

by yamaimojg

Milt and Rich,
Later inlay is a case by case judgment. Because the originality of the tsuba is compromised, there will usually be a significant point reduction for later inlay to a good, older iron plate. If the inlay is extensive it will probably fail. If it is an attempt to "convert" the tsuba to a different type (even if mumei), or is very crude or done after the Edo period, it will fail.

A hypothetical good Muromachi period Tosho guard with a subtle mon inlaid during the Edo period would probably pass, but with a much lower point level that the unaltered plate would have been awarded. Also, if there is significant alteration to the piece, the date given will be that of the latest work.

Most of the late gomoku zogan, mukade, etc., will fail for poor quality. Maybe an exceptional example could pass, but I can't think of any examples. Does anyone have one of these guards with an origami?

My observations only, not endorsed by anyone.
Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Kozuka

by yamaimojg

Rich,
Kyo kanagu shi is 京 Kyoto 金具 metal fittings 帥 maker. (Although dictionary definitions of this shi kanji are more like master or commander, I haven't seen it translated that way for this compound term.) I have seen it used as an attribution on both NTHK and NBTHK origami.

The meaning sounds rather similar to Kyo Kinko (京金工), or Kyoto metalsmith, but in practice usually designates somewhat lower quality work. A lot of mumei Edo period kozuka and kogai made in Goto style, but not actually Goto work, will get an attribution to Kyo Kinko. Something that deviates further from Goto style and is a bit lower quality will be called Kyo Kanagushi. That's not an exhaustive definition, but I hope it gives you some idea.

A lot of Edo period kinko material isn't distinctive enough to allow an attribution to an individual or even a group. For another example, a lot of later, average, unsigned menuki will be attributed to Kyo Kanagushi. It's not necessarily bad stuff, but there's no way to identify it further.

Your kozuka is probably borderline for passing due to the quality and condition being a bit rough, but if it did, the attribution would likely be Kyo kanagu shi based on what I've observed.

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Kozuka

by yamaimojg

Uttori is a technique where the metal foil was attached without soldering by crimping/staking/pushing the edges of the gold or silver around the "design" into the base metal. It was popular in the Muromachi and Momoyama periods and then died out rather abruptly. The problem with uttori is that once the foil is worn through in a spot there is nothing to stop the edges from being peeled back and the foil pulled away from wear.

The uttori technique was replaced by various iroe technologies where the metal foils were attached to the base by soldering or other "glue" techniques. It wears better, but it isn't quite as beautiful as uttori to those of us who like the old stuff. Most iroe gold isn't as thick as uttori, perhaps because it doesn't have to be so strong to withstand the forming and crimping (and thick is expensive). Still, some iroe can be rather thick, so the distinction in the technique is in how the foil is attached rather than the thickness.

There is a series to be found in Goto, Waki Goto, Kyo Kinko and Kyo Kanagushi. Uttori would point toward Ko-Goto (pre Edo Goto). The quality of the base metal is one key in differentiating the levels -- true black shakudo at one end grading into a brown alloy at the other. The quality of the carving is of course very important as is that of the inlay. It is all case by case as many people at different levels copied and modified classic Goto motifs.

Jim
