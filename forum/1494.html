by yamaimojg

Matt,
Yujo refers to Goto Yujo, the first generation. I hadn't noticed the term Yujo yasuri before, although as I mentioned in an earlier post he does seem to be the first one applying fine sujigai yasuri to gold kogai, at least as far as surviving and published examples go.

Checking Kajihara's glossary again, the terms Yujo-yasuri habaki and koshi-Yujo habaki are listed in the habaki section on page 146. There is no drawing for a Yujo-yasuri habaki, just some text. There is a drawing of a koshi-Yujo habaki, which looks like the ones you pointed out. Both sources are saying that the fine filework on the lower half are the Yujo-yasuri. I think that the work on the upper part is what is usually referred to as cat scratch. Although Kajihara describes the term nekogaki, he doesn't provide an illustration.

I have never seen file work by Yujo or other Goto makers that had the raised and burnished burs that appear on these habaki. Their file marks are fine and smooth. Probably a somewhat different tool and/or technique. This work with the raised bumps is quite typical of habaki, but I have seen it on the backs of some machibori kodogu.

Oddly the text in Kajihara about Yujo yasuri habaki does not appear in the English translation volume. It says something like that this is a kind of ji-yasuri habaki that is called Yujo-yasuri because it is like the style of yasuri particular to Yujo. The koshi-Yujo habaki is based on this.

If someone knows more about this or other habaki lore, please let us know.

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Cat scratches - yasurimei on habaki

by Barry Thomas

Jim Gilbert wrote:

> If someone knows more about this or other habaki lore, please let us know.

Well, not so much lore, but "just another thought". One of the great things I
have admired in Nihonto is that even the apparently decorative always has a
practical purpose. The taper on a habaki performs the same function in the saya
as does the taper on a car's rear axle and hub. It "locks", requiring some
force to break the "union" of the two parts. The "cat scratches/rain" roughen
the surface which would allow more "bite" - hence locking force - of a habaki in
the saya mouth.

How many of you have seen a still well-fitting habaki in an antique koshirae
where the sword can be held upside down and vigouously shaken without the blade
falling out?? Magnificent work of the artisans.

Barry Thomas.

Re: [nihontostudy] Nanban Tsuba

by yamaimojg

Matt,
The variety of terms used to describe tsuba decorated in foreign style can be confusing (Nanban, Canton, Kagonami, etc.). Of course Hizen, and Nagasaki in particular, was the major point of contact with foreigners and their goods. Guards made in Europe or China would have been seen first in Hizen. A lot of the foreign-influenced styles of "nanban tsuba" were probably initially made in Hizen. The "dragon and vines" type is so numerous that it was probably mass produced in other parts of Japan.

I'm sure that you can find exceptions in the usage, but most of the time the term Hizen is used for solid plate, iron guards with profuse, showy silver or multi-color nunome. The motifs are often foreign-influenced, the plates are sometimes cupped like European guards, and they do share some decorative traits with the "dragon and vines" type like dragon motifs and a rectangular seppa dai (although in the case of the Hizen tsuba, it's usually just outlined with nunome). I don't think I've ever seen the dragon and vines type called Hizen.

There seems to be some consensus that the earliest nanban tsuba from the late 16th to early 17th C were made outside of Japan. I think that is what Graham is talking about and they are rather rare. The huge majority of pieces that we see in "dragon in vines" style are later and made in Japan.

Foreign influence found its way into arts beyond tsuba. There were various nanban-style wares made in Japan, lacquer chests and furnishings (even nanban saya), clothing, painting, etc. The nanban items made in Japan must far outnumber the imported ones that inspired them.

Jim
