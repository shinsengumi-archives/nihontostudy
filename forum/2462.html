Re: [nihontostudy] back on track with a tsuba question

by yamaimojg

Rich,
Pieces like that seem to have been used to change the shape of the seppa dai to accommodate a certain shape and/or size of saya and tsuka. There are tsuba where the seppa dai has been enlarged by fitting pieces all of the way around. Sometimes various pieces (including the more common hitsuana plugs) are inserted by a later collector to restore the look of the guard to something like what he believes to be its original state.

I could imagine a piece like being inserted to repair damage if it were somewhere else on the guard. It's hard to imagine how that particular spot would be damaged.

I have seen a fair number of tsuba that have a pronounced asymmetry to the shape of the seppa dai in that location. There is metal "missing" from the same position at the upper right. My guess is that at some time and/or place a slightly asymmetrical tsuka or saya shape was in style and that tsuba were either made or modified to accommodate that shape. I haven't come across such a koshirae and not being a sword user I can't guess as to why such a shape might be desirable.

The insert on your guard might be an attempt to change one of these tsuba to the more common symmetrical oval shape.

Any other ideas?

Jim

by drjohnplissenden

Dear Rich

At the risk of climbing onto my personal hobby horse, I cannot
accept the suggestion that the illustrated tsuba is Namban. Even
accepting the redundant definition of Namban tsuba as being 'work
exhibiting foreign influences',I am unable to reconise any foreign -
even Chinese - influences on this guard. Even were such influences
be present, it would surely qualify as Hizen rather than as Namban
work?

My impression, although I would hate to have to present a reasoned
argument for it, is rather of Miochin work. Is this an acceptable
label?

Regards, John L.

by yamaimojg

Hirado Kunishige and others made nanban-style fittings in brass decorated with nonsense inscriptions in roman characters. Some of the letters are correct, but others are upside down, backwards or simply made up. Some resemble Greek letters.

This isn't Kunishige's work and the characters, whatever they are, are more "freestyle" than is usually seen in that type of nanban work. A couple of the characters do resemble tensho script. It's not one of the common nanban styles, but dote mimi examples do exist and the iron has the grainy quality seen in a lot of nanban tsuba.

Late Hoan would usually be etched rather than carved. The iron, finishing and carving don't look like Myochin, although they did make small, bold guards. As always, seeing it in person could give a different impression.
