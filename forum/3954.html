by kdw21620

I think I can answer some of this. Just like in
swords, making your own steel stopped. You may have
heard of "factory plate". Basically, you could buy
the steel to make the tsuba, and many schools/artists
got away from the plate and focused more on the
decoration. From my reading, the truly outstanding
smiths were able to do both: have a great plate while
having a superb design. As a student of tsuba you
need to concentrate on the plate and not get
sidetracked by fancy stuff (poorly paraphrazing some
sensei)
Ken

Something which has bothered me for some time now is the lack of
historic record keeping of the early tsubako (pre Edo). With the
exception of the Goto there doesn't seem to be too much of a record.
Now there was a record of the sword smiths. This is why I question
the 'relative' value of the tsubako before Edo. After the civil war
period artistic merit became more important and all of a sudden we
see records of the tsubako. It may be possible that we are
dogmatizing the whole iron tsuba field, in other words making it more
important than it was ever viewed by the people of those times. Now
it is also important to remember that there is nothing wrong with
recognizing artistic beauty where it was not appreciated before. I
just have to wonder if sometimes we are overdoing it, that's all.
The 'beautiful' tekotsu bumplies on the mimi were just not worth the
time or effort to smooth off, and the 'surface texturing' was done to
hide the irregularities in the metal.

A couple of quick things to think about...

Note that some of the first tsuba makers to sign their names made work with just these irregularities, and their work was highly valued. Nobuie, Hoan, Yamakichibei.

Also note the tea wares that were in fashion at the time and very highly valued as well. Raku, Shino, Oribe. Worth looking up if you're not familiar with ceramics.

It is true in tea ware at the time that earlier, purely utilitarian, non-art pieces were sometimes elevated to high regard and staggering monetary value.

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Dem Bones...

by yamaimojg

Milt,
Agreed, Sharaku prices are positively Tokubetsu Juyo.

1. One or two tekkotsu are usually more interesting than none. Most good pieces of the types that have tekkotsu will have them distributed here and there around the rim. Some pieces are positively bristling with them, and while it's a matter of taste it can get to be a bit much. While one or two small bones may not have much carry much "aesthetic weight" they are a pretty good clue that the iron is not just homogenous factory plate.

2. Yes, they're mostly visible on the rim only, although Kanayama, Yamakichi, Hoan, etc., will show them on the face as well. I think that it may be a function more of how the plate is finished than the forging. If you file/grind/sand the surface down, the bones aren't going to be visible unless some later process (rust, wear, ?) brings them out. I think that where to "put" or "leave" bones was an aesthetic choice.

3. There are running bones and there is masame hada. The ones that really look like masame hada are just the layers in the iron. I think typically if they stand out the guard was finished with some sort of etching process, or the layers were brought out by rusting. A running bone is not as obvious or as uniform as masame hada. It's a true tekkotsu that will be visible for a centimeter or few around the rim. It is also probably following a higher carbon layer, but it's not as obvious as a masame hada. It's frequently seen in early Akasaka.

4. Without seeing the piece I don't know. I've never seen inlay used to fill in voids in the forging, though. On the other hand, if some of the gold fell out, you will still see the slots cut for the inlay. Nunome should be a definite but shallow cross hatch (cloth) pattern. Of course hira-zogan or other techniques would be small cuts.

Short form answers, but I hope that gets at your questions.

Jim
