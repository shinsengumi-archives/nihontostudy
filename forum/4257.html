Menuki Posts -- interesting link

by klepet33319

For anyone interested in this question please go to:
http://world.choshuya.co.jp/
If you sign up for their member's only page they have several
very high end pieces including a Soumin Futatokoromono, the menuki
which exhibit exactly the post arangement I am asking about. As
Soumin studied under Goto masters and was one of the founders of
Machibori I find it interesting he here is using the open/closed end
posts such as the mainline Goto masters often used. Having come
across an article by Thomas B. Buttweiler, 'The History of Pre-Edo
Menuki', Token TaiKai 1976, on pp. 62 & 63 the following is noted:

"By the end of the Dolmen Period the Tsuka ornaments had been
modified to function as Mekugi. Because of the constraints placed on
Menuki, the size was reduced and the design became rounded and more
compact. These first Menuki were made to be an integral part of the
Mekugi. The solid pin of one Menuki extended through the Mekugi-Ana
of the sword and was capped on the other side of the Tsuka by the
hollow cylindrical pin of the matching Menuki. This hollow-solid
cylindrical arrangement remained standard in the Goto School for five
hundred years after the Menuki ceased to be an integral part of the
Mekugi.

By the end of the Nara Period the wrapped Tsuka came into common
use. This inovation sounded the death knell of the integral Mekugi-
Menuki. Since the tight wrapping allowed virtually no space between
adjacent turns, the Mekugi-Menuki had to be mounted over the Tsuka-
Ito. The softness of the wrap allowed the pins to move relative to
one another. The subsequent metal to metal wear, resulted in loss of
adhesion, with the possible disappearance of one Menuki. An
alternate method of external mounting was attempted. This consisted
of mounting the two Menuki on an independent wooden Mekugi. The
mounting was accomplished using a hollow cylindrical pin in each
Menuki, to fit over the Mekugi proper. This approach was doomed to
failure for the same reasons stated earlier. The double hollow
cylindrical pin arrangement, remained as a vestige of this mounting
technique, until the Muromachi Period, in the Ezo School.

These two attempts at using externally mounted Menuki, either
attached to or made part of the Mekugi, can only be classed as
transitional. From a functional standpoint they were doomed to
failure from the onset.

Even though the Menuki could not be mounted as part of the
mekugi, tradition dictated that the Menuki be retained. They were
inserted under the wraps using the rather feeble justifiaction that
their use improved the warriors grip on the sword.

Aside from the debate over the dubious functionality of wrapped
Menuki, they had, by the end of the Heian Period, become an
established member of the compliment of Kodogu. Their status has
remained, except for variation of motif, unchanged up to the present
day".

I still have to wonder if there is a known justification for the
inclusion of this arrangement or if it is simply a nod to tradition?
