Re: [nihontostudy] Early sukashi tsuba attribution

by yamaimojg

Ron,
The broad seppa dai and the shape of the hitsuana are seen in Owari tsuba. The style of the sukashi is more like Kyo work, though. It's difficult to judge iron from photographs, but it doesn't look typically Owari. I've seen guards that looked quite like Owari be judged as Shoami because of uncertainty over the lack of some classic characteristic or other. Some of these sukashi pieces can be very difficult to pigeonhole with confidence because they share characteristics of Kyo and Owari. My guess is that your guard would be attributed to Shoami. Probably early Edo period or a bit older.

The other guard is interesting. Some of that type have plates like Kamakura bori and have a simple carving around the rim. Others have plates more like Onin guards. Both types can have a simple ring or two of brass inlay. I don't think I've seen an example that has the rim hammered like yours, but aesthetically it seems like the same idea as the carved decoration seen on others.

The style of the plate, the shape of the kozuka ana and the simple inlay decoration would all point to an Onin attribution. How thick is it? Probably mid Muromachi at least.

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Early sukashi tsuba attribution

by yamaimojg

Matt,
Very good calls -- well done. However, I have to say that Akasaka would be difficult to defend. That mokko shape would be very unusual for Akasaka. Their seppa dai tended to be more narrow and were often somewhat to very pointed at the top in the early generations. The broad shape would be, again unusual. They didn't normally do quadrilateral symmetry like that and the hitsuana are also not typical, although Tadashige often made "dovetails" for (often non-existent) sekigane like we see here on the kozuka ana side. Akasaka construction often used a hard, smooth kawagane that looks quite different from the texture of the iron on this guard. The rim is usually very round in the early generations.

BUT... there are a bunch of signed Akasaka guards by later generations that are not obviously in Akasaka style based on the carving, motifs and even inlay. It's an interesting study. The first Akasaka died around 1657, FWIW. Pretty sure it's not an Akasaka piece, though.

Writing opinions is a great way to learn. Keep it up and don't worry about the feet. You did good.

Jim
