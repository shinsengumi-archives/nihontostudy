Katana/nagamaki with naginata-hi and blade inscriptiion

by sigp210

Hi All,
Pardon the cross posting on the forums, but I am looking for info on a
rather unusual sword.
A friend has owned this sword for many years that I quite fancy. Very
long nagasa, and appears to be o-surige. Has an o-kissaki, and well
cut naginata-type hi. Nakago looks to be around shinshinto period, but
just my novice opinion.
What is odd about the sword (apart from the fact that it looks like a
nagamaki blade made into a katana) is the inscription on the blade on
both sides.
Sorry for the bad pics. Only had 5 minutes to take these limited pics
today, and had to do them in segments in bad light. The sword has a
beefy blade, with some weight to it, and the hi are very nicely cut.
Hamon is not very visible in the pics, but is clearly visible in
person, and quite wide suguha.
Anyways, a translation done very long ago supposedly came up with
something along the lines of "I am so sharp, I can cut clouds" but I
have strong doubts about this.
Looking for a translation from anyone please.
What would the significance of the inscription be, and could it be a
gift sword or a dedication, or a commission blade? Fittings are nice
tachi mounts, and the blade doesn't appear to my humble eyes to be a
junk sword. Original nagasa would be estimated at well over 28"
originally.

A post on another forum gave the following helpful info:
"This inscription is most likely a reference to something found in
Chinese literature or Chinese mythology. I say this because its not
made up of typical Japanese compounds and doesn't resemble any
Buddhist phrases that one typically sees on swords. I think the 3rd
and 4th kanji combine to form the word "lightning" in Chinese
(somebody correct me if I'm wrong), but the word for lightning in
Japanese uses a different kanji. Read kanji-for-kanji as Japanese you
end up with a jumble of word roots. The kanji on the left are
剣光閃電鞭刀輝破浮雲
When translated it reads; sword, light, lightning (taking the Chinese
reading), whip, katana, brilliant, rupture, floating, and cloud. It
doesn't take much imagination to read this as you have stated in your
post ("Lightning-flashing, cloud-rupturing sword"). But as I say its
probably a direct reference to a particular myth or poem, which
contains this exact phrase. The owner would then have the added bonus
of appearing quite erudite and well-read in the Chinese classics. I
think samurai were keen on secret societies and this inscription may
have some significance in this context as well. In any event its not a
phrase that you would encounter in Japanese, which leads me to believe
it's a Chinese literary reference."


Thanks in advance.

Brian

Ah, the dreaded red lacquer in the grooves - in this case, the kanji. The
characters are neither well-cut nor well-balanced, IMHO. To me this looks like
a Shinshinto crapper with red lacquer in the kanji!!! IMHO the kanji are not
well carved, as I said, and the whole thing reeks of a tourist trap to me!!! At
best it is a genuine Boshin War piece, but the quality doesn't strike me from
these pics as anything marvellous. See the two ware evident in the picture, for
example. Also, the hamon looks weak. It is always better to have a sword "in
the hand", but as opinions are being sought on the basis of the pictures I have
given the best opinion I'm capable of. It would have been helpful to see the
nakago and boshi as well to augment "the story" as we need to see a sword in its
totality, and not just a "selected slice"... Oh, congratulations to whoever
stitched the images together. Nice job.
