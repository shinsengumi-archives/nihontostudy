G'day Rich -

My opinions are based largely on "gut feeling" when seeing the guard since, on photographs at least, these groups do resemble each other. Having the tsuba in-hand would of course make for a stronger argument for (or against) the groups mentioned, I would think.

Maybe I shouldn't had called the edge treatment "bones" but it would be the lines visiable from the three-fold construction of Akasaka. Last year I purchased a wonderful "willow tree" iron sukashi tsuba, mumei--one of my "dream" tsuba ever since Sasano illustrated this motif in his sukashi tsuba book. I felt it was Akasaka and it did have these linear lines (bones?) in its edge. Jim Gilbert and Henrik Eschbaum...

(Was checking a spelling and accidently "sent" this...hope I got the right name here!)

Anyway, they did agree to Akasaka and pointed out this layering which was quite evident on this tsuba. Of course, I was thrilled. In this case the guard could had been Kyo-sukashi or even Higo. It brought some nice discussion.

As for Tosa Myochin, I have not had a lot of experience with their work but do think the plate might not be a "good" compared to Akasaka. They also often used the "mustache" type chiseling around the nakago-ana. Your tsuba does not show this (nor does one I have that is signed Tosa Myochin). When I see the surface decoration as is in your tsuba, I must admit it shoots me to Nishigaki group. Interesting to compare this treatment of the various Higo groups illustrated in Sasano's books.

Exciting tsuba, all!! Again, sorry for the incomplete earlier email.

Ron
