Peter,
The original metal fittings appear to be a set by one maker or at least a single workshop. I think that they could be work from Shonai in northern Honshu. Is the fuchi or tsuba signed? The kozuka is a later substitution of lower quality. I can't really see the menuki in the photos. The kashira appears to be of a different material. I'm guessing that it may have been lost and that the tsuka has been rewrapped, but I can't see much in the photos.

The bevel on the saya would be up against the body when the sword was worn so it's probably not just for show. My guess is that it might be more comfortable to wear or would help position it somehow (?). It's an unusual feature. Does anyone know what it's called or what it is really for?

As a lone wakizashi it could have been worn by a non-samurai, but I don't see anything about the koshirae that would make that necessarily so.

Jim

Jim,

Digging up an old post here as I've got some additional info from Steve
Smith fittings expert from Liverpool Museum & British Northern Token
Society.

http://www.c.farrar.btinternet.co.uk/wak/

The mounts are the work of Sekibun Akabumi or his brother of the same name.
Mid 19th Century. Liverpool Museum has mounts of identical type in its
inventory.

The mystery of the beveled saya has also now been answered by Steve. The
bevel is because the Wak was part of a daisho the katana would also have a
bevel so the two sit tightly together in the belt. This also confirms the
blade was carried by a Samurai!

http://www.c.farrar.btinternet.co.uk/wak/Dscf0073.jpg

Peter

by ashigaru1

hey guys,

also, flat side of saya could rest against the body for a snug fit.
there were no "rules" as such, about how to carry daisho, except
always on left side. Wakizashi, or uchikatana in the tachi wearing
days, would stick out where comfortable for warrior to access. very
much an individual thing. do not look at late Edo period photos for
answer, either . those last samurai had never known war. The peace
of the Togagawa Shogunate had the unfortunate effect of diluting the
spirit of Bushido, and causing the diminished quality of swords made,
as well as spawning scores of schools for teaching martial arts, as
the best teacher --- combat, war --- was reduced to duels, or
criminal behavior.

Techniques of forging were lost. The past was mythologized,
idealized. The arts flowered, but the high ranking samurai stagnated.
many low ranking samurai, often for financial reasons, became
merchants, artisans, even farmers [returning to their roots, for some]
Many of the traditions we assume were common since the Kamakura Era
were really just idealizations written down in late Shinto or
ShinShinto times [1700-1868] times as the moribund samurai of the Edo
Era tried to keep the spirit of the 1st samurai, [though they were
not called that in 670 AD] the Emperor Temmu, alive.

doug e
