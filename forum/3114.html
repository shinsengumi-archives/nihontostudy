Heianjo Sukashi questions

by davidzincavage

I'm a bit puzzled about Heianjo sukashi tsubas. Why do you suppose it is that neither Torigoye nor Sasano discuss them? Heianjo, in this case, I would assume, just means they were made in Kyoto. So, how would Heianjo sukashi be different from Kyo sukashi? How does one identify these?

Re: [nihontostudy] Heianjo Sukashi questions

by neobliviscar

I think I asked this same question of Jim or somebody
else not long ago. It confused me a bit regarding a
tsuba I was studying at the time.

My understanding is that it is more of an age
division. For example, exact same Sasano tsuba is
photographed in 2 books. It is a mid 1500s piece. In
the older book it is called 'Heian Jo Sukashi'. In
another book it is called 'Kyo Sukashi' and the age is
denoted in the subsequent description.
I suppose you could call it a "ko kyo" sukashi, but
sounds odd to me.

I would have thought Richard George's first tsuba
could be called "Heianjo Sukashi" or late Muromachi.


Curran

Re: [nihontostudy] Heianjo Sukashi questions

by robsongord

Hi,

The old term Heianjo Sukashi is no longer in use having been replaced
by Kyo-Sukushi. If you have an older book, you will still see this
term. This also helps avoid confusion with Heianjo tsuba, which are
typically thin iron plates with brass inlays.

Gordon

My explanation was based on Japanese sources and Japanese usage. Wakayama
sensei in particular did not like the use of Heianjo sukashi. I would hope
that Sothebys is not got the final say on how tsuba are described.

Gordon
