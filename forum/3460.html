A good story about one tsuba with some interesting details missing

by sosidore

Dear all,

I have this interesting tsuba posted on Nihontostudy at:

All Albums | Photo Albums > Sosidore > Iron Tsuba Dragon

Anyone well versed in Chinese artifacts knows this tsuba is an
unusual one. I would like to give some clue so that readers of this
message would came up with similar beliefe as I did.

1. Chinese emperor was the only one who is entitled to put dragon
decors on everything he owned.
2. However dragons could also put to use as icons by other persons or
entities only if the number of dragon's fingers reduced to either 4
or 3. Five fingers claws must not be used in any dragon unless the
owner of that dragon design is Chinese emperor himself. Violation of
such rule was subject to capital punishment.
3. There used to be a continued flow of sword import from Japan
dating back more than a thousand years.
4. Swords as possibly one of the contributions to Chinese emperor
during Qing Dynasty would demand unique design for their tsubas.
Guess what design would it use?
5. Any contribution to the king would bear no signature of the
artist. Guess what? This elegant iron tsuba bear no signature on
neither side.
6. I am in no position to do the authentication on the style of the
dragon shown on this tsuba. Somehow its style poised to resemble the
one shown on Meiji coins. There is strong likelihood that this tsuba
was of Japan origin dating back to late 19th century.
7. Guess who is in the position in Japan to offer this tsuba as part
of a sword to Chinese emperor then?

I hope you enjoy as much as I when pondering the answers to those
questions. You probably would find some interesting part of details
missing. Readers and I would like to hear them.

Now, it is time that tsuba expert and historians take over
authentication processes from here.

Chen, Ying-chaw
Taipei

It was Japan who kept the record in detail about presenting swords to
Chinese emperors during 15th century. (Reference -----in Chinese:
http://www.chinadaojian.com.cn/ctdj/chinadj/200510/140.html
Historic Record on Marshal Art --Swords and Knife-- Exchange Between
China and Japan. -----written in Chinese citing among others Japanese
record)
1. One sword to Chinese emperor (Ming dynasty) in 1401.
2. Again, 100 swords among 202 pieces of varieties of swords
in 1403 had been presented to Chinese emperor
3. A total of 1200 swords had been presented to Chinese
emperor before Ming dynasty ended in 1644.
I think there is a strong likelihood that Japanese swords remained an
important item among list of gifts presented to Chinese emperor until
mid 19th century.
Time and again in China, very fine quality old Japanese swords (for
royal use?) can still be found. Some with tsuba missing
How about royal tsuba?
So far this dragon design tsuba seems to be the first one that
appears to fit an emperor¡¯s sword pretty well.

p.s. This tsuba was not cast. There is no trace of casting work can
be found on this piece.

So the question really is which emperor in Chinese history was the
most probable receiver of this tsuba (once fit itself on a beautiful
sword as a gift)? Could there be historical record about it that
might still be well kept in Japan?

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: A good story about one tsuba with some interesting details missing

by Barry Thomas

Mr Chen Ying-chaw wrote:

> It was Japan who kept the record in detail about presenting
> swords to Chinese emperors during 15th century. (Reference
> -----in Chinese:
> http://www.chinadaojian.com.cn/ctdj/chinadj/200510/140.html
> Historic Record on Marshal Art --Swords and Knife-- Exchange
> Between China and Japan. -----written in Chinese citing among
> others Japanese record)

Yes, of course Mr Chen Ying-chaw, you are right. I believe this is known as the
Kango Trade in the Kamakura period and later. This was published in English
about 25 or so years ago in the USA - one of the Tai Kai publications. I had not
read of the Trade in the early Shinto period/late Ming, but of course this is
not a well-researched area in the West, nor, I guess, in China either given the
vicissitudes of the past 110 years. With this lack of knowledge it isn't
surprising that no one (?) on this list knows anything about such a tsuba as Mr
Chen Ying-chaw has drawn our attention to. So, given this lack of specific
knowledge about the Japan-China trade in Nihonto I can only go by the tsuba AS A
TSUBA in the context of my Japanese-centred knowledge. More and better quality
images of this very interesting tsuba are warranted given the queries and issues
raised.

I've looked long and hard at the very interesting image Mr Chen Ying-chaw has
drawn our attention to, and I note the following:

- the quality of the work is poor.
- note particularly the asymmetric shape of the nakago ana, as I have mentioned
- note also the clumsy, unrefined shape of the seppa dai outline
- note also the nakago ana placement in the seppa dai lacks symmetry.
- finally, the mimi (rim) varies in width in a manner most uncharacteristic of
Japanese tsubako.

Being familiar with the exquisite excellence of Japanese metal work in tsuba and
fuchigashira, I cannot accept that this tsuba was made under the auspices of the
Japanese Emperor for the Chinese Emperor. Let me propose another theory.

Mr Chen Ying-chaw notes:

> 1. Chinese emperor was the only one who is entitled
> to put dragon decors on everything he owned.
> 2. However dragons could also put to use as icons by
> other persons or entities only if the number of dragon's
> fingers reduced to either 4 or 3. Five fingers claws must
> not be used in any dragon unless the owner of that
> dragon design is Chinese emperor himself. Violation of
> such rule was subject to capital punishment.

However, in Japan the dragon is a very common theme on all manner of things.
Granted I have only seen 3 fingered dragons in Japanese art, but the so-called
Canton tsuba were imported into Japan and I suggest that this tsuba could be of
Chinese origin intended to be exported to Japan at one time. Supporting this
theory is the observation that this tsuba appears to have never been fitted to a
sword, as indeed a great number of Japanese tsuba were never fitted to swords.
I cannot recall seeing hitsuana of the specific type shown in the image.

Finally, Mr Chen Ying-chaw wrote:

> Time and again in China, very fine quality old Japanese
> swords (for royal use?) can still be found. Some with
> tsuba missing

Some years ago there was a very comprehensive (but not particularly
informative!) exchange of views on Nihonto in China on these sword lists - I
still have the correspondence in my archive. There was no conclusive
information of "very fine quality old Japanese swords" being found in China.
I'm sure we would all like to hear more about such finds with pictures and
scholarly information if they exist. Indeed, the article on the Kango Trade
speculated that many of the very best quality Koto were exported to China and
hoped that some could still be found and restored to the world.

This exhausts my poor and inadequate opinion, so now I will retire from the
lists and hope someone can offer a more definitive opinion. I stand by my
opinion that this is NOT a Japanese-made item.

Best regards,

Barry Thomas
(Melbourne, Australia)

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: A good story about one tsuba with some interesting details missing

by gaigin2000

Hi Barry and Mr. Chen;

Interesting thread. While there is quite a bit of evidence relating to the trade in weapons in the Muromachi period between Japan and China, the style of the tsuba you have illustrated is not of a style from this period of trade, but from a much later period. There have been many discussions trying to determine what happened to the thousands of swords exported to China during this period. Also the popular opinion is that these swords were mounted in koshira, so would have had the required tsuba and other fittings, at least on a portion of the swords exported even if not all. Seeing as the numbers exported were in the tens of thousands over a long period, it seems reasonable to think there should be a quantity of tsuba remaining from these weapons.

Here is a link to a picture of a tsuba that I think was made for this export market in Japan, and it is in the style and iron of the period used in Japan, it is the second picture from the top. If you look at the next picture it is of a similar style of tsuba but the iron is different as is the style of construction and it is from a later period of production even though of a similar style, and is about 100 years later in manufacture. This would cause one to wonder if this trade continued though the years but perhaps in smaller quantities.

http://www3.telus.net/sword/sword/index.htm/MyWebs6/tsb1gal.htm

John

Chen Ying-chaw has drawn our attention to. So, given this lack of specific
knowledge about the Japan-China trade in Nihonto I can only go by the tsuba AS A
TSUBA in the context of my Japanese-centred knowledge. More and better quality
images of this very interesting tsuba are warranted given the queries and issues
raised.

I've looked long and hard at the very interesting image Mr Chen Ying-chaw has
drawn our attention to, and I note the following:

- the quality of the work is poor.
- note particularly the asymmetric shape of the nakago ana, as I have mentioned
- note also the clumsy, unrefined shape of the seppa dai outline
- note also the nakago ana placement in the seppa dai lacks symmetry.
- finally, the mimi (rim) varies in width in a manner most uncharacteristic of
Japanese tsubako.

Being familiar with the exquisite excellence of Japanese metal work in tsuba and
fuchigashira, I cannot accept that this tsuba was made under the auspices of the
Japanese Emperor for the Chinese Emperor. Let me propose another theory.

Mr Chen Ying-chaw notes:

> 1. Chinese emperor was the only one who is entitled
> to put dragon decors on everything he owned.
> 2. However dragons could also put to use as icons by
> other persons or entities only if the number of dragon's
> fingers reduced to either 4 or 3. Five fingers claws must
> not be used in any dragon unless the owner of that
> dragon design is Chinese emperor himself. Violation of
> such rule was subject to capital punishment.

However, in Japan the dragon is a very common theme on all manner of things.
Granted I have only seen 3 fingered dragons in Japanese art, but the so-called
Canton tsuba were imported into Japan and I suggest that this tsuba could be of
Chinese origin intended to be exported to Japan at one time. Supporting this
theory is the observation that this tsuba appears to have never been fitted to a
sword, as indeed a great number of Japanese tsuba were never fitted to swords.
I cannot recall seeing hitsuana of the specific type shown in the image.

Finally, Mr Chen Ying-chaw wrote:

> Time and again in China, very fine quality old Japanese
> swords (for royal use?) can still be found. Some with
> tsuba missing

Some years ago there was a very comprehensive (but not particularly
informative!) exchange of views on Nihonto in China on these sword lists - I
still have the correspondence in my archive. There was no conclusive
information of "very fine quality old Japanese swords" being found in China.
I'm sure we would all like to hear more about such finds with pictures and
scholarly information if they exist. Indeed, the article on the Kango Trade
speculated that many of the very best quality Koto were exported to China and
hoped that some could still be found and restored to the world.

This exhausts my poor and inadequate opinion, so now I will retire from the
lists and hope someone can offer a more definitive opinion. I stand by my
opinion that this is NOT a Japanese-made item.

In China it would be unthinkable to have tsuba made using dragon as
insignia. Some might challenge my view by asking, ¡°But I do find
that dragon designs appear on robes that Chinese emperor used to
wear.¡± That is quite right. Only that custom as a rule is quite
exceptional in case of tsuba. We all know that tsuba is intended for
hand protection. The tsuba will receive heavy chopping force
initiated by the enemy. In case of dragon tsuba sword, it would mean
that the emperor was getting hit each time the tsuba received chops
by the enemy. Damage of any kind on the dragon of this tsuba would
imply that the emperor¡¯s fate would soon be as damaged as the tsuba.
There never was rules prohibiting Chinese craftsman to produce tsuba
with dragon insignia on it. The idea simply would not come up in
anyone¡¯s mind in China. As a matter of fact I seriously doubt that
the Japanese sword bearing this dragon tsuba never ever reached
Chinese Palace. The Chinese Foreign Trade officer would have singled
it out from the list of Japanese contributions and confiscated the
sword along with the palms burning dragon tsuba. A sword¡¯s blade
with dragon designs engraved on it? Yes, it is possible. And I
myself can show you ancient swords to that effect. Why was it
possible? Well, blade is something that has power of hitting ones
enemy. The idea goes harmoniously well with the powerful image of an
emperor. You just could not put a Chinese emperor on a tsuba in form
of a dragon in order to expose His powerless position of getting hit
repeatedly by his enemy.
The issue of whether or not this tsuba being made in Japan really
rest with the authentication result of the dragon design itself.
There are differences in terms of detail design between Japan and
China. May be the Japanese royal artifact specialist would be more
interested to do the authentication. I strongly believe that, well
made or not, this tsuba was of Japanese origin.
I have posted two more photos of the tsuba I am referring to just in
case anyone of you would like to take a stronger view. (Photos
placed in folder named SOSIDORE)
In my previous message I once proclaimed that five claws and only
five claws can prove that the dragon concerned was exclusively
crafted for Chinese emperor. It was not true. The more I studied the
more evidence indicated that four claws or even three claws dragons
had been found to be owned by Chinese emperors. Sorry for
misinformation.

The captioned tsuba could be an artifact that can best illustrate

Japan used to send contribution to Chinese emperors since as long
ago as 8th century A.D. And swords were among the list of Japanese
contributions. In return China had Japanese delegate brought home
much much more than they gave. I did not invent stories like this.

documented. Japanese gives and takes. I wish someday Chinese
friendship contributions could be made possible for all to see.

Apart from material, workmanship and style in general, the most
obvious point in focus is the mustach of the dragon. The dragon on
this tsuba is thick with mustach. That is what so unique about it.
Please try to take a strong view on Meiji silver one-yen coin. The

on Japanese one-yen Meiji silver coin. In case of Chinese dragon,
