O-Tanto?

by bh325

Hello all,

I am a new member, I am looking for information on a Japanese edged
weapon that was gifted to me rather unexpectedly. I would like to find
out as much information as I can about it, I suspect it may be a WWII
era officer's weapon or it may be older than that. The blade polish
has been completely destroyed, and the edge has been dulled. The tsuba
is tarnished and much of the silver/gold decoration is missing. What I
know about it:

The blade is between 16-17 inches from end of tang to tip. It is
curved, but the handle is nearly straight. The handle itself has been
re-wrapped and re-covered with rayskin, and two spacers have been
added between the Habaki and the tsuba, and the tsuba and the handle
collar. These are not original, they look like they were taken from a
much larger weapon. There is no idication of a hamon. The Habaki looks
original and is copper, polished on top with diagonal file marks at
the bottom and on the sides.

The handle collar is copper and is marked with three symbols, two
characters and a "flower" that is composed of a central circle and
three semi-circular lobes. The handle cap is horn, and is original by
my information.

There are characters chisled into the tang, I attempted to take a
rubbing but it wasn't successful. They are only one one side, but the
reverse side has two small triangular notches near the top of the tang.

I realize that no one would be able to tell much from the words, but
maybe someone can inform me on how to best record and display the
needed markings/information that would allow identification or any
other information to be determined.

Thanks in advance.

Re: [nihontostudy] O-Tanto?

by neobliviscar

bh325,

You have done a good job describing it, though a
picture tells alot. If it is not possible or practical
to take a picture, you might consider taking it to a
sword club near you.

Given the size and curvature of the blade, it is
probably a wakizashi or a naginata-naoshi (a halberd
like weapon that has been cut down to make an oversize
tanto. "O-tanto" basically is a large or oversized
tanto). The mounts sound fine to me. Many of these
came back as souevinirs with WW2 veterans. As they
played with them over the years, parts seppas, etc> got mixed and matched.

Don't mess with the blade using any cleaning
chemicals. Despite the polish being gone and the edge
destroyed, there may be some life left to it.

Please share in what state or country you live, and we
will try to point you to a nearby sword society or
club.

Curran

RE: [nihontostudy] Re: O-Tanto?

by robsongord

Hi Ben,



What you have is a wakizashi. The signature reads Sanjo Munechika saku.
From the quality of the mounts and etc., I am pretty confident that you have
a sword manufactured for the export market, typically in Kuwana or some
other local area, and then sent to Yokohama to provide an inexpensive, but
very exotic souvenir for some clipper ship crewman and etc. From an artistic
and historical level, such swords are not very important. Nonetheless, they
make nice wall hangers. For the veterans, everyone knows who Sanjo Munechika
is, so there is no need to explain more, but for the newbies, most of these
export swords have the names  not signatures  of big named smiths. Sanjo
Munechika is a Heian period smith who is basically the founder of the
Yamashiro tradition. This use of big names is sort of like a street artist
signing his chalk rendered Rembrandts with Rembrandts name. No one would
mistake this for Munechikas work, it just adds a bit of interest.



Gordon

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: O-Tanto?

by neobliviscar

Ben,

Thank you for the photos.
Gordon seems to have said most of what I could say, in
that the wakizashi is not by "Munechika" but rather
probably a late 1800s blade made for export.

I see a small mune-ware in the photos that says to me
the wakizashi may be of classical construction. Hence
it is possible it was an older (but not much older)
wakizashi which had the tang reworked and a false
signature added. With the sword market hitting rock
bottom during those times, it would have been probably
easier and/or cheaper to rework an unsigned wakizashi
than go to the trouble of classically constructing
one. Hence a famous name was added.

It is unlikely the blade merits the monetary expense
of polish, but you can learn from it. The tsuba is a
nice little Higo province type thing, and has some
minor value to it.

I live in Florida and am a wee bit East Coast centric,
so regretably I do not know any sword clubs in
Arizona. Several fittings collectors live out that
way. I believe the moderator of this list (Jim
Gilbert) also lived in Arizona for a while and might
be able to direct you to local people that can tell
you more about the wakizashi.
My first Nihonto was a wakizashi very similar to
yours. I wish I'd kept the menuki from it. Not
valuable, but very unique. I've never seen another
pair like them.

Welcome to Nihonto!

Curran
