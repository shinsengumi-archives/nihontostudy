Dear Mr Hoyle

Comments on the Proposed Control of Weapons (Amendment) Regulations 2003

Thank you for your email of 14 August 2003 to Mr Chris Shea of the
Department of Justice regarding the proposed Control of Weapons
(Amendment) Regulations 2003.

Your views have been noted and will be considered by the Minister for
Police and Emergency Services when assessing what action to take on the
proposed Regulations.

I would note that the proposed Regulations propose to make swords a
"prohibited weapon". The lawful use and possession of a "prohibited
weapon" requires an Approval from the Chief Commissioner of Police (for
which a fee is charged). Persons may be exempted from this requirement
by the Governor-in-Council. A person, therefore, under the proposed
Regulations, could still lawfully own and possess a sword if, for
example, they obtained an Approval to do so from the Chief Commissioner
of Police.

The proposed Regulations will not necessitate destruction of swords. In
the event that the proposed Regulations come into operation, persons in
possession of a sword who do not obtain an Approval from the Chief
Commissioner of Police and who do not fall under an exemption granted by
the Governor-in-Council could dispose of their weapons through, for
example, an authorised weapons trader.

The Regulatory Impact Statement (RIS) which assesses the impact of the
proposed Regulations has been publicly released and I attach a copy of
the RIS for your information. Should you wish to make a further
submission on the proposed Regulations after reading the RIS (the
proposed Regulations themselves are at Attachment 1 to the RIS) you
would be most welcome to do so. The closing date for submissions is
Monday 8 September 2003. Details on how to make a submission can be
found in the RIS.

Further copies of the Regulatory Impact Statement can be accessed on the
internet at www.justice.vic.gov.au

Thank you for raising your views with the Department of Justice.

Yours sincerely

Signed

Marisa De Cicco
Director, Justice Policy

Re: [nihontostudy] FW: Regulatory Impact Statement on proposed Regulations (Ozzland sword ban!)

by davidzincavage

In the latest Guns & Ammo magazine, I was reading of a new program in which dealers in firearms and hunting equipment voluntarily donate 1% of a sale to the National Rifle Association, or offer a 1% discount to NRA members.

If we in the United States are to continue to be permitted to own Japanese swords in the future, this will happen because the National Rifle Association successfully defended the right to keep and bear arms in the firearms context. If and when private ownership of most firearms is prohibited, it will only be a short time (as the case of Australia demonstrates) before the same policies are extended to other collectable weapons, including Nihonto.

Obviously, complete political mobilization of every single Nihonto collector in the United States would produce no effective result. That constituency is far too small to have any significant influence. The real battle will be fought and decided over gun control. Japanese swords will be only a small regulatory afterthought. Nihonto collectors need to reflect, and realize that the NRA is even now fighting their fight too. Every Japanese sword owner should join and support the NRA, and I would suggest that the sword dealing community give serious thought to subscribing to that 1% to the NRA program.

www.nra.org


David Zincavage
Life Member, NRA

Re: [nihontostudy] FW: Regulatory Impact Statement on proposed Regulations (Ozzland sword ban!)

by davidzincavage

I don't think Nihonto is particularly on the NRA's radar specifically. But it is the same issue fundamentally, the private possession of arms, whether the particular arms under consideration consist of the Pennsylvania farmer's hunting rifle, or the SF Bay area tech mogul's Ichimonji daito. My point was that the most effective lobbying organization on the basic issue is the National Rifle Association, whose membership typically runs between three and four million, and whose membership is well know to turn out and vote.

If the NRA loses the fight over so-called assault weapons, then they will come after handguns. If we lose on handguns, then they will go after rifles and shotguns. And when all the firearms are on the prohibited list, as in Australia, swords, including Nihonto, will be added. Those who collect antique arms would be well advised to participate in the fight now while they still have a large enough general constituency of hunters, target shooters, and ordinary firearms owners on the same side to constitute a constituency capable of making an electoral impact, and really influencing the politics of the situation.

I don't imagine that the NRA's organizational leadership is very conscious of the Nihonto collecting community at the present time, but I'm sure the NRA philosophically regards the right to own and collect Nihonto as part of the same general right to keep and bear arms it devotes most of its energy and resources toward defending. If American Nihonto organizations were inclined to work with the NRA on Second Amendment issues, I'm sure that the NRA would welcome their assistance.

I don't know the inside story, but I seem to recall that the NRA was involved in publishing Man at Arms, the general arms collecting magazine, recently for some period of time, presumably to keep that journal afloat. A feature on Japanese swords might not fit in terribly well in the American Rifleman, but would certainly be entirely appropriate to Man at Arms.

Just to clarify: in the US, bans against assault weapons typically have targeted ugly, military style long arms which do not actually meet the real definition of an assault weapon. An assault weapon [translation of the German: sturmgewehr] is really, definitionally speaking, a long arm, firing a cartridge intermediate in power between conventional pistol and rifle cartridges, capable of both full and semi-automatic fire. Arms included in US "assault weapon" bans usually, but not necessarily, resemble the exterior of assault weapons, but, since fully automatic weapons have been federally restricted since the 1930s, are merely semi-automatics. Some shotguns, guilty of military appearance, have been included in such bans.

I don't know positively, but I think the NRA has filed amicus briefs on some knife cases. Were litigation on Nihonto underway, I think it would be highly likely that the NRA, and other gun rights organizations, would be willing to assist.

Personally, I think the case of Australia illustrates very clearly what will happen to weapons collectors who are sufficiently in the minority, and who fail to organize in time. Bans of private weapons are feel-good symbolic measures giving politicians the opportunity to be seen taking action. In the modern world, a serious and potent animosity toward private ownership of weapons exists on the part of a substantial and highly influential portion of the urban population. This animosity is linked to the spirit of reformist ameliorism, the very popular belief that a specific solution by governmental diktat exists to any identifiable problem or ill. Its roots lie in centuries old ideologies of religious utopianism, which literally desire all the swords to be beaten into plowshares. And this philosophy is very popular on the political left, which yearns to increase the powers of the state, particularly the state's monopoly of force. The affluent and educated sectors of American society typically live in congested urban environments. They do not hunt or target-shoot. They are estranged from the tradition of military service and the use of arms. And they look with contempt on less educated, rural sectors of American society in which hunting, ownership of arms, and military service are highly valued traditions. The wealthy bourgeois living in a million dollar house in Palo Alto thinks it is simply criminally irresponsible to allow a bunch of rubes and other denizens of American society's lower orders to play with dangerous weapons. Who knows? one of these losers might even shoot him.
