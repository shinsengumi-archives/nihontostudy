The above link is to an interesting site in Japan of the Nihontou
Tosougou Kenkyukai. You can patch the page into the altavista link
for a 'translation' although we all know how Japanese to English
translates by now. On the left side of the page there are a series
of lectures which have many examples to see, both sword and
fittings. Lecture three has several Ezo examples.

OK Alan, here is a further quote from Buttweiler;

'The surface of Ezo Menuki were originally covered by a thick
layer of Gold applied as an amalgam. The Gold was burnished with a
rather large needle which left well defined polishing marks. The
Gold used for the overlay was of very low karat. The resulting color
is a very pale Silver-Golden hue. The Gold will frequently be worn
from the high spots leaving a black Shakudo-like patina'.

I have a feeling that much of what we see tody are the remnants of
field worn gear. This might explain the relatively poor condition of
many of these very early fittings. Fine pieces for the commanders or
Daimyo were passed on as family treasures or left to shrines. The
vast majority of the rest were simply lost to battle or to time.
