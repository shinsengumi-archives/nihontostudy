Re: Soten & Namban

by drjohnplissenden

Dear Elliot

I know of no relationship whatsoever between the Namban and the Soten
groups of tsuba. The only feature that may be considered common to
them both is some evidence of a Chinese influence. However, while
with Namban tsuba this resulted in a completely novel metalworking
technique, Soten tsuba use conventional, Japanese techniques to
illustrate historical sujects which may be of Chinese derivation.
But there a lots of differences:-

Soten tsuba came initially from a recognised 'school' of makers,
although they were later mass produced outside this. There was never
a recognised Namban 'school', the namban influence being ubiquitous.

Namban tsuba had their origin in China, were imported into Japan at
the end of the C16, and reached the peak of their popularity in the
late C18. Soten tsuba date from the early C18 and continued to be
made, as poor quality shiiremono, into the late Edo period.
(Although I have no evidence for this, I suspect that many of the
latter may also have been produced using the 'lost wax' method of
casting.)

True examples of the Namban group are characterised by extensive
openwork and undercutting, whereas with Soten tsuba, while they may
demonstrate some degree of openwork, this is not a constant feature
and never includes undercutting.

Namban tsuba commonly have profuse gold decoration, either as nunome-
zogan or fire gilding. Soten tsuba are characterised by selective
and delicate inlays of gold, silver, copper and shakudo.

Soten tsuba do not demonstrate the decorartive mimi, seppa-dai and
ryo-hitsu which characterise Namban tsuba. Signatures, although
admittedly usually gimei, feature frequently on Soten tsuba and
almost never in the Namban group.

The design on Soten tsuba is themed, being is representative of
specific characters or incidents, while that on Namban tsuba is
purely decorative.

No, I personally know of no relationship between the two groups.

Regards, John L.
