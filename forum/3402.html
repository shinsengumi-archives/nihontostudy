by retalt

Hi Petter, ok. That's a nice tsuba for $10.00, be very happy with it.

Kyo does refer to Kyoto, as Kyoto was the political hub of Japan during
the Muromachi period. So Kyo Sukashi works may have started in the mid
late 1400's but I am unsure if Kyo Shoami started that early but they
certainly began in the late Muromachi era.

You guard looks typical of the late 17th to early 18th century pieces I
think. That's just my hunch. I may be wrong.

Hitsu ana Ume were used to fill the Hitsu ana when there were no Kozuka
or Kogai used on the koshirae. It was an aesthetic thing I think. No
need for holes when there's nothing to put in them. In your tsubas case,
there would have been a Kozuka, but no Kogai, so the Kogai hole was
filled, with Shakudo covered copper mostly.

The Sekigane that fills the nakago ana is a packer or filler to ensure
the blade fits snugly on the nakago. This tsuba was most likely been
fitted to a second or even third sword in it's life.

I have seen furniture from the Meiji period were tsuba have been mounted
in door of cabinets and the likes but I don't think they wore them as
pendants if that's what you mean :-) I think they were used mostly as
tsuba. Though I think a few very special pieces ( Maybe Ko Goto and the
likes ) were probably stored away and kept in safe places.

There were not to many wars in Japan after the Tokugawa took over so it
is unlikely this tsuba has seen battle, unless a one on one.

hope that helps some.

Richard

by klepet33319

Just a little chronology with help from Dr. Torigoye.
Apparently the Ko-Shoami School developed out of the Katchushi tsubako
in the late Muromachi Jidai. After the hostilities ended with Edo
Jidai emerging there was a dispersal of the Shoami School to different
regions of Japan with the remaining Kyoto artisans being the Kyo-
Shoami. (Kyo-sukashi and Heianjo-sukashi developed in Kyoto during
Momoyama Jidai and into Edo until they more or less merged).
Just a note -- the tsuba of Petter could also very well be Bizen Shoami
as they had similar styles with the Kyo-Shoami in sukashi and nunome.
Dr. Torigoye mentioned the mimi thickness was a delineating point of
reference, the Bizen being slightly thicker. I am not certain if this
style was used in the Ko-Shoami but a note of interest is that the gold
nunome of the older pieces apparently was accomplished with greater
skill so that pieces of greater age often have more of the nunome left
as opposed to the later Edo school pieces.
