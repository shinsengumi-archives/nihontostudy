Hi -

These look pretty typical so-called Mino Goto work of what I like to call average-good quality. There are some made much lower quality and then some that are a step above these. Notice the rounded punchings around the nakago-ana; your tsuba has three at top and seven at the bottom. Most of these kind have these punchings but I seem to recall most have five at the bottom, not seven. I'm not so sure if anyone has ever come up with a reliable answer as to the meaning of these punchings but certainly, these Mino-Goto works do carry them. Age? I think these are still considered middle Edo works, but correct me if I'm wrong. Your fittings do look very nice.

Ron

Re: [nihontostudy] Help Kantei a set of fittings

by yamaimojg

Fredrik and Ron,

These are often referred to as Nagoya-mono. It's a kind of shiiremono that
copied Mino/Goto style. These fittings are often marketed as "Mino Goto,"
but the quality of the materials and workmanship is not at that level of
work.

The problem is not that they have been cleaned, but that they are not made
out of shakudo. This type of fitting is often coated with a thin black wash
to simulate shakudo, but that is often worn away from the seppa dai. The
nanako is more coarse than would be seen in genuine Mino or Goto work.
Rather than using gold inlay/overlay a thin gold wash is used. Where this
is rubbed on the high spots the underlying copper shows through. The
quality of the carving is not at the level Mino or Goto work.

As Ron says there is a continuum of this type of material ranging from very
poor to pretty good. The distinction between lower quality Mino-bori and
higher quality Nagoya-mono can be pretty fuzzy.

I doubt that the fuchigashira and tsuba are by the same person. The details
of the carving of the peony are quite different. They are the same type of
work though. They're probably from the late Edo period. There are quite a
few wakizashi mounted with this style of tosogu.

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Help Kantei a set of fittings

by yamaimojg

Ron,
The other menuki is a sho with a side-blown flute depicted behind it. The
sho a mouth-blown reed instrument used in bugaku/gagaku (along with that
type of flute, drums, etc.). Masks are used in bugaku, some with
exaggerated noses, but I don't know whether a tengu mask like that was used.

Several books mention that the bugaku mask depicting the shinto god
Sarutahiko no Mikoto is frequently confused with a tengu. I think he's more
of a big wide-nosed rather than long-nosed type, though.
