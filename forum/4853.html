Tochibata Tsuba Question

by neobliviscar

List,

I am hoping a lurker out there might be able to
comment on Tochibata tsuba. I am familiar with them as
written up on page 58-59 of Harry Watson's Nihonto
Koza translation of Kodogu Part 1.

I've seen a limited number of these over the years,
and they are distinct enough to me that I feel they
constitute a distinct _if minor_ school.

Q1: Has anyone ever seen a tsuba NBTHK / NTHK papered
to Tochibata?

Q2: Can anyone point me to further writing on this
school?

They tend to be strong solid open tsuba that remind of
Owari, are thick, yet very clean. The last I saw was
identical to the one on the bottom of NihontoKoza pg.
59 and sold as "Owari". I suspect the papering
authorities just designate them "Owari", but hope to
find an either one papered Tochibata or hope to find
another source beyond the Nihonto Koza.

Thanks in advance,

Curran

Re: Tochibata Tsuba Question

by bbmich3003

Hi Curran et al:
I have a Tochibata tsuba that I bought from a well-known Japanese
dealer. It is not papered, but, he wrote this info on the bill of
sale: " The old village of Tochihata eventually became Izumo in
Shimane Prefecture; during early Edo, the tsubako of this village
specialized in making tsuba with the ji as carved rope, hence the
name Tochihata for this type of tsuba" [mine is a roped mimi].
From this statement from him, I conjecture that a 'roped' mimi tsuba
called 'Tochibata' does't refer to a mei of "Tochibata", but to
having been made on the island of Tochibata. It is possible that
there are no tsuba with a mei "Tochibata". I welcome all corrections
to the above.
