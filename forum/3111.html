Menuki Thoughts?

by sigp210

Hi all,
I recently made an offer on a set of menuki on eBay, and got them. I'm
not a fittings guy at all, but wanted a nice pair for a future
mounting project, and as an example for study.
They were described as Goto or at least waki-goto. I doubt they are
Goto...but am wondering if anyone has any thoughts on the quality,
school etc? Are there any identifying marks on menuki that might lead
towards a certain school?
Was really attracted to them because of the fine carving work, and
detail. The colour is a nice dark brownish-black shakudo, not like the
lighting in the photo shows.
They are actually nicer than in the pics..with some very fine carving,
and detail on the toes, ribs, tail etc. Some odd marks on the
legs..which are 2 ovals with a small dot in the middle.
Middle to late edo I assume? Condition is very good. Any comments from
"menuki guys" appreciated.

http://www.image-hosting.net/images/messageboard/retalt/Menuki.jpg

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Menuki Thoughts?

by davidzincavage

Bob McCoy provides the following in an undated NCJSC fact sheet distributed with an unknown issue of To-Ron.






References:

1. "Menuki, The magnificent Miniatures" By R.B. Caldwell, Arts of Asia, Jan/Feb 1992, pp70-77

2. "Menuki, The Magnificent Miniatures of Japan" by R.B. Caldwell, JSS/US, Jul/Aug 1990, vol 22, #4, ppl 7-31

3. "Shumi no menuki" by Kanzan Sato, ed. Limited printing of 800 copies, 1969, boxed, 306 pages.

4. "Kinko Mino Bori" by Kokubo Kenichi. Published by Token Shunjo Shinbunsha, Tokyo, 1973.

5. "Swords of Japan, A visual Glossary" by Kotoken Kajihara, 500+ pages, hardbound.

6. "Fine Japanese Sword Fittings, MENUKI", by Musashiya Co, LTD, Vol 1, 162pp FIB, 1985,

7. "Fine Japanese Sword Fittings, MENUKT', by Musashiya Co, LTD, Vol 2, 146pp FIB, 1994,

8. "Nihon to Koza, Kodogu Part 1, Volume VI", 1993, 481pp HB, translated by Harry AFU Watson



There was also another color four page fold-out, dated 2002/1, distributed with an issue of To-Ron a bit later.

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Menuki Thoughts?

by sworddad

Hi -

One of my favorite articles on menuki was presented in Token Taikai, a booklet containing transcripts of eleven papers presented during the 1976 Token Taikai sponsored by the NCJSC. The late Tom Buttweiler's artice, "The History of Pre-Edo Menuki" was nicely illustrated and clearly showed the various styles of menuki that evolved during that time-frame in history. That article was a key stimulus that set me off on my fascination with early menuki. This publication would really be a worthwhile one for the NCJSC to consider reprinting one day. If not that, then perhaps the JSS/US could get permission to reprint several of these papers in their newsletter.

Another 2-volume set of books out of Germany several years ago (1995) -- one volume on tsuba, the other on kodogu -- had a pretty good section on menuki. I'm not sure of the formal title to the "set" but it was by H. U. B. Verlag (ISBM 3-931150-00-3). Nice large photographs with the text translated into English, German and Japanese.

In all these years I have seen only one small illustrated article that dealt with the making of menuki. That appeared in a NBTHK Token Bijutsu journal perhaps 15 years ago and showed a modern day menuki maker creating his works. Years later I tried to locate that article to get it translated, but I never did locate it again. Not much written (in depth) about menuki, so it seems.

Ron
STL

Re: [nihontostudy] Pin holes ?????

by yamaimojg

Strange Rich. Normally that sort of punching was done to spread the metal out laterally (to shrink the nakago-ana), so nothing would be broken away or gouged out. This case is pretty extreme and to no obvious effect, but still, removing iron isn't difficult and the holes wouldn't be much help as far as I can imagine. Iron is soft and it looks like the holes were drilled after the punching. It strikes me as some later fiddling to mount it on something, but who knows.

The design is weights, not axe heads, btw (not your idea, I know). The Japanese tool for splitting bamboo looks like a broken off sword (no kisaki). A friend of mine from Higashi Hiroshima comes from a farming family and their bamboo knife is in fact a broken Masahide sword with a nice mei.

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Pin holes ?????

by davidzincavage

The weight (fundo) was one of the Myriad Treasures (takaramono) associated with the Seven Gods of Good Luck, who carry them in a sack. As such it is an auspicious symbol. Perhaps the bamboo is split open to demonstrate that it is empty (in the positive Taoist/Buddhist sense) at its core, as a samurai opens his belly to disclose his sincerity.

David Zincavage
