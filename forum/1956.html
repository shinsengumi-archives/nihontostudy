by yamaimojg

Matt,
The old Chinese/Japanese system had 12 Junishi (the zodiac animals) and 10 Jikkan that were combined to make a calendar cycle of 60 years. Reaching 60 years of age is still cause for a celebration since you've completed one cycle and are about to start on the second (which you're not likely to see the end of). I've heard it described as being reborn and starting a second childhood, or just as the start of old age. In theory it is the point at which one no longer has to deal with dreary adult responsibilities, but I don't know if that was ever really what happened. Maybe someone else can provide further insight, particularly on how it was viewed 300 or 400 years ago, but I think that when you're 61, you're at a new stage of life as a person and fittings artist and you gain certain prerogatives.

I haven't studied exactly which groups followed this practice. Generally when a guard is inscribed with the maker's age, it's from late in life. Not all signed or signed and dated guards were done after age 61, although perhaps that was the case in Higo. It'd be hard to prove the date of all signed but undated or "un-age inscribed" guards to be sure none were done before age 61.

Hayashi Matashichi is probably the most highly valued maker among the Higo groups, although the first Jingo and Hikozo aren't far behind. I don't think that there are any known signed examples from the first Jingo or Nishigaki Kanshiro. Some Matashichi guards have kinzogan mei, but these are probably added later. I have heard some speculation that some could have been done by Matashichi because of the fineness of the inlay, but I don't think that's widely accepted. None of the soft metal first Hikozo guards are signed, but I think there's one iron one with a signature.

Later generations of the various groups did start signing, but you'll have to hit the reference books for yourself there. I think that for people who did sign, if all else is equal a signed guard would be priced higher than an unsigned one. A middling signed guard probably wouldn't be priced higher than a very good mumei one by the same maker. An unsigned Hayashi Matashichi is going to be worth way more than signed later generation work. The quality of the later generations' work is generally not close to the founder's work within a given line. The Kamiyoshi family, which started later, are the stars of later Higo.

The question about at what age the best work was done is complicated and would be different with each individual (and would also depend upon how much work that individual was actually doing himself). Senility aside, I find de Kooning's late work to be pretty wimpy. Further, I was surprised years ago when I realized that all of his paintings that I though were really great were done between '56 and '60. That would be what... de Kooning's mid 50's? Guess he couldn't have signed them if he was working in Higo.
Jim

Re: Hayashi Tsuba (was Re: Jingo Tsuba)

by arnold frenzel

Hi:
Sorry for the tardy reply; I've been away. Your questions have probably
all been answered, but here is my very inexpert opinion.
A. This one is easy. In the east Asian scheme of things for those using
the Chinese zodiac, sixty is the full cycle of 12 times 5 and a very
important year. 61 is merely the first year thereafter, so the launching
of a new cycle. To achieve 60 is the occasion for a major birthday
party.
B. I don't know.
C. I would think that the edge would go to Hayashi.
D. As a rule of thumb, given the way you posed the question, the
unsigned would be much less valuable, and were there really two that
were, cet. par., "identical" it would be amazing for the unsigned to
have survived being "up graded." The distinguished collector RB
Caldwell, from Dallas, once said that if one did commission work for an
important man, e.g., a Daimyo, the convention was not to sign as such
would be disrespectful. I have only seen that in print from him and have
no other authority, but if true it might explain why some super pieces
are unsigned.
E. I would think that in the arts a man's high point would be in
mid-life, particularly if he is involved in much creativity. If it is
just a matter of doing the same thing over and over, perhaps the
richness of experience would lead to his skills not seeming to wane.
Just my 2 cents worth.
Arnold Frenzel
