Fredrik,

I'll stick my neck out first.
I'd say same design, later copy. Possibly much
later. There are several differences. For starters,
the shape of the seppa dai. The quality of the iron is
also an important one and probably why Jim's is dated
as he has it. The sukashi on his is very gentle, yet
the iron like extra hard armor iron so the tsuba could
be considered either kyo-sukashi or katchushi.

In a case like yours, someone just needs to make a
judgement call on the iron and the workmanship of the
sukashi.

Re: [nihontostudy] Tsuba on Jim Gilbert's webpage

by yamaimojg

Fredrik and all,

I agree with the earlier posts.

The one I have is strange because the seppa dai, kozuka ana and sukashi carving are very much like Kyo sukashi, but the thickness and folding are like a Ko-Tosho guard. Ita plate tsuba with extensive sukashi work are usually attributed to Katchushi, and that's why I placed the guard on that page. The iron is more like Owari than any of the above, though. It's a strange combination, but each "component" is consistent with ca. late Muromachi.

Your guard has a wider seppa dai, wider kozuka ana, thicker sukashi walls and a thicker plate. It has a lot of rust and pitting that make it hard to see the surface but the unrusted areas look very flat and sharp. Nothing about it points to an early origin, despite using the same design. Without seeing it in person it's hard to say if it is a modern repro that has been intentionally rusted or an Edo period guard in poor condition. I wouldn't be surprised if it's modern. Thanks for posting your tsuba and thanks for the great responses.

Jim

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: Tsuba on Jim Gilbert's webpage

by yamaimojg

Milt mentioning Sasano made me think of an example from the NTHK shinsa in London. Not talking about copies or junk, there was an early guard with the classic kiri mon and karakusa motif often seen in Kyo-sukashi tsuba. Just like number 37 in Sasano's Early Japanese Sword Guards. If I was looking at a photo I would have no problem calling it Kyo sukashi. The "problem" is that the rim was a mass of Owari-style tekkotsu. It's easier to change sukashi design than to change ore/processing/forging so the kantei went to Owari rather than Kyo. Kantei from photos is risky.

Jim

Ita plate tsuba with extensive sukashi work are usually attributed to Katchushi, and that's why I placed the guard on that page. The iron is more like Owari than any of the above, though. It's a strange combination, but each "component" is consistent with ca. late Muromachi.
>
>Your guard has a wider seppa dai, wider kozuka ana, thicker sukashi walls and a thicker plate. It has a lot of rust and pitting that make it hard to see the surface but the unrusted areas look very flat and sharp. Nothing about it points to an early origin, despite using the same design. Without seeing it in person it's hard to say if it is a modern repro that has been intentionally rusted or an Edo period guard in poor condition. I wouldn't be surprised if it's modern. Thanks for posting your tsuba and thanks for the great responses.
