Re: Crass Commercial Message, books and sword for sale. JUYO COMMENT

by Barry Thomas

Matthew Hoyle wrote:

> Right and now on to the sword that I have for sale.
> This Nambokucho (14th century) sword was
> purchased last year from Aoi Arts in Japan...
>
> I was toying with the idea of submitting it to Juyo Shinsa,
> but have so far not got round to it. I don't know if it
> would stand a chance of going Juyo, but I think that it
> should be submitted if only just for the experience which
> would be very educational in my opinion...

"...should be submitted if only just for the experience..." - With the recent
increases in shinsa costs I believe this experience (of submitting a sword to
the NBTHK Juyo shinsa) can cost US$3,000 over and above "the education"?? And
it must first pass the Tokubetsu Hozon shinsa. I would think that very, very
few swords could go straight from Hozon to the Juyo shinsa. All in all, VERY
expensive. Friends of mine have tried in aggregate FOUR times for Juyo shinsa
and 3 failed. Payment is in advance and no refund if your sword doesn't "get
up".

A mate of mine was in Japan staying with his polisher. The togishi was
repolishing a sword that was already Juyo - ALREADY JUYO, note. It was being
repolished because there were features of that particular smith that were not
evident in its present state of polish as a Juyo-ranked sword. The repolish was
successful as the sword made Tokubetsu Juyo and the polisher won an annual prize
for the work.

Best regards,

Barry Thomas
(Melbourne, Australia)

by jaso1670

I would suggest sending the blade to someone like Cary Condell who has a
good relationship with Tanobe sensei.

Often, Tanobe will look over the blade ahead of time and tell you what he
thinks of the blade, what level it will get to, and what you need to do with
it in order for it to go higher.

It's not that expensive, and it's very much worth it so you don't spend
unneeded money chasing after a paper the sword won't get.

-Thom
