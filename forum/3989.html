Re: [nihontostudy] Re: tsuba kantei 3

by robsongord

Hi Jim,

I may have seen this one before, but I will go ahead and give an
opinion. The manner in which the tsuba is "dished out" with the plate
narrower at the center and then gradually thickening towards the rim is
the first strong identification feature. Next, the obvious hammer work,
giving the tsuba both a wonderful texture and great strength is another
strong feature, along with the beaten over rim. The deep purple, black
color and the shallow carvings are also very strong features of this
artist's work. The soft rounding of the work in the surface is also
classic for this man. The mokko shape is also typical of this worker.
The combination of small and large filled hitsu indicate a fairly early
guard -- late Muromachi to Momoyama period. The particularly small
kogai hitsu would go well with Ko-Mino kogai, some of which are
extremely narrow. Given these attributes and the early date, there is
only one possible maker and that is Nobuie. I suspect it has a juyo
rating as well. A wonderful piece.

Gordon

Re: tsuba kantei 3

by klepet33319

Hi Barry -- The technique is called 'yakite shitate' and involves the
tsuba to be placed into the furnace until the surface slightly
melts. This produces a glaze like finish. It's most remembered in
the kanayama school pieces but used in others.

I'm going to take a chance and be more specific. I would guess that this tsuba is signed "Nobuiye," with the hanare style of mei (also called Gamei).

The tsuba is obviously Nobuiye: yakita shitate (looking glazed) surface, with surface-hammered ji mon engraving.

I attribute it to the hanare signature Nobuiye, because of its subtle and dignified overall appearance. The mokko shape adds to the likelihood of it being that Nbuiye.

DZ.

tsuba kantei 3

by sworddad

Hi Jim, all -

When posting my thoughts on the latest (#3) tsuba kantei (Nobuiye) I mentioned that the example tsuba reminded me of a tsuba of mine made by Jirotaro Naokatsu (shinshin-to swordsmith). Taking a look at that tsuba today, I was rather impressed with faithfulness that Naokatsu followed to reproduce the work of Nobuiye. I've posted a (less than quality?) photo of the Naokatsu for you to see:

http://tinyurl.com/rs6hm

Notice the lumpy "bones" at the edge of the tsuba at 10 o'clock. Shodai Naokatsu added "bones" to the surface of his tsuba and these can be seen at 1 o'clock and 5 o'clock positions. Notice the similar "plum blossom" design also used by him, no exact, but similar to the Nobuiye. (My tsuba--about 88 mm--is slightly smaller than the kantie Nobuiye. Mine is NBTHK papered.)

Neat comparison!

Ron
