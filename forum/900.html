one heck of a tanto...more comments

by ashigaru1

After having time beween work assignments, i looked at the blade
again. and have further comments.
1st - I understand that extra mekugi ana might mean new tsuka were
put on, but why not use the old ana? Surely, a suitable mekugi could
be made to fit an old ana. so my question remains, why the three
mekugi ana?

2nd - The blade is over priced, at least from the prices I have
seen on much more interesting looking Koto tanto on the web that had
papers, were ubu, mumei, and even came in full koshire, that were
much less expensive. I am most likely wrong on this.

3ird - If the seller is who i suspect & living in Montreal (see e-
bay seller profiel), he his very well thought of, judging from the
comments on his web site. I will not give that address out since he
might care to remain unkown on e-bay, but the elder members I am sure
know who I am talking about.

Lastly, I apologise for talking so much, as a new member and novice
to Nihon-to. I find the whole subject exciting and tend to run on &
on. I am sure this enthusiasim will fade somewhat over the years,
and burnish to a soft cynisim.

Re: [nihontostudy] Re: one heck of a tanto...more comments

by samurai@xhasc.com

<ashigaru1@...> writes:

> yeah, that's who i was thinking of...

It illustrates nicely the danger of making assumptions. I think the
second statement you made about the pricing is also not based on good
research.

I'm the owner of the tanto.

So I obviously disagree about the pricing aspect (which was actually set
for me by one of the most respected members of the non-Japanese sword
community), but it's kind of pointless to try to argue that kind of
thing. I'll settle for explaining how I look at pricing for any
particular piece.

Pricing is subjective to begin with, and your model for finding a
fitting replacement for this piece in the marketplace I think is missing
out on several aspects.

Some aspects of a collectable piece can be compared and contrasted
simply. The habaki, the sayagaki, the shirasaya, the papers. The costs
of these are all fairly flat and known and can be added into the bottom
line and calculated easily.

Beyond that, "koto tanto" is a very broad and vague term, kind of
equivalent to saying "old painting."

There are "old paintings" available for next to nothing, and some "old
paintings" are priced in the hundred million dollar range. If you are
just in the market for an "old painting" with price as your guide, then
pretty much everything by comparison is overpriced vs. anything from the
bargain bin.

Where you really want to start with a piece like this I think is with
the smith, rather than the period, and his ratings and valuations.

Naotsuna is a very famous smith, he is one of the "Masamune Juttetsu"
though modern scholarship considers he probably had no direct
relationship. Some still believe, but regardless of either, he is part
of the old stories. He is considered Jo-jo saku by Fujishiro, and this
is a very high rating achieved by very few smiths.

He is also rated at 850 man yen in the Toko Taikan, or 8.5 million yen
for a Juyo Token daito quality piece. Cut that in half for a tanto. Some
might say the piece has to be signed, but for many of these smiths there
are no signed works and it is not clear that the Toko Taikan was
intended to be used like that. Rather, if you use a "high" Juyo Token as
the basis I think it works better.

So beginning with 4.25M yen, we can split that in half again for a
Tokubetsu Hozon piece. So now we're down to about 2.12 million yen.

We can probably split that in half because the piece has endured many
polishes. I am trying to not nickel and dime this on the way down.

This gives us 1.05 million yen. Since tanto are rare in the scope of
Naotsuna's work, this makes the piece more attractive. Say add
10%. Horimono add I think 20% to the value of a work, and since horimono
are virtually non-existent in the body of Naotsuna's work, we can add
perhaps another 5%. That works out to about 1.46M yen, or around
$12,700.00.

This number probably includes something like the dollars spent on
papering, but may not include the factors such as the quality of the
habaki (gold foil, silver foil, solid, etc) and the sayagaki. In a lower
priced piece like this, it is fair to weigh out these aspects.

I tried to use commonly accepted values in the calculation, but each
person may have their own subjective valuation for each aspect that may
change the total. And that is how things should be, some aspects do not
matter to some people, and they do to others. Ideally, you want to come
to some kind of collective representation in your pricing model that
embraces as much as possible.

I mentioned up top that I sought out advice from one of the most
knowledgable and respected members of the community before I set my
pricing, and I priced it in line with his instructions.

With the "valued" pricing in mind, one has to remember that the ideal
price for any unique item like this should be considered "too high" by
at least 95% of the people considering it. That is, most should look at
the piece and weigh out in favor of keeping their cash in their wallet.

If you have priced something so that 95% of the people considering it
will exchange cash for the piece you are selling, this means you have
come in far below fair market pricing and have caused yourself
injury. If you have priced it such that 100% of the people find it too
expensive, well then it will sit there forever and you will end up
repricing it in the future :-).

Because most people may not buy it at a given price, doesn't mean
something is overpriced.

I'm really uncomfortable with having to come out and make a comment, but
as you had already erred on the seller, I felt that it was important for
me to at least defend my decisions as there is some rationality behind
them. And, as I mentioned, it was mostly based on the advice of someone
with 30+ years of experience doing this.

Lastly, I am not a dealer, I am just a collector and am going to have to
rotate pieces out of my collection from time to time.

To conclude, I personally think that this is an example of why pricing
discussions should be kept off the nihonto study list (at least). In the
end they're a matter for subjectivity, and careless comments about who
owns what, and what is priced where can cause people a lot of injury.

Those are more appropriately comments to reserve to private
conversations, where if you are incorrect on anything, the scope of
damage you can cause is much smaller than when it's yelled from the
rooftops.

Thanks,

- darcy
