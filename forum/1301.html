Someone asked about matching a single menuki showing a brush
and inkstone. While I think trying to find the needle in the haystack
would be easier, I can at least help point you toward what to look
for.

There are two possible themes referred to with these items;
one is Sugarawa Michizane and the other is Tanabata. Since I can't
remember who posted the query I will assume you do not instantly
recognize these.

Sugawara Michizane was a famous scholar, courtier and
minister who ran afoul of certain members of the Fujiwara who smeared
his name and turned the Emperor agaisnt him. He was known to study
under the Plum trees in his garden. When he was to leave for exile in
Kyushu, he penned a now famous poem to his trees entreating them to
send their sweet Frangrance to him though he be far away. At which
point one of the trees uprooted and flew off to Kyushu and his place
of exile. So if you see plum blossoms this is the theme, and your
match might be a scroll with bits of the poem on it, or Michizane
riding an Ox, or a simple plum branch.

Tanabata is celebrated on the 7th day of the 7th month, in
China it is called Kikoden (Japanese reading here). The lord of the
heavens allowed his daughter to marry a simple shepard but she
behaved so badly on the wedding day that he forced them to live on
opposites sides of the Milky Way. Once a year they are allowed to see
each other when the Ama no Gawa (River of Heaven) Is bridged by a
murder of crows. The rest of the year, she spends pining to see her
husband and weaving, so is known as Ori-Hime,"Ori" means to weave. In
the Chinese tradition fresh inkstones were laid outside on "kuzu"
leaves, on top of these were laid bunches of Yam leaves to "catch the
dew". On the day of the festival celebrants would write their wishes
with the dewdrops from the river of heaven.According to Joly
this "emblematic representation of the Tanabata festival was a
familiar theme of tsuba decoration by the Umetada."

Since Ori-hime spends all year wishing to see her husband,
this is the one day all year when wishes are fulfilled. So the custom
is to write your wishes on colored strips of paper, since this is a
rather romantic story it is also a day for romance and love poems in
Japan. If along with the inkstone and brushes you see leaves, strips
of paper or a (weaving) shuttle then your theme is Tanabata.

As was suggested simple kanji might be a good match though I
couldn't find them paired "as a theme", also an ink well with it's
lid would be good. According to the Tsuba Gadai Jiten, These everyday
items are indespensable, ink and brush are inseperable. So I guess
they symbolize a well ordered world, a well rounded gentlemen¡Ä
