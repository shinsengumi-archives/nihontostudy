A thought

by retalt

Hi all, I was thinking about this on the weekend. We see a lot of Swords that have been shortend during the second world war to fit Gunto koshirae and many of these swords turn out to be high quality items. It is of course dependant on the original swords size as to whether it became Suriage or O'suriage but my question is this.
What is the opinion of Suriage/O'suriage in Japan.

I would assume it is the same as ours and maybe things have changed a bit in the time since WW2 but If I had a family sword that was know to be of quality and by a well known smith, be him Koto or Shinto, I am unsure if I would want to cut the mei off or shorten the nakago by any length to fit into fitting's to take on the battle field.

Was it not possible to have a tsuka made to fit the sword ? I know there are many Juyo Suriage blade in museums in Japan but they are of course very special and for the most part, very old. Was it more important to the officer that he carried his family sword into battle, regarless of what that required, be it shortening or whatever.

cheers

Rich T

by robsongord

Hi Rich,

I am not convinced, first, that many good blades were altered for gunto
mounts. I would expect they were altered long before that. You would have to
look at the condition of the rust. If the rust is of the type found on
blades that were made during the same period, then they were altered then.
Of course, some could have been chemically rusted, but I think that would
still be noticable. On the other hand, we are presuming that all Japanese
soliders of WWII were sufficiently sensitive to sword culture to not make
such alterations. I am not very convinced that they were then or that the
average Japanese is now.

Gordon
