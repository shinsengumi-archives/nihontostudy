Hi Richard,

The fuchi-kashira are MIno in style. Can't tell much about the menuki or the
tsuba, which appears to be iron. Kozuka is also difficult to tell much
about. Unfortunately, when my daughter was home the last time with her
boyfriend, they switched my internet connnection to Opera, which only reads
English, so I can't read the Japanese text.
On a slightly different note getting back to the use of Ko-Tosho and
Ko-Katchushi tsuba, I had mentioned Sasano's ideas about this. This morning
I took another look at his book Tosogu no Kigen or Origins of Sword Fittings
(actually, Katana Fittings). One of the illustrations he cites is the Ban
Daiagon scroll, which shows an elderly foot solider with an uchigatana that
has a tsuba. This scroll dates to the late Heian period. There are other
warriors with shorter blades -- koshigatana? -- that are without tsuba.
There is also a plain round iron tsuba that would be called a Ko-Tosho that
was mounted on a nagamaki. We know this because the seppa were rusted to the
tsuba and they are large and round of the type that would be for a nagamaki
handle. The tsuba is very large at 9.3 cent. across. Anyway, just thought I
would share this.

Gordon
