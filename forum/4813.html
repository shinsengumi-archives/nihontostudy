Becky's Sword

by wallacbb

I'd like to thank everyone who has been helping me with Japanese Sword info lately. We did go to the Gun show here in Dallas today. Sold the WWII Japanese rifle we had - it was not a very good piece and I'm glad to have it out of the closet. Had several people look at the sword. The blade, as has been pointed out by some of you, was not signed and it has one nick that is slightly too big to be polished out, which detracts considerably from the value. There was concurance that this sword was made in the 1600s and is a Washika (spelling) the shoter sword. Everyone liked the tsuba, end cap and collar. I got offered about $500 for the tsuba and $100 each for the collar and end cap, but just $300 for the whole thing! The conclusion I came to was that a gun show was really more like a giant pawn shop than the place to really get a fair assessment of value, but it was very interesting, I ran into at least 3 of my co-workiers from Lockheed who had booths, and I did come
away knowing a few things: 1. the sword is old and really not in bad shape - I can continue to use it in the event of an intruder and could probably take one out with it - I'm not a handgun fan :-) 2. If I really had it appraised, documented, or auctioned, I'd get $1500 or so for it, 3. I like it more than that so will be keeping it for the forseeable future. I'll probably drop out of this group in a few weeks having accomplished my mission, but thanks for the education. I can sure see how this could become a very interesting hobby.

Becky

by sworddad

Becky, all -

It is interesting that when offering a "mounted" sword of this quality that people will want to pay less than if you would break it apart and sell the blade and fittings seperately. Not quite sure what that is but I've more than a few times have (as most of us have) sold off fittings seperately from the sword. (Unless, I should add, the sword is nicely mounted in a relatively complete and intact koshirae.)

As for a sword like this being worth much more after being "appraised, documented or auctioned," one has to realize that these directions take money to do these things. I'm not sure what a reliable appraiser would charge today but for a stateside "paper" you would pay around $250; to send a sword to Japan it might run $1,200 (Tokubetsu Hozon) after you are finished with shipping and fees and registrations. Auction houses charge seller fees and catalogue fees. Nothing comes cheap!

Just rattling on a bit...

Ron
STL

Re: Becky's Sword

by klepet33319

I think it's important to remember that it will require polishing and
shira saya prior to submission to shinsa. The 'nick' in the ha will
cause either major change in geometry or necessitate shortening
(machi okuri). By the time you're done you will have at least $3000
and two to three years into the project and a blade which might only
be worth a fraction of that cost. What would probably be the best
bet would be running some very good pictures past a good polisher to
find out if it's worth it.
Just my two cents.

Re: My Kunitomo, Elliott's photos #1 & #2

by klepet33319

OK -- I guess I'll be the one to go in where angels fear to tread!
-- LOL -- I will limit my comments to the two organizations I have
personal experience with having no predjudice toward any others.

First, I would suggest reading through this link:

http://www.nihonto.ca/ratings.html

Darcy Brockbank did a great job going over the organizations ratings
systems and although aimed at swords it crosses over to fittings well.

Basically both organizations are excellent and their papers are given
high regard internationally and their shinsa teams are both top
notch. It has been 'my' experience that if you are papering for
authentication with insight into details then the NTHK might be more
appropriate as they rate by point and give description sheets with
pertinent information (I believe this is only at their US shinsa and
does not apply to the ones held in Japan). The NBTHK is the larger
organization and perhaps has the advantage of greater popular
recognition/charisma but their papers do not have the worksheets to
supply information and that listed on their origami (paper) can be
limited. It is also a consideration that the NBTHK papers tend to be
more expensive to obtain. I have both and have submitted for both --
both organizations are worthy of our support.

As far as submission (assuming you live outside of Japan) for the
NBTHK you will need an agent to submit and would be best served going
through one of the dealer/agents you can find on-line and/or by
recommendation. The NTHK has shinsa here in the USA and it would be
well worth your while to go to the shinsa/show (in Chicago this
Spring) and be part of the process. They will have a question/answer
session after the close of the day's session which is very
informative. You also have a show to attend in the meantime which
will give you great contacts and many ways to spend your savings!
(LOL) If you simply can't go then there are again dealer agents who
can assist you.

I would strongly suggest that anyone who has the ability to attend
the Chicago Show/Shinsa do so. This is a 'hands-on' field and you
need all the first hand experience you can get. Books are a far
second to what you can learn in person. Be patient as it takes some
time to become known and make contacts. In the long run the faces
will become familiar (for better or worse - LOL) and you will find
your 'niche'. I guarantee it can be time well spent. Bottom line is
get full immersion in the field by hands on experience
whenever/wherever possible, keep an open mind, respect your elders,
wash behind your ears and leave the fine tuning for later.
